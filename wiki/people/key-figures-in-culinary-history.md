## Overview
François Pierre de La Varenne (1618–1678) was a seminal [[French]] [[chef]] and [[author]] from the [[region]] of [[Burgundy]], who died in [[Dijon]]. His work is considered the foundation of modern [[French Cuisine]]. His most famous book, *Le Cuisinier François* (1651), marked a significant departure from the [[Italian Cuisine]] traditions that had dominated French [[cookery]] since the 16th century. Writing for a professional audience, La Varenne was a key figure among [[chefs]] who [[codified]] the [[culinary arts]] for the era of King [[Louis XIV]], establishing a uniquely French gastronomic identity.

## Culinary Philosophy & Innovations
La Varenne's primary contribution was to [[systematize]] [[food preparation]] according to logical [[rules]] and principles, moving [[French cooking]] out of its medieval phase. He introduced numerous [[foundational techniques]] and [[preparations]] that are still central to [[cooking]] today. He is credited with creating the first [[Bisque]] and [[Béchamel Sauce]]. In a revolutionary shift, he advocated for using [[Roux]] (a cooked [[mixture]] of [[flour]] and [[fat]]) to [[thicken]] [[sauces]] instead of [[crumbled bread]], and championed the use of [[Butter]] over [[Lard]]. His work contains the first recorded use of terms like [[Bouquet Garni]], [[Fonds de Cuisine]] (or [[Stocks]]), and [[Reduction]]. He also documented the [[technique]] of using [[Egg Whites]] for [[Clarification]] and provided the earliest known printed [[recipe]] for the [[Mille-feuille]]. Unusually for his time, he also gave significant attention to the [[cooking]] of [[vegetables]], with one [[recipe]] for [[asparagus sauce]] being a clear forerunner to [[Sauce Hollandaise]].

## Major Works
*Le Cuisinier François* (1651) is La Varenne's masterpiece and is regarded as the founding text of modern [[French Cuisine]]. It was one of the first [[cookbooks]] to present [[recipes]] in a systematic, [[codified]] manner, laying out clear [[rules]] and principles for the [[professional chef|chef]]. The book captured the [[culinary innovations]] of 17th-century [[France]] and remained influential for decades, setting a new standard for [[culinary writing]] and practice.

## Contemporaries
La Varenne was the foremost member of a group of [[influential chefs|chefs]] and [[authors]] who were collectively shaping the future of [[French food]]. His contemporaries included [[Nicholas de Bonnefons]], [[author]] of *Le Jardinier François* (1651) and *Les Délices de la Campagne* (1654), and [[François Massialot]], whose book *Le Cuisinier Royal et Bourgois* (1691) was a standard reference that was updated well into the 18th century.
