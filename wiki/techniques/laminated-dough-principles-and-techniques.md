## Overview
Lamination is the process of creating a dough with hundreds of alternating layers of fat and dough. This is achieved by enclosing a block of fat (the *beurrage*) within a base dough (the *detrempe*) and then repeatedly rolling and folding the package. The result is a pastry that bakes into an exceptionally light, flaky, and rich final product.

This technique is the foundation for much of the [[Viennoiserie]] category of pastries. Yeast-leavened examples include the classic [[Croissant]] and [[Danish Pastry]]. An unleavened version is [[Puff Pastry]], also known as *pâte feuilletée*, which is used to make dishes like [[Mille-Feuille]]. Other famous applications include [[Pain au Chocolat]] and savory pastries filled with ingredients like [[Cheese]], [[Spinach]], or [[Ham]]. The entire process is highly dependent on precise temperature control to keep the fat and dough layers distinct.

## The Science
The magic of laminated dough occurs during [[Baking]]. As the pastry heats in the oven, the [[Water]] present in both the dough and the [[Butter]] turns into steam. This steam is trapped between the countless, thin, waterproof layers of fat. The pressure from the expanding steam forces the dough layers apart, creating the dramatic rise and flaky separation characteristic of these pastries. This is a powerful form of physical [[Leavening]].

Simultaneously, the fat layers melt, enriching the dough and frying the layers from within, which contributes to the pastry's tenderness and crispness. In yeast-leavened doughs like [[Croissant]]s, this steam leavening works in conjunction with the gas produced by [[Yeast]] during [[Fermentation]] and [[Proofing]]. The initial management of [[Gluten]] is also critical; the *detrempe* is minimally mixed to keep it pliable for rolling, with strength being built through the folding process itself.

## How to Do It
The process can be broken down into several key stages, requiring patience and attention to temperature.

**1. Preparing the Dough (Detrempe)**
The base dough ingredients vary. [[Puff Pastry]] dough is a simple mix of [[Flour]], [[Water]], and [[Salt]]. [[Croissant]] dough is enriched with [[Sugar]], [[Yeast]], and [[Milk]], while [[Danish Pastry]] dough adds [[Egg]]s for further richness. The dough is mixed only until it forms a sticky mass to avoid excessive [[Gluten]] development, which would make it difficult to roll.

**2. Preparing the Fat (Beurrage)**
[[Butter]] is the preferred fat for its flavour, though [[Lard]], [[Margarine]], or [[Shortening]] can be used. The fat is chilled to approximately 55°F/13°C, then pounded and rolled into a thin, rectangular slab between sheets of [[Parchment Paper]]. It must be cold but pliable.

**3. Locking in the Fat**
The chilled *detrempe* is rolled out, and the *beurrage* is placed on top. The dough is then folded over the fat to completely enclose it. Common [[Folding (Dough)|folds]] for this step include the [[Envelope Fold]], [[Single Fold]], [[Letter Fold]] (or triple-fold), and [[Book Fold]].

**4. The Turns (Rolling and Folding)**
This is the core of lamination. For large-scale production, a [[Dough Sheeter]] is used.
- After locking in the fat, the dough is wrapped and chilled for 30-45 minutes to relax the gluten and firm up the butter.
- The dough is then "turned" 90 degrees and rolled out into a long rectangle.
- It is folded again (typically a letter- or book-fold). This completes one "turn."
- The dough is chilled again for 30-45 minutes.
- This process of turning, rolling, folding, and chilling is repeated, typically for a total of 3-4 turns, creating hundreds of layers.

**5. Final Shaping and Baking**
After the final turn and chill, the dough is rolled to its final thickness.
- It is cut into desired shapes, such as triangles for [[Croissant]]s or rectangles for [[Pain au Chocolat]].
- The pastries are shaped, often with fillings like [[Chocolate]] or [[Almond Paste]].
- Yeast-leavened doughs undergo a final [[Proofing]] to allow them to rise.
- Finally, they are often brushed with an [[Egg Wash]] and baked in a hot oven.

## Common Mistakes
- **Incorrect Fat Temperature:** If the [[Butter]] is too cold, it will shatter during rolling, creating chunky, uneven layers. If it is too warm, it will melt and be absorbed by the dough, destroying the lamination entirely.
- **Over-working the Dough:** Developing too much [[Gluten]] in the initial mix makes the dough elastic and resistant to rolling, leading to tearing.
- **Insufficient Chilling:** Skipping or shortening the chilling periods between folds prevents the gluten from relaxing and the butter from re-firming, which can cause the layers to merge.
- **Forgetting to Turn the Dough:** Failing to rotate the dough 90 degrees between folds will stretch the gluten in only one direction, resulting in an uneven, elongated crumb structure.
- **Using Too Much Bench Flour:** Excess [[Flour]] used during rolling can be incorporated into the dough, making the final pastry tough and dry. Always brush off excess flour before folding.

## When to Use It
This technique is central to [[French Cuisine]] and is the basis for the entire category of [[Viennoiserie]].
- **Breakfast Pastries:** It is essential for making classics like [[Croissant]]s, [[Danish Pastry]], and [[Pain au Chocolat]].
- **Desserts:** It is used to create elegant desserts such as [[Mille-Feuille]], palmiers, and fruit tarts.
- **Savory Dishes:** [[Puff Pastry]] is used for savory applications like [[Vol-au-Vent]], beef wellington, sausage rolls, and tart shells filled with [[Cheese]], [[Spinach]], or other ingredients.
