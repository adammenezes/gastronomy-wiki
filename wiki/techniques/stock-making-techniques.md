## Overview
[[Stocks]] are fundamental, flavorful liquids used as the base for countless culinary preparations, including [[Soups]], [[Sauces]], and [[Stews]]. They are created by gently [[Simmering]] various ingredients in [[Water]]. Depending on the desired outcome, stocks can be based on [[Meat]], [[Poultry]], [[Fish]], [[Game]], or [[Seafood]], and are typically flavored with a [[Mirepoix]] (a mixture of [[Onions]], [[Celery]], and [[Carrots]]), [[Herbs]], and [[Spices]]. [[Vegetable Stocks]] are also common, made from an assortment of produce or intensely flavored with a single ingredient like [[Mushrooms]], [[Tomatoes]], or [[Leeks]].

In [[French Cuisine]], several terms describe these foundational liquids: "fond" (meaning base), "broth," "bouillon," "fumet," and "nage." While often used interchangeably, a [[Broth]] is typically made from [[Simmered]] meats, whereas a stock is primarily derived from [[Bones]]. A [[Bouillon]] is a general term for any liquid produced by [[Simmering]] ingredients. A [[Fumet]] is a concentrated liquid, often incorporating [[Wine]], and a [[Nage]] (meaning "to swim") refers to cooking ingredients in a [[Court Bouillon]], frequently associated with [[Shellfish]].

Stocks are broadly categorized as [[White Stock]] or [[Brown Stock]]. [[White Stock]] is uncolored and subtly flavored, while [[Brown Stock]] utilizes [[Roasted]] components to achieve a rich color and robust flavor. A notable example from [[Japanese Cuisine]] is [[Dashi]], a light stock made with [[Dried Seaweed]] ([[Kombu]]), [[Dried Bonito Fish Flakes]] ([[Katsuobushi]]), and sometimes [[Shiitake Mushrooms]].

## The Science
The primary goal of stock preparation is the extraction of flavor, [[Gelatin]], and other soluble compounds from the ingredients. When [[Bones]], particularly those from younger animals or [[Gelatinous Cuts]] like [[Veal Feet]] or [[Pork Feet]], are [[Simmered]], their [[Collagen]] breaks down into [[Gelatin]]. This [[Gelatin]] dissolves into the liquid, contributing to the stock's body, mouthfeel, and viscosity.

Starting the stock in [[Cold Water]] is crucial. As the water slowly heats, it gently dissolves [[Proteins]] and other impurities from the [[Meat]] and [[Bones]]. These impurities, often coagulated [[Proteins]], rise to the surface as a scum, which can then be [[Skimming|skimmed]] off. This process helps achieve a clear stock. If the stock is started in hot water or allowed to [[Boiling|boil]] vigorously, these impurities can become emulsified into the liquid, resulting in a cloudy stock. Vigorous [[Boiling]] can also emulsify fats, making the stock greasy and opaque.

[[Acidity]], often introduced by [[Tomatoes]] in [[Brown Stock]] or [[White Wine]] in a [[Fumet]] or [[Court Bouillon]], aids in the extraction of flavor compounds and can contribute to the clarification of the liquid. The [[Maillard Reaction]], a chemical reaction between [[Amino Acids]] and reducing sugars, is responsible for the deep, savory flavors and brown color developed when [[Bones]] and [[Mirepoix]] are [[Roasting|roasted]] for a [[Brown Stock]]. The slow, gentle [[Simmering]] allows for a controlled and efficient transfer of flavors without over-extracting bitter compounds or causing excessive evaporation too quickly.

## How to Do It
Preparing a quality stock involves careful selection and handling of ingredients, along with precise [[Simmering]] techniques.

1.  **Ingredient Preparation:**
    *   **Bones:** Use high-quality [[Bones]] from younger animals for maximum [[Gelatin]] content. Cut them into small pieces (around 2-3 inches) to maximize surface area for flavor extraction. For [[Fish Stock]], use lean [[Fish Bones]] and remove the [[Gills]] to prevent off-flavors. [[Meat Trimmings]], cleaned of excess [[Fat]] and gristle, can also be added.
    *   **Mirepoix:** The classic [[Mirepoix]] consists of [[Onions]], [[Celery]], and [[Carrots]]. For [[White Stock]], add the [[Mirepoix]] towards the end of the [[Simmering]] process to preserve fresh flavors. For [[Brown Stock]], [[Roast]] the [[Mirepoix]] along with the [[Bones]] to develop deeper flavors and color. Other aromatics like [[Leeks]], [[Garlic]], [[Mushrooms]], [[Tomatoes]], or [[Fennel]] can be used to create specific flavor profiles.
    *   **Sachet d'epices:** A standard [[Sachet d'epices]] includes [[Parsley Stems]], [[Bay Leaf]], [[Thyme]], and [[Crushed Peppercorns]]. Tie these in a [[Cheesecloth]] for easy removal.
    *   **Liquid:** Always start with [[Cold Water]]. A [[Remouillage]] (a second wetting of previously used bones) can be substituted for water to enhance flavor.

2.  **Cooking Process:**
    *   **Optional Pre-treatment:** For [[Brown Stock]], [[Roast]] the [[Bones]] and [[Mirepoix]] until deeply caramelized. For [[White Stock]], [[Blanch]] the [[Bones]] in cold water, bring to a boil, drain, and rinse to remove impurities for a clearer stock.
    *   **Combine Ingredients:** Place the prepared [[Bones]] (and roasted [[Mirepoix]] if making [[Brown Stock]]) in a tall [[Stockpot]].
    *   **Add Water:** Cover the ingredients with [[Cold Water]].
    *   **Heat and Skim:** Slowly bring the liquid to a gentle [[Simmer]]. As it heats, impurities (scum) will rise to the surface. Use a [[Ladle]] or fine-mesh [[Skimmer]] to diligently [[Skimming|skim]] these off.
    *   **Add Aromatics:** If making [[White Stock]], add the [[Mirepoix]] and [[Sachet d'epices]] during the last hour of cooking.
    *   **Simmer Gently:** Maintain a very gentle [[Simmer]]; never allow the stock to [[Boiling|boil]]. [[Boiling]] will emulsify fats, make the stock cloudy, and can extract bitter flavors. Do not [[Stir]] the stock, as this will reincorporate impurities.
    *   **Replenish Liquid:** If significant evaporation occurs, replenish with hot water to keep ingredients submerged, but avoid over-diluting the flavor.
    *   **Cooking Time:** [[Fish Stock]] and [[Vegetable Stock]] typically cook for 45 minutes to 1 hour. [[Poultry Stock]] takes about 2.5 to 4 hours. [[Meat Stock]] (e.g., [[Beef Stock]], [[Veal Stock]]) can cook for 4 to 8 hours, though some chefs like [[Michel Roux]] suggest shorter times (e.g., 2.5 hours for meat/poultry) for optimal flavor without over-extraction. The key factor is the size of the [[Bones]]; smaller pieces extract flavor faster.

3.  **Straining and Cooling:**
    *   **Strain:** Once cooked, carefully [[Straining|strain]] the stock through a [[Chinois]] or [[China Cap]] lined with [[Cheesecloth]] to remove all solids.
    *   **Cool Quickly:** Rapidly cool the strained stock to prevent bacterial growth. This can be done by placing the pot in an [[Ice Bath]] or dividing it into smaller containers.
    *   **Storage:** Store cooled stock in the refrigerator for up to 3-4 days or freeze for longer storage.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Using Poor Quality Ingredients:** A stock is only as good as its components. Avoid using old, spoiled, or excessively fatty [[Meat Trimmings]] or [[Vegetables]].
*   **Not Removing Gills from Fish Bones:** [[Fish Gills]] contain compounds that impart a bitter, off-flavor to [[Fish Stock]].
*   **Boiling the Stock:** Allowing the stock to [[Boiling|boil]] vigorously will emulsify fats, make the stock cloudy, and can extract undesirable bitter flavors from the [[Bones]] and [[Vegetables]]. Always maintain a gentle [[Simmer]].
*   **Stirring During Cooking:** Stirring reincorporates impurities and sediment into the liquid, resulting in a cloudy stock.
*   **Adding Salt Too Early:** [[Salt]] is typically not added to stocks because they are often [[Reduction|reduced]] later, which would concentrate the salt to an unpalatable level. Season the final dish, not the stock.
*   **Adding Too Much Water:** Over-diluting the stock with too much [[Water]] will result in a weak, flavorless product.
*   **Overcooking Herbs and Spices:** Prolonged [[Simmering]] of delicate [[Herbs]] can lead to a loss of fresh flavor or the development of bitter notes. Add them towards the end for [[White Stock]].
*   **Not Skimming Impurities:** Failing to diligently [[Skimming|skim]] the scum that rises to the surface will result in a cloudy stock with an unappealing appearance.
*   **Unbalanced Mirepoix:** Too much [[Mirepoix]] in a [[Meat]], [[Poultry]], or [[Fish Stock]] can make it taste overly sweet or bitter, throwing the flavor out of balance.

## When to Use It
[[Stocks]] are incredibly versatile and form the backbone of many culinary preparations:
*   **Base for Liquids:** They are the essential foundation for [[Soups]], [[Sauces]], and [[Stews]], providing depth and complexity of flavor that [[Water]] alone cannot.
*   **Braising and Poaching:** Used as the liquid for [[Braising]] [[Meats]] (e.g., [[Osso Buco]]) or [[Poaching]] [[Fish]] and [[Poultry]], imparting flavor to the cooked item.
*   **Deglazing:** A small amount of stock can be used to [[Deglazing|deglaze]] a pan after [[Sautéing]] or [[Roasting]], capturing flavorful fond (caramelized bits) to create a pan sauce.
*   **Grain Cooking:** Substituting stock for [[Water]] when cooking [[Rice]] (e.g., [[Risotto]]), [[Quinoa]], or [[Couscous]] significantly enhances their flavor.
*   **Sauce Production:** Essential for classic [[French Sauces]] like [[Velouté]] and [[Demi-Glace]], as well as modern pan sauces.
*   **Glace and Reductions:** Stocks can be further [[Reduction|reduced]] to create a highly concentrated [[Glace]] (glaze), which offers intense flavor and viscosity, perfect for enriching other [[Sauces]] or as a natural soup base.
*   **Cost-Effective Cooking:** Utilizing [[Bones]] and [[Vegetable Trimmings]] that might otherwise be discarded makes stock production an economical practice in professional kitchens.
*   **Specific Cuisines:** [[Dashi]] is indispensable in [[Japanese Cuisine]] for dishes like [[Miso Soup]] and noodle broths.

*   [[Simmering]]: The gentle heat application technique central to stock preparation, ensuring clear and flavorful results.
*   [[Reduction]]: A technique often applied to finished stocks to concentrate their flavors and increase viscosity, leading to glaces and intense sauces.
*   [[Mirepoix]]: The aromatic vegetable base (onions, carrots, celery) that provides foundational flavor to most Western stocks.
*   [[Sachet d'epices]]: A bundle of herbs and spices, typically tied in cheesecloth, used to infuse flavor into stocks and other liquids.
*   [[Broth]]: A liquid similar to stock, often made primarily from meat rather than bones, and typically seasoned for immediate consumption.
*   [[Fumet]]: A highly concentrated stock, usually made from fish, often incorporating wine and aromatics for intense flavor.
*   [[Glace de Viande]]: A very highly reduced brown stock, cooked down to a syrupy consistency, used as a powerful flavor enhancer.
*   [[Gelatin]]: The protein derived from collagen in bones and connective tissues, crucial for giving stocks their body and mouthfeel.
*   [[Dashi]]: A foundational stock in Japanese cuisine, typically made from kombu and katsuobushi, offering a distinct umami flavor.
