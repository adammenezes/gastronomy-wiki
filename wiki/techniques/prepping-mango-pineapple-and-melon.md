## Overview
Fruit fabrication is the foundational [[Knife Skills|knife technique]] for breaking down whole fruits into usable forms, such as cubes, slices, or wedges. This page focuses on three common but structurally distinct fruits: mangoes, pineapples, and melons. Mastering their preparation is essential for minimising waste and achieving clean, professional-looking cuts for dishes ranging from a simple [[Fruit Salad]] to a complex [[Mango Salsa]] or a decorative [[Garnish]]. Each fruit presents a unique challenge: the mango's large, flat pit; the pineapple's tough, spiny rind and fibrous core; and the melon's smooth, thick rind and seed-filled cavity.

## The Science
The primary scientific considerations in fruit fabrication are cellular structure and enzymatic activity. A sharp knife is crucial as it severs cell walls cleanly, minimising bruising and the release of cellular fluids, which preserves the fruit's texture and juiciness.

Pineapple contains a potent [[Protease]] enzyme called [[Bromelain]]. This enzyme breaks down proteins, which is why fresh [[Pineapple]] can cause a tingling sensation in the mouth and is an effective ingredient in meat marinades. It also prevents gelatin from setting.

Once cut, fruit flesh is exposed to oxygen, which can trigger [[Enzymatic Browning]]. This is a chemical reaction involving enzymes like polyphenol oxidase that causes discoloration. While less pronounced in these fruits than in apples, a light [[Acidulation]] with [[Lemon Juice]] can inhibit the reaction and preserve the fruit's vibrant colour. The firm texture of melons is due to high water content creating turgor pressure within their plant cells, a process governed by [[Osmosis]].

## How to Do It
A stable [[Cutting Board]] and a sharp [[Chef's Knife]] are essential for all three fruits.

### Preparing a Mango
1.  Stand the mango on its end. The large, flat pit runs vertically through the center.
2.  Using a [[Chef's Knife]], slice downwards on either side of the pit, cutting off the two large "cheeks."
3.  For the remaining flesh around the pit, carefully slice it away with your knife.
4.  To process the cheeks, you can either:
    *   **Score and Invert:** Score the flesh in a crosshatch pattern without cutting through the skin. Gently press the skin from underneath to invert it, creating a "hedgehog" effect. The cubes can then be sliced off.
    *   **Glass Method:** Hold a sturdy drinking glass and press the rim against the base of the mango cheek, where the flesh meets the skin. Slide the glass upwards to scoop the flesh out in one clean piece.

### Preparing a Pineapple
1.  Lay the pineapple on its side and slice off the top crown and the bottom base.
2.  Stand the pineapple upright. Carefully slice downwards along the contours of the fruit to remove the tough, spiny skin.
3.  You will see diagonal rows of brown "eyes" remaining. Remove them by cutting V-shaped trenches diagonally along the fruit with a [[Paring Knife]].
4.  Slice the pineapple into rounds, then stack them to cut out the tough, fibrous central core. Alternatively, quarter the pineapple vertically and slice the core off each wedge.

### Preparing a Melon (Cantaloupe, Honeydew)
1.  Slice the melon in half from stem to end.
2.  Use a large [[Spoon]] to scoop out the seeds and fibrous pulp from the central cavity.
3.  Place each half cut-side down and slice into wedges.
4.  Run your [[Chef's Knife]] between the rind and the flesh of each wedge to separate them.
5.  Cut the flesh into cubes or desired shapes. For decorative spheres, use a [[Melon Baller]] on the halved melon before removing the rind.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Using a dull knife:** This is the most common mistake. A dull blade requires more pressure, increasing the risk of slipping. It also crushes fruit cells, leading to a mushy texture and loss of juice. See [[Knife Sharpening]].
*   **Wasting flesh:** Cutting too deeply when removing the rind of a pineapple or melon results in significant waste. Follow the fruit's contour closely.
*   **Not knowing the fruit's anatomy:** Attempting to cut through the center of a mango pit or failing to remove the woody core of a pineapple leads to frustration and poor results.
*   **Improper storage:** Leaving cut fruit exposed to air accelerates [[Enzymatic Browning]] and dehydration. Store in an airtight container in the refrigerator, perhaps with a squeeze of [[Lemon Juice]].

## When to Use It
This technique is a fundamental part of [[Mise en Place]] for any recipe involving fresh mango, pineapple, or melon. It is the first step for creating:
*   **Salads:** Both sweet [[Fruit Salad]] and savoury salads.
*   **Salsas:** Especially tropical versions like [[Mango Salsa]] or [[Pineapple]] salsa.
*   **Beverages:** For blending into a [[Smoothie]] or muddling into a [[Cocktail]] like a [[Piña Colada]].
*   **Desserts:** As a topping for yogurt, a component in a [[Fruit Tart]], or the base for a [[Sorbet]].
*   **Garnish:** For adding a fresh, colourful element to plated dishes.
