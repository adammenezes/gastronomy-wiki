## Overview
Tournée, French for "turned," is a classic [[Knife Skills|knife cut]] from [[French Cuisine]] used to shape root vegetables into a seven-sided, oblong "football" shape. While most famously applied to the [[Potato]] to create dishes like [[Pommes Château]], the technique is also used on [[Carrot]]s, [[Turnip]]s, [[Parsnip]]s, and even firmer squashes like [[Zucchini]]. The primary purposes of this cut are to ensure even cooking by creating a uniform shape and to provide an elegant, classical presentation.

## The Science
The science behind the tournée cut is primarily physical rather than chemical. By shaping each piece of vegetable into a uniform barrel or football shape, the surface-area-to-volume ratio is consistent across all pieces. This uniformity ensures that heat penetrates each piece at the same rate, resulting in exceptionally even cooking. There are no thin edges or small corners to overcook and turn mushy before the center is tender. Aesthetically, the smooth, curved facets are visually appealing and provide an ideal surface for catching glazes or sauces.

## How to Do It
Executing a proper tournée requires significant practice and a very sharp knife, preferably a specialized [[Tourné Knife]] or a bird's beak [[Paring Knife]].

1.  **Preparation**: Wash and peel the vegetable. Cut it into uniform lengths, typically around 2 inches (5 cm). Square off the sides to create a rough rectangular block.
2.  **Grip**: Hold the vegetable piece firmly in your non-dominant hand. Hold the [[Tourné Knife]] in your dominant hand with a secure grip.
3.  **First Cut**: Place the blade at the top corner of the vegetable block. In a single, decisive motion, turn the vegetable towards the blade with your thumb, carving a smooth, curved facet from top to bottom.
4.  **Repeat**: Rotate the vegetable and repeat the cutting motion, creating a second facet that meets the edge of the first.
5.  **Complete**: Continue this process until you have created seven equal, symmetrical facets around the vegetable. The final shape should be a neat, barrel-like oval with slightly blunted ends.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Creating Points**: The ends of the tournée should be slightly flat, not sharp points like an American football. This happens when the cut is started too far in from the edge.
*   **Uneven Facets**: The most common error is creating facets of different sizes. This is a result of inconsistent pressure or rotation. The only fix is consistent practice.
*   **Excessive Waste**: This cut inherently produces a lot of trim. A skilled chef minimizes waste, but it's unavoidable. A common mistake is discarding the trim; it should be saved for making [[Stock]], [[Soup]], or [[Purée]].
*   **Using a Dull Knife**: A dull blade will slip, creating jagged cuts and posing a significant safety risk. Always use a well-honed [[Paring Knife]].

## When to Use It
The tournée cut is a hallmark of classical and fine-dining [[French Cuisine]]. It is used when presentation is paramount and even cooking is critical.

*   **Classic Potato Dishes**: Essential for making [[Pommes Château]] (larger tournéed potatoes, often roasted) and [[Pommes Cocotte]] (smaller versions).
*   **Glazed Vegetables**: The smooth surfaces of tournéed [[Carrot]]s or [[Turnip]]s are perfect for holding a glaze.
*   **Braises and Stews**: In high-end versions of dishes like [[Boeuf Bourguignon]], tournéed vegetables hold their shape beautifully during long cooking times.
*   **Garnishes**: A perfectly executed tournée is an impressive garnish for roasts and other center-of-the-plate proteins.
