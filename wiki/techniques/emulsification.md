## Overview
[[Emulsification]] is the [[technique]] of combining two [[Immiscible Liquids]] — typically a [[Fat]] and a [[Water]]-based [[liquid]] — into a stable, unified [[mixture]]. It's one of the most important [[techniques]] in [[cooking]], underpinning everything from [[Mayonnaise]] and [[Hollandaise]] to [[Vinaigrettes]] and [[Pasta Sauce]]s.

## The Science
[[Oil]] and [[Water]] don't naturally mix because [[oil]] [[molecules]] are [[Non-Polar]] and [[water]] [[molecules]] are [[Polar]]. An [[Emulsifier]] (like [[Lecithin]] in [[Egg Yolk]]s, or [[Mustard Protein]]s) has both a [[Hydrophilic]] (water-loving) end and a [[Hydrophobic]] ([[fat]]-loving) end. The [[emulsifier]] positions itself at the [[Oil-Water Interface]], surrounding tiny [[Oil Droplet]]s and preventing them from [[Coalescing]].

There are two main types of emulsions:
*   [[Oil-in-Water Emulsion]] (O/W): [[Oil Droplet]]s dispersed in [[Water]]. Examples include [[Hollandaise]], [[Beurre Blanc]], [[Mayonnaise]], [[Vinaigrette]], and [[Pasta Carbonara Sauce]].
*   [[Water-in-Oil Emulsion]] (W/O): [[Water Droplet]]s dispersed in [[Oil]]. A common example is [[Butter]].

Key emulsifiers in cooking include:
*   [[Egg Yolk Lecithin]]: The most common, found in [[Egg Yolk]]s and used extensively in [[Mayonnaise]], [[Hollandaise]], and [[Pasta Carbonara]].
*   [[Mustard]]: [[Dijon Mustard]] contains [[Mucilage]] that helps stabilize [[Vinaigrettes]].
*   [[Honey]]: Can act as an [[emulsifier]] in [[Dressing]]s.
*   [[Starchy Pasta Water]]: The [[Starch]] acts as an [[emulsifier]] in dishes like [[Cacio e Pepe]] and [[Pasta Carbonara]].

## How to Do It
To create a stable emulsion:
1.  Start with one [[liquid]] in your [[Bowl]] or [[Pan]].
2.  Add the second [[liquid]] VERY slowly at first — drop by drop if needed. This is crucial for the [[emulsifier]] to properly encapsulate the [[dispersed phase]].
3.  [[Whisk]] or [[Blend]] constantly and vigorously. This provides the [[mechanical energy]] needed to break one [[liquid]] into tiny [[droplets]] within the other.
4.  [[Temperature]] matters: Most [[emulsions]] work best at [[Room Temperature]] or slightly warm. Too hot and [[Egg Yolk]]s can [[Scramble]]; too cold and the [[Fat]] can [[Solidify]], causing the [[emulsion]] to [[break]].

If your [[emulsion]] [[breaks]] (separates), you can often rescue it by:
*   Starting fresh with a new [[Egg Yolk]] in a clean [[Bowl]].
*   Slowly [[Whisk]]ing the broken [[sauce]] into the new [[yolk]].
*   Alternatively, adding a small amount of [[Boiling Water]] and [[Whisk]]ing vigorously can sometimes bring it back together.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Adding the [[dispersed liquid]] too quickly:** This is the most common error, overwhelming the [[emulsifier]] and preventing the formation of stable [[droplets]].
*   **Insufficient [[Whisking]] or [[Blending]]:** Not enough [[mechanical energy]] means the [[liquids]] won't properly disperse and combine.
*   **Incorrect [[Temperature]]:** Too much [[heat]] can cause [[Protein Denaturation]] in [[Egg Yolk]]s, leading to [[scrambling]], while too cold can solidify [[fats]], both [[breaking]] the [[emulsion]].
*   **Using too much of the [[dispersed liquid]]:** There's a limit to how much of one [[liquid]] an [[emulsifier]] can stabilize within another.

## When to Use It
Emulsification is fundamental to many classic and modern [[preparations]]:
*   [[Mayonnaise]]: [[Oil]] emulsified into [[Egg Yolk]]s, often with [[Vinegar]] or [[Lemon Juice]].
*   [[Hollandaise]]: Melted [[Butter]] emulsified into [[Egg Yolk]]s with [[Lemon Juice]].
*   [[Beurre Blanc]]: Cold [[Butter]] [[Whisked]] into a [[White Wine Reduction]].
*   [[Pasta Carbonara]]: [[Egg Yolk]]s emulsified with [[Pasta Water]] and rendered [[Pork Fat]].
*   [[Vinaigrette]]: [[Oil]] and [[Vinegar]] (an unstable [[emulsion]] that often requires [[Mustard]] to help stabilize, and [[shaking]] before use).
