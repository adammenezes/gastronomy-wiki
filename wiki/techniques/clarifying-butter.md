## Overview
Clarified butter is pure [[Butterfat]] produced by removing the [[water]] and [[Milk Solids]] from regular [[Butter]]. This process significantly raises the butter's [[Smoke Point]], making it an excellent [[fat]] for [[high-temperature cooking methods]] like [[Sautéing]] without the risk of [[burning]]. In [[Indian Cuisine]], a form of clarified butter that is cooked until the [[milk solids]] [[brown]] is known as [[Ghee]].

## The Science
Standard [[Butter]] is an [[emulsion]] consisting of about 80% [[Butterfat]], 15-18% [[water]], and 1-2% [[Milk Solids]]. These [[milk solids]], which contain [[proteins]] and [[sugars]], are responsible for butter's relatively low [[Smoke Point]] (around 350°F / 177°C), as they begin to [[burn]] and [[scorch]] at [[high temperatures]]. The process of clarifying butter involves gently [[heating]] it to break this [[emulsion]]. The [[water]] [[evaporates]], and the [[milk solids]] [[separate]], either [[foaming]] at the top or sinking to the bottom. By removing these [[solids]], the resulting [[pure fat]] can be heated to a much higher [[temperature]] (around 450°F / 232°C) before it begins to [[smoke]].

## How to Do It
The most efficient method for making clarified butter involves [[simmering]] and [[straining]].

1.  Place [[unsalted Butter]] in a [[heavy-bottomed Saucepan]] over [[low heat]].
2.  Allow the [[butter]] to [[melt]] completely and come to a gentle [[simmer]]. Do not [[stir]].
3.  As it [[simmers]], the [[butter]] will [[separate]] into three layers: a top layer of [[foam]] ([[Milk Solids]]), a middle layer of [[clear yellow Butterfat]], and a bottom layer of more [[milk solids]] and [[water]].
4.  Continue to [[simmer]] gently until the [[foam]] on top subsides and the [[liquid]] becomes [[clear]]. The [[solids]] at the bottom of the [[pan]] may begin to [[brown]] slightly, which will impart a [[nutty flavour]].
5.  Carefully remove the [[pan]] from the [[heat]]. [[Skim]] any remaining [[foam]] from the surface with a [[spoon]].
6.  Slowly [[pour]] the [[clear butterfat]] through a [[fine-mesh Strainer]] lined with [[cheesecloth]] into a clean, dry [[container]], leaving the [[milk solids]] at the bottom of the [[saucepan]] behind.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Heating too quickly:** Using [[high heat]] can cause the [[Milk Solids]] to [[burn]] before the [[water]] has fully evaporated, imparting a [[bitter]], [[acrid taste]] to the final product. Always use [[low, gentle heat]].
*   **Incomplete separation:** Failing to [[cook off]] all the [[water]] or [[strain out]] all the [[milk solids]] will lower the [[Smoke Point]] of the clarified butter and reduce its [[shelf life]]. Be patient and [[strain]] meticulously.
*   **Aggressive skimming:** While one method involves [[skimming]] the [[foam]] from the top, it can lead to a significant [[loss]] of usable [[Butterfat]]. The [[simmer-and-strain method]] provides a better [[yield]].

## When to Use It
Clarified butter is ideal for any application requiring the [[flavour of butter]] but a [[higher heat tolerance]].

*   **High-Heat Cooking:** Perfect for [[Sautéing]] [[vegetables]], [[pan-searing]] [[fish]] like [[scallops]] or [[sole]], and getting a [[golden-brown crust]] on [[meats]].
*   **Sauce Making:** It is the [[essential foundation]] for [[stable emulsified sauces]] in [[French Cuisine]], such as [[Hollandaise Sauce]] and [[Béarnaise Sauce]], as the [[absence of water]] and [[solids]] prevents the [[sauce]] from [[breaking]].
*   **Roasting:** Use it to [[baste]] [[poultry]] or [[vegetables]] for a [[rich flavour]] and [[even browning]] at [[high oven temperatures]].
*   **As a Dipping Sauce:** Its [[pure, rich flavour]] makes it a [[classic accompaniment]] for [[seafood]] like [[lobster]] and [[crab]].
