## Overview
Pâte Brisée is a classic French shortcrust pastry, forming the foundation for countless sweet and savory dishes. Its name translates to "broken pastry," referring to the method of "breaking" or cutting cold fat into flour. The standard ratio is often cited as 3 parts [[Flour]], 2 parts [[Fat]], and 1 part [[Water]]. The resulting dough is tender and flaky, making it a versatile vehicle for fillings in dishes ranging from a savory [[Quiche]] or [[Pot Pie]] to a sweet fruit [[Tart]]. Variations of this fundamental crust are found globally, including the rustic [[French Cuisine|French]] [[Galette]] and [[Croustade]], the [[Italian Cuisine|Italian]] *croustata*, and the [[Latin American Cuisine|Latin American]] [[Empanada]]. The primary fats used are [[Butter]], [[Lard]], or [[Vegetable Shortening]], each contributing a different flavor and texture.

## The Science
The characteristic flaky, tender texture of Pâte Brisée is a direct result of controlling [[Gluten]] development and managing the state of the fat.

- **Gluten Control:** [[Gluten]] is a protein network that forms when [[Flour]] is mixed with water, providing structure and elasticity. For a tender crust, this network must be kept weak and undeveloped. Over-mixing the dough creates a strong gluten network, resulting in a tough, leathery crust. This is why the instructions emphasize minimal mixing, only until the dough just comes together.
- **The Role of Fat:** Cold, solid [[Fat]] is the key to flakiness. By [[Cutting In|cutting the fat]] into the flour to the size of small peas, the flour particles get coated. This coating acts as a barrier, waterproofing the flour and physically inhibiting long [[Gluten]] strands from forming. When the pastry bakes, the solid pieces of fat melt, and the water within them turns to steam. This steam pushes the layers of dough apart, creating the small, flaky pockets that define a good shortcrust. If the fat is warm, it will melt into the flour, creating a mealy, greasy texture rather than distinct layers.

## How to Do It
This method uses the classic 3:2:1 ratio. All ingredients and equipment should be kept as cold as possible.

**Ingredients:**
- 1 lb./450 g [[Flour]], all-purpose
- 1 tsp./5 g [[Salt]]
- 8 oz./225 g [[Fat]] ([[Butter]], [[Lard]], or [[Shortening]]), chilled and cubed
- 4 oz./240 ml [[Water]], ice cold

**Method:**
1.  **Combine Dry Ingredients:** In a large [[Bowl]], combine the [[Flour]] and [[Salt]].
2.  **Cut in the Fat:** Add the chilled [[Fat]] to the flour. Using a [[Pastry Blender]] or your fingertips, cut the fat into the flour until the mixture resembles coarse crumbs with some pea-sized pieces of fat remaining.
3.  **Add Liquid and Form Dough:** Add the cold [[Water]] all at once. Mix gently with a fork or your hands only until the dough begins to form a cohesive ball. Do not overwork it.
4.  **Chill the Dough (First Rest):** Shape the dough into a flat disk, wrap it tightly in [[Plastic Wrap]], and chill in the refrigerator for at least one hour. This allows the [[Gluten]] to relax and the fat to re-solidify.
5.  **Roll Out:** Lightly flour a work surface and a [[Rolling Pin]]. Roll the chilled dough from the center outwards, rotating it a quarter turn after each roll to ensure even thickness. Aim for a thickness of approximately 1/8” (3mm).
6.  **Pan and Chill (Second Rest):** Transfer the dough to a [[Pie Pan]] or [[Tart Pan]] by folding it in quarters or rolling it onto the rolling pin. Gently ease the dough into the pan, being careful not to stretch it. Trim the excess, leaving a slight overhang. Chill or freeze the lined pan for at least 30 minutes to prevent shrinkage during baking.
7.  **For Pre-Baking (Blind-Baking):** If the recipe calls for a pre-baked shell, perform a technique called [[Docking]] by pricking the bottom of the crust with a [[Fork]]. Line the shell with [[Parchment Paper]] and fill with [[Pie Weights]] or dried beans. This prevents the crust from puffing up during baking.

## Common Mistakes
- **Over-mixing the Dough:** This develops too much [[Gluten]], leading to a tough, chewy crust that is difficult to roll out. *Fix:* Mix only until the ingredients are just combined and the dough forms a shaggy mass.
- **Using Warm Ingredients:** If the [[Fat]] or [[Water]] is not cold, the fat will melt into the flour instead of remaining in distinct pieces. This results in a greasy, mealy crust with no flakiness. *Fix:* Keep all ingredients, especially fat and water, thoroughly chilled. If the kitchen is warm, chill your bowl and tools as well.
- **Stretching the Dough:** Stretching the dough to fit the pan will cause it to shrink back significantly during baking, pulling away from the sides. *Fix:* Gently lift and settle the dough into the corners of the pan.
- **Not Resting the Dough:** Skipping the chilling periods prevents the [[Gluten]] from relaxing and the fat from firming up. This leads to shrinkage and a less tender final product. *Fix:* Always chill the dough for at least one hour after mixing and again for 30 minutes once it's in the pan.

## When to Use It
Pâte Brisée is a highly versatile pastry used as a container for a wide variety of fillings.

- **Savory Preparations:** It is the classic choice for savory pies like [[Pot Pie]], open-faced tarts such as [[Quiche]], and hand-held pastries like [[Empanadas]].
- **Sweet Preparations:** It provides a sturdy, buttery base for fruit [[Tart|Tarts]], [[Custard]] pies, and rustic, free-form [[Galette|Galettes]] where the crust is a key component of the flavor profile.
- **Blind-Baked Shells:** The technique of [[Blind-Baking]] allows the shell to be fully cooked and then filled with delicate, unbaked fillings like pastry cream, mousse, or fresh fruits.
