## Overview
Concassé, from the French verb *concasser* meaning "to crush or grind," is a foundational preparation technique in [[French Cuisine]]. It involves briefly [[Blanching]] an ingredient (most commonly a [[Tomato]]) to facilitate skin removal, followed by [[Peeling]], [[Seeding]], and dicing or roughly chopping the flesh. The result is a uniform, refined pulp free of tough skin and bitter seeds. This preparation is essential for creating smooth sauces, such as [[Tomato Sauce]], or as a fresh topping for dishes like [[Bruschetta]]. While most associated with tomatoes, the principles can be applied to other ingredients like [[Peppers]].

## The Science
The effectiveness of concassé relies on basic principles of food science. The initial [[Blanching]] step involves rapid [[Heat Transfer]], which causes the cells just under the [[Tomato]] skin to rupture and release enzymes. This action, combined with the expansion of air and water trapped under the skin, effectively loosens it from the flesh, making [[Peeling]] effortless. Immediately shocking the tomato in an [[Ice Bath]] halts the cooking process, preserving the firm texture and fresh flavor of the raw flesh. Removing the seeds and the surrounding gelatinous core eliminates bitterness and excess water, resulting in a purer, more concentrated tomato flavor.

## How to Do It
The most common application of this technique is for a tomato concassé.

1.  **Score:** Using a [[Paring Knife]], make a shallow "X" on the bottom of the [[Tomato]], opposite the stem.
2.  **Blanch:** Prepare a pot of simmering [[Water]] and a separate [[Ice Bath]]. Submerge the tomato in the simmering water for 15-30 seconds, just until the skin around the "X" begins to curl and pull away.
3.  **Shock:** Immediately transfer the tomato to the [[Ice Bath]] using a slotted spoon. This stops the cooking and cools it for safe handling.
4.  **Peel and Seed:** Remove the tomato from the water. The skin should now slip off easily. Cut the tomato in half horizontally and gently squeeze or scoop out the seeds and core.
5.  **Chop:** [[Dice]] or roughly chop the remaining tomato flesh to your desired size.

### Variations: Prepping Peppers
The source material also describes related techniques for preparing [[Peppers]]:

*   **Filleting:** This technique is especially useful for [[Hot Peppers]]. It involves carefully cutting away the fleshy part of the pepper, leaving the membrane and seeds behind. Since most of the [[Capsaicin]] (the compound responsible for heat) is concentrated in the membrane, this method allows for controlling the pepper's spiciness.
*   **Roasting:** To peel a pepper and enhance its sweetness, [[Roasting]] is the preferred method. This can be done over an open flame on a gas burner, on a [[Grill]], under a [[Salamander]] or [[Broiler]], or with a [[Blow Torch]]. The goal is to blacken and char the entire skin. Once charred, place the pepper in a covered bowl to steam, which further loosens the skin. Scrape the blackened skin off rather than rinsing it, as rinsing washes away flavorful oils and the smoky character developed during roasting.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Over-blanching:** Leaving the [[Tomato]] in simmering water for too long will begin to cook the flesh, resulting in a mushy, soft texture. The goal is only to loosen the skin.
*   **Skipping the Ice Bath:** Failing to shock the tomato allows residual heat to continue cooking the flesh, compromising its fresh texture and vibrant color.
*   **Rinsing Roasted Peppers:** While it may seem faster, rinsing charred [[Peppers]] under water washes away the smoky flavor compounds developed during [[Roasting]]. It is always better to scrape the skin off with the back of a knife.

## When to Use It
Concassé is used whenever the skin or seeds of a [[Tomato]] would be texturally undesirable.

*   **Sauces:** It forms the ideal base for smooth, refined sauces like [[Tomato Sauce]] or [[Marinara Sauce]].
*   **Fresh Preparations:** It is perfect for uncooked dishes like [[Bruschetta]] or fresh [[Salsa]], where a clean, uniform texture is desired.
*   **Soups and Stews:** In classic [[French Cuisine]], it is used to add tomato flavor to dishes without introducing the toughness of the skin.
