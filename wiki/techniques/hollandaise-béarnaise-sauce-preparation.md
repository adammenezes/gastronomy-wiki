## Overview
[[Hollandaise]] and [[Béarnaise Sauce]] are classic [[French Cuisine]] [[emulsions]] renowned for their rich, creamy texture and vibrant flavor. They are fundamentally [[emulsions]] of [[melted butter]] and [[egg yolks]]. The preparation begins with a [[reduction]] of [[wine]], [[wine vinegar]], and [[peppercorns]]. For [[Béarnaise Sauce]], [[shallots]] and [[tarragon]] are added to this reduction base. These sauces are versatile accompaniments, elevating dishes from [[Eggs Benedict]] to [[grilled steak]].

## The Science
The core of [[Hollandaise]] and [[Béarnaise Sauce]] preparation is the creation of a stable [[emulsion]]. An [[emulsion]] is a mixture of two immiscible liquids, in this case, [[fat]] (from [[melted butter]]) and [[water]] (from the [[reduction]] and [[egg yolks]]), forming an [[oil-in-water emulsion]]. The primary emulsifying agent is [[lecithin]], a [[phospholipid]] naturally present in [[egg yolks]]. [[Lecithin]] molecules have both [[hydrophilic]] (water-attracting) and [[hydrophobic]] (fat-attracting) ends, allowing them to surround tiny droplets of [[fat]] and prevent them from coalescing, thereby stabilizing the [[emulsion]].

The [[acids]] (from [[wine]], [[wine vinegar]], or [[lemon]]) are crucial as they raise the [[coagulation point]] of the [[egg yolk]] [[proteins]]. This prevents premature [[protein denaturation]] and [[coagulation]] (scrambling) of the yolks when heated, providing a wider temperature window for successful emulsification. Maintaining the sauce at a low temperature, around 120°F/50°C, is vital. Higher temperatures can lead to the [[egg yolks]] overcooking and the [[emulsion]] breaking, while lower temperatures can cause the [[butter]] to solidify, also breaking the sauce. The instruction to prepare as close to service as possible and discard after 4 hours is due to the presence of [[egg yolks]] and the risk of bacterial growth within the [[Food Temperature Danger Zone]].

## How to Do It
1.  **Prepare the Reduction:** For [[Hollandaise]], combine [[wine]], [[wine vinegar]], and [[peppercorns]] in a [[saucepan]]. For [[Béarnaise Sauce]], add [[shallots]] and [[tarragon]]. Reduce over medium heat until only a tablespoon or two of liquid remains. Strain and let cool slightly.
2.  **Combine Yolks and Reduction:** In a heatproof bowl (ideally set over a [[double boiler]]), [[whisk]] [[egg yolks]] with the cooled [[reduction]].
3.  **Temper and Thicken:** Place the bowl over simmering water (not boiling) on the [[double boiler]]. Continuously [[whisk]] the mixture until it thickens to a ribbon stage, reaching a temperature of about 140-150°F (60-65°C). This process is a form of [[tempering]] the yolks.
4.  **Emulsify with Butter:** Gradually drizzle in warm [[melted butter]] (often [[clarified butter]] is preferred for stability and purity of flavor) while continuously [[whisking]] vigorously. Begin with a very slow stream until the [[emulsion]] starts to form, then you can increase the rate slightly. The sauce should become thick and glossy.
5.  **Season and Finish:** Season with [[salt]], [[white pepper]], and a squeeze of [[lemon juice]] (for [[Hollandaise]]) or chopped fresh [[tarragon]] (for [[Béarnaise Sauce]]). Adjust consistency with a few drops of warm water if too thick.
6.  **Hold and Serve:** Hold the sauce at a low temperature, around 120°F/50°C, in a warm spot or a very low oven. Serve as soon as possible, ideally within 4 hours, due to the presence of [[egg yolks]] and the [[Food Temperature Danger Zone]].

## Common Mistakes
*   **Overheating the Yolks:** Leads to [[protein denaturation]] and [[coagulation]], resulting in scrambled eggs instead of a smooth sauce. Use a [[double boiler]] and a [[thermometer]] to maintain temperatures below 160°F (71°C).
*   **Adding Butter Too Quickly:** Causes the [[emulsion]] to break, as the [[lecithin]] in the [[egg yolks]] cannot encapsulate the [[fat]] droplets fast enough. Add [[melted butter]] in a slow, steady stream while [[whisking]] constantly.
*   **Cold Butter:** If the [[melted butter]] cools too much, it can solidify upon contact with the [[egg yolk]] mixture, breaking the [[emulsion]]. Ensure the butter is warm but not scorching hot.
*   **Insufficient Whisking:** Proper [[whisking]] is essential to create small, evenly dispersed [[fat]] droplets, which are necessary for a stable [[emulsion]]. Whisk vigorously and continuously.
*   **Holding Too Long/Wrong Temperature:** Holding the sauce for extended periods, especially within the [[Food Temperature Danger Zone]], increases the risk of bacterial growth and sauce breakdown. Prepare close to service and discard after 4 hours.

## When to Use It
[[Hollandaise]] is famously paired with [[Eggs Benedict]], [[asparagus]], and various [[fish]] dishes like [[poached salmon]]. [[Béarnaise Sauce]] is a classic accompaniment to [[grilled steak]], [[roast beef]], and other [[red meats]], as well as [[poultry]] and [[fish]]. These sauces elevate simple ingredients, adding richness and a luxurious texture. They are staples in [[French Cuisine]] and often found in [[brunch]] menus and fine dining.
