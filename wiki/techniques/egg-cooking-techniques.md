## Overview
[[Egg Cookery]] encompasses a wide range of [[Cooking Techniques]] that transform the versatile [[Egg]] into countless dishes. From delicate [[Custard]]s and [[Soufflé]]s to simple [[Fried Egg]]s and [[Scrambled Eggs]], the method of cooking dramatically influences the final texture, flavor, and appearance. This guide explores the fundamental principles behind both [[Moist-Heat Cooking Techniques]] and [[Dry-Heat Cooking Methods]] for eggs, emphasizing the critical role of [[Temperature Control]] in achieving desired results and preventing common pitfalls. Whether creating a savory [[Quiche]] or a sweet [[Crème Brûlée]], understanding how [[Egg Protein]]s react to heat is key to culinary success.

## The Science
The core principle behind most [[Egg Cookery]] is [[Protein Coagulation]]. [[Egg Protein]]s, primarily [[Albumin]] in the [[Egg White]] and [[Lipoprotein]]s in the [[Egg Yolk]], are long chains of [[Amino Acid]]s. When heated, these proteins undergo [[Protein Denaturation]], unfolding from their coiled structures and then bonding with each other to form a network that traps water, causing the egg to solidify or thicken.

The key to successful egg cookery lies in precise [[Temperature Control]].
*   **Low Temperatures** (e.g., for [[Custard]]s, [[Sous Vide Egg]]s, [[Onsen Tamago]]) promote a gradual and even coagulation, resulting in tender, smooth textures. Overheating [[Custard]]s, for instance, causes excessive protein contraction, expelling moisture in a process called [[Syneresis]], leading to a curdled, grainy, or rubbery texture.
*   **High Temperatures** (e.g., [[Boiling]] eggs vigorously) can lead to rapid, uneven coagulation, resulting in tough [[Egg White]]s. In [[Hard-Boiled Egg]]s, prolonged high heat can also cause a chemical reaction between [[Iron]] in the [[Egg Yolk]] and [[Hydrogen Sulfide]] released from the [[Egg White]]s, forming [[Iron Sulfide]], which manifests as an unappetizing green ring around the yolk.
*   **[[Tempering]]** is a technique used for [[Custard]]s to prevent immediate coagulation of [[Egg]]s when adding hot liquids. It involves gradually raising the temperature of the [[Egg]]s by slowly [[Whisking]] in a small amount of the hot liquid, allowing the proteins to adjust to the heat without curdling.
*   **[[Aeration]]** is vital for dishes like [[Soufflé]]s and some [[Omelet]]s. [[Whisking]] [[Egg White]]s incorporates air, creating a foam. The heat then sets this foam, trapping the air and giving the dish its light, airy texture. Over-mixing or improper folding can deflate these air pockets.
*   **[[Emulsification]]** plays a role in dishes like [[Scrambled Eggs]] when [[Milk]] or [[Cream]] is added, helping to create a smoother, richer texture by suspending fat droplets. While not explicitly detailed in the text, [[Egg Yolk]]s are also powerful [[Emulsifier]]s, crucial for sauces like [[Hollandaise]] or [[Mayonnaise]].

## How to Do It
Mastering [[Egg Cookery]] involves understanding the specific requirements of different preparations, but general principles apply.

### General Principles
1.  **Preparation**: Always start with fresh [[Egg]]s. For most preparations, [[Whisking]] [[Egg Yolk]]s and [[Egg White]]s thoroughly ensures a homogeneous mixture. Season with [[Salt]] and [[Pepper]] as desired. For [[Custard]]s, [[Tempering]] the [[Egg]]s with warm [[Milk]] or [[Cream]] is crucial to prevent curdling.
2.  **Heat Application**:
    *   **Moist-Heat (e.g., [[Custard]]s, [[Poached Egg]]s, [[Eggs Cooked in the Shell]]):** Use low to moderate heat. For [[Custard]]s, heat [[Milk]] or [[Cream]] to 130-170°F (55-77°C) before combining with [[Egg]]s. Cook [[Custard]]s to 176-183°F (80-84°C). For [[Poached Egg]]s, maintain water at 180°F (82°C). For [[Eggs Cooked in the Shell]], a gentle simmer is preferred over a rolling boil.
    *   **Dry-Heat (e.g., [[Fried Egg]]s, [[Scrambled Eggs]], [[Omelet]]s, [[Baked Eggs]]):** Use moderate heat. Optimal pan temperature for [[Fried Egg]]s is 250-300°F (120-150°C). For [[Baked Eggs]] or [[Quiche]], bake at 325°F (160°C). [[Soufflé]]s require a hotter oven, 400-425°F (200-218°C), to achieve maximum rise.
3.  **Monitoring**: Use an [[Instant-Read Thermometer]] for precise temperature control, especially for [[Custard]]s and [[Sous Vide Egg]]s. For other methods, observe visual cues: [[Egg White]]s setting, [[Egg Yolk]]s thickening, or browning.
4.  **Finishing**: Remove eggs from heat immediately once desired doneness is reached to prevent [[Carryover Cooking]]. For [[Eggs Cooked in the Shell]] or [[Poached Egg]]s, cool immediately in an [[Ice Water Bath]] to stop the cooking process and prevent green yolks.

### Example: Basic Custard (Stovetop)
1.  **Prepare**: Heat [[Milk]] or [[Cream]] (e.g., 130-170°F/55-77°C) in a [[Saucepan]]. Add [[Sugar]] and [[Vanilla]] for sweet, or [[Salt]], [[Pepper]], and [[Nutmeg]] for savory.
2.  **Temper**: In a separate [[Bowl]], [[Whisk]] [[Egg Yolk]]s (or [[Whole Egg]]s). Gradually [[Whisk]] a small amount of the hot [[Milk]] into the [[Egg]]s to raise their temperature.
3.  **Combine & Cook**: Slowly add the tempered [[Egg]] mixture back into the [[Saucepan]] with the remaining hot [[Milk]]. Cook over low heat, stirring constantly with a [[Whisk]] or [[Heat Resistant Spatula]], until the mixture thickens and reaches 170°F (77°C). Do not boil.
4.  **Serve**: Remove from heat immediately. Serve warm or chill.

### Example: Fried Eggs
1.  **Prepare**: Crack the [[Egg]]s into a separate dish.
2.  **Heat Pan**: Heat a well-seasoned or [[Nonstick Sauté Pan]] over moderate heat (250-300°F/120-150°C). Add [[Whole Butter]] or [[Oil]] for flavor and to prevent sticking.
3.  **Cook**: Gently slide [[Egg]]s into the pan. Cook until the [[Egg White]]s are set and the [[Egg Yolk]] reaches desired doneness. For "over" styles, carefully flip the egg. Alternatively, baste with hot fat or add a few drops of water and cover to steam the surface.
4.  **Serve**: Season with [[Salt]] and [[Pepper]] and serve immediately.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Overcooking**: The most frequent error. Leads to rubbery [[Egg White]]s, grainy or curdled [[Custard]]s, and green-tinged [[Egg Yolk]]s in [[Hard-Boiled Egg]]s. Always remove from heat slightly before desired doneness to account for [[Carryover Cooking]].
*   **High Heat**: Cooking eggs at too high a temperature causes rapid [[Protein Coagulation]], resulting in tough textures and potential burning. Always use low to moderate heat for most egg preparations.
*   **Not [[Tempering]]**: For [[Custard]]s, adding cold [[Egg]]s directly to hot liquid will cause them to curdle instantly.
*   **Improper [[Whisking]]/Folding**: For [[Scrambled Eggs]] or [[Omelet]]s, insufficient [[Whisking]] can lead to uneven textures. For [[Soufflé]]s, over-mixing [[Egg White]]s or folding them too aggressively will deflate the air, preventing rise.
*   **Browning [[Scrambled Eggs]]**: Classic [[Scrambled Eggs]] should be light, fluffy, and moist, not browned or hard-crumbly. Remove from heat when slightly underdone.
*   **Ignoring Variables**: Factors like [[Egg Size]], [[Egg Age]], starting temperature (cold vs. room temperature), and the cooking medium (water vs. steamer) all affect cooking time and results. Adjust accordingly.

## When to Use It
[[Egg Cookery]] is fundamental across global [[Cuisine]]s and is used for a vast array of dishes:
*   **Breakfast & Brunch**: [[Fried Egg]]s, [[Scrambled Eggs]], [[Poached Egg]]s (e.g., for [[Eggs Benedict]]), [[Omelet]]s (e.g., [[Classic French Omelet]], [[Frittata]]), [[Hard-Boiled Egg]]s, [[Soft-Boiled Egg]]s.
*   **Desserts**: [[Custard]]s like [[Crème Brûlée]], [[Flan]], [[Crème Caramel]], [[Crème Anglaise]], and [[Soufflé]]s.
*   **Savory Dishes**: [[Quiche]], [[Shirred Eggs]] ([[Oeufs sur le Plat]]), [[Savory Soufflé]]s, [[Pasta Carbonara]] (using [[Egg Yolk]]s for sauce).
*   **Specialty Preparations**: [[Mollet Egg]]s, [[Sous Vide Egg]]s, [[Coddled Egg]]s, and [[Onsen Tamago]] (a [[Japanese Cuisine]] specialty).
*   **Ingredient**: Eggs are also used as [[Binder]]s, [[Leavening Agent]]s, and [[Emulsifier]]s in many other recipes, demonstrating their versatility beyond being the main component.
