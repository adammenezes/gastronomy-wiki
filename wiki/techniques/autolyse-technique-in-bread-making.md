## Overview
Autolyse is a bread-making technique that involves a brief rest period after mixing only the [[Flour]] and [[Water]] from a recipe. Before ingredients like [[Salt]] or [[Yeast]] are added, this pause allows the flour to fully hydrate and activates natural [[Enzyme|enzymes]] within the dough. This simple step significantly improves the dough's extensibility and handling, requiring less [[Kneading]] to develop the [[Gluten]] network. The result is often a loaf, such as a [[Sourdough Bread]] or [[Baguette]], with better volume, a more open crumb structure, improved flavour, and a longer shelf life.

## The Science
The autolyse process is driven by two primary scientific principles: hydration and enzymatic activity.

1.  **Hydration:** When [[Flour]] and [[Water]] are combined, the proteins [[Glutenin]] and [[Gliadin]] begin to absorb water and form the initial bonds of the [[Gluten]] network. Allowing this to happen passively without the interference of other ingredients ensures complete and even hydration.

2.  **Enzymatic Activity:** Flour contains naturally occurring [[Enzyme|enzymes]] that become active when hydrated. The two most important for autolyse are [[Protease]] and [[Amylase]].
    *   **Protease** begins to break down the [[Protein]] strands, which relaxes the dough and increases its extensibility (stretchiness). This makes the dough easier to shape later on.
    *   **Amylase** converts the flour's starches into simple [[Sugar|sugars]]. This not only adds subtle flavour to the final bread but also provides a readily available food source for the [[Yeast]] once it is added, leading to more vigorous [[Fermentation]].

[[Salt]] is excluded during this stage because it tightens the [[Gluten]] network, which would inhibit full hydration and slow enzymatic activity.

## How to Do It
1.  In a [[Mixing Bowl]], combine all the [[Flour]] and [[Water]] (or other liquids) called for in your recipe.
2.  Mix with a spatula or your hands just until no dry flour remains. The dough does not need to be smooth.
3.  Cover the bowl and let it rest at room temperature for a period ranging from 20 minutes to one hour, or sometimes longer depending on the specific recipe.
4.  After the rest period, add the [[Salt]], [[Yeast]], or [[Sourdough Starter]], and any other remaining ingredients.
5.  Proceed with your recipe's instructions for [[Mixing]] and [[Kneading]]. You may notice that the dough comes together more quickly and requires less kneading time than it would without the autolyse.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Adding Salt or Yeast Too Early:** Introducing [[Salt]] or [[Yeast]] during the initial mix negates the purpose of the technique. Salt inhibits the enzymes and tightens [[Gluten]], while yeast would begin [[Fermentation]] prematurely.
*   **Resting for Too Long:** In a very warm environment, or with flours high in enzymatic activity (like freshly milled rye), an excessively long autolyse can cause the [[Protease]] enzymes to break down the gluten structure too much, resulting in a slack, weak dough that won't hold its shape.
*   **Incomplete Initial Mix:** Failing to ensure all the [[Flour]] is hydrated means some parts of the dough will not benefit from the autolyse, leading to an inconsistent final texture.

## When to Use It
This technique is particularly beneficial for artisan-style breads where an open, airy crumb and good extensibility are desired. It is commonly used in recipes for:
*   [[Sourdough Bread]]
*   [[Baguette]]
*   [[Ciabatta]]
*   High-hydration [[Pizza Dough]]

It is also highly effective when working with [[Whole Wheat Flour]] or other whole grains, as the bran and germ particles take longer to absorb water fully. An autolyse gives them the time needed to soften, resulting in a less dense final product.

*   [[Gluten]]: The protein network that gives bread its structure, which is passively developed during autolyse.
*   [[Fermentation]]: The process driven by yeast, which benefits from the simple sugars created during autolyse.
*   [[Hydration (Dough)]]: The ratio of water to flour in a dough, a key factor that autolyse helps to manage effectively, especially in high-hydration doughs.
