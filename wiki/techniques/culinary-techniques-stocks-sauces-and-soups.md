## Overview
[[Hollandaise]] and [[Béarnaise]] are classic [[French Cuisine]] [[sauces]], renowned for their [[rich]], [[creamy]] [[texture]] and vibrant [[flavor]]. They are examples of warm [[emulsion sauces]], where [[clarified butter]] is suspended in a base of [[egg yolk]] and [[acid]]. The [[technique]] involves carefully combining these [[ingredients]] while maintaining a specific [[temperature]] to create a stable, [[velvety]] [[sauce]]. [[Hollandaise]] is typically made with [[lemon juice]] or [[white wine vinegar]], while [[Béarnaise]] is a derivative of [[Hollandaise]], flavored with a [[reduction]] of [[shallots]], [[tarragon]], [[peppercorns]], and [[vinegar]].

## The Science
The preparation of [[Hollandaise]] and [[Béarnaise]] relies on the principle of [[emulsification]], specifically creating a stable [[oil-in-water emulsion]]. [[Egg yolk]] acts as the primary [[emulsifier]] due to its high content of [[lecithin]], a phospholipid that has both hydrophilic ([[water]]-attracting) and lipophilic ([[fat]]-attracting) properties. When [[egg yolks]] are [[whisked]] with an [[acid]] (like [[lemon juice]] or [[vinegar]]) over gentle [[heat]], the [[lecithin]] molecules surround tiny droplets of [[clarified butter]] as it's slowly incorporated, preventing them from coalescing.

[[Temperature control]] is crucial. The [[sauce]] must be warm enough to keep the [[butter]] liquid and allow the [[lecithin]] to function effectively, but not so [[hot]] as to cause [[protein denaturation]] in the [[egg yolks]], which would lead to [[scrambling]] and a [[broken emulsion]]. The continuous [[whisking]] action helps to break the [[butter]] into minute droplets, increasing the surface area for the [[lecithin]] to bind to, thereby stabilizing the [[colloidal dispersion]].

## How to Do It
To prepare these [[sauces]], you will need a [[whisk]], a [[heatproof bowl]], and a [[double boiler]] or [[bain-marie]].

1.  **Prepare [[Clarified Butter]]:** Gently [[melt]] [[unsalted butter]] over low [[heat]]. [[Skim]] off the [[milk solids]] that rise to the top and carefully [[pour]] off the clear [[yellow fat]], leaving the [[water]] and remaining [[milk solids]] at the bottom. Keep the [[clarified butter]] warm (around 120-140°F / 50-60°C).
2.  **Prepare the [[Egg Base]]:** In the [[heatproof bowl]], combine [[egg yolks]] with a small amount of [[acid]] (e.g., [[lemon juice]] for [[Hollandaise]], or a [[vinegar]] [[reduction]] with [[shallots]] and [[tarragon]] for [[Béarnaise]]).
3.  **Cook the [[Egg Base]] ([[Sabayon]]):** Place the [[bowl]] over a [[double boiler]] with [[simmering]] (not [[boiling]]) [[water]], ensuring the bottom of the [[bowl]] does not touch the [[water]]. Continuously [[whisk]] the [[egg yolk]] mixture vigorously until it [[thickens]], lightens in [[color]], and forms a ribbon when the [[whisk]] is lifted. This process is a form of [[tempering]] and creates a [[sabayon]]. The [[temperature]] should not exceed 160°F (71°C) to prevent [[scrambling]].
4.  **[[Emulsify]] with [[Butter]]:** Remove the [[bowl]] from the [[heat]] (or briefly lift it off the [[double boiler]]) and slowly, in a very thin [[stream]], begin to [[drizzle]] in the warm [[clarified butter]] while continuously [[whisking]] vigorously. The [[sauce]] will gradually [[thicken]] and become [[opaque]]. If the [[sauce]] becomes too [[thick]], add a few drops of warm [[water]] or [[acid]].
5.  **[[Season]] and [[Finish]]:** Once all the [[butter]] is incorporated and the [[sauce]] has reached the desired [[consistency]], [[season]] with [[salt]], [[white pepper]], and additional [[lemon juice]] if making [[Hollandaise]]. For [[Béarnaise]], [[strain]] the [[tarragon]]-[[shallot]]-[[vinegar]] [[reduction]] into the [[sauce]] and [[stir]] in fresh chopped [[tarragon]] and [[chervil]]. Serve immediately or keep warm in a [[thermos]] or over a very gentle [[bain-marie]] for a short period.

## Common Mistakes
*   **[[Broken Emulsion]]:** This is the most common issue, often caused by adding the [[clarified butter]] too quickly, the [[sauce]] being too [[hot]], or the [[sauce]] being too [[cold]].
    *   *Fix:* If the [[sauce]] [[breaks]], take a fresh [[egg yolk]] or a teaspoon of [[hot water]]/[[acid]] in a clean [[bowl]] and slowly [[whisk]] in the [[broken sauce]], [[drop by drop]], until it [[re-emulsifies]].
*   **[[Scrambled Eggs]]:** [[Overheating]] the [[egg yolks]] will cause them to [[curdle]].
    *   *Fix:* Always use a [[double boiler]] and remove the [[bowl]] from the [[heat]] periodically while [[whisking]] to control the [[temperature]].
*   **[[Too Thin/Thick]]:** If the [[sauce]] is too [[thin]], it may need more [[clarified butter]] (added slowly). If it's too [[thick]], [[whisk]] in a few drops of warm [[water]] or [[acid]].
*   **[[Lack of Flavor]]:** Ensure your [[clarified butter]] is fresh and your [[acid]] (e.g., [[lemon juice]]) is bright. For [[Béarnaise]], a well-[[reduced]] and flavorful [[tarragon]]-[[shallot]] [[reduction]] is key.

## When to Use It
[[Hollandaise]] is a classic accompaniment for [[Eggs Benedict]], [[poached eggs]], [[asparagus]], [[broccoli]], and [[poached salmon]]. Its bright, [[buttery]] [[flavor]] complements [[delicate ingredients]].

[[Béarnaise]] is traditionally served with [[grilled meats]] like [[steak frites]], [[filet mignon]], and [[lamb chops]]. Its [[herbaceous]], [[peppery]] notes stand up well to [[richer flavors]]. Both [[sauces]] are best served fresh.
