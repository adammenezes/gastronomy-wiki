## Overview
[[Hollandaise]], [[Béarnaise]], and their derivatives make up a class of [[Emulsion Sauces]] that also includes the cold sauce [[Mayonnaise]]. These sauces are prepared by [[emulsifying]] [[melted butter]] or [[oil]] into [[egg yolks]], through rapidly [[whisking]] the ingredients, to break the [[butter]] or [[oil]] into tiny droplets that are held in suspension by [[lecithin]] in the [[yolks]]. These sauces often include [[wine]], [[wine vinegar]], or [[lemon]] (sometimes as cooked [[reductions]]), which help to stabilize the sauce and prevent [[curdling]]. Another important warm butter emulsion is [[Beurre Blanc]], and [[Beurre Monté]] refers to melted butter that remains emulsified at higher temperatures.

## The Science
[[Emulsion sauces]] are formed when two immiscible liquids, typically [[oil]] (or [[melted butter]]) and [[water]] (present in [[egg yolks]] or other liquids), are dispersed into each other. The key emulsifying agent in [[egg yolks]] is [[lecithin]], a [[phospholipid]] that has both [[hydrophilic]] (water-attracting) and [[hydrophobic]] (fat-attracting) properties. This allows [[lecithin]] to surround tiny [[fat]] droplets, preventing them from coalescing and separating.

The inclusion of [[wine]], [[wine vinegar]], or [[lemon]] (often as cooked [[reductions]]) causes [[protein denaturation]] in the [[egg yolks]], which helps to stabilize the sauce by creating a stronger network around the [[fat]] droplets. These [[acids]] also raise the [[coagulation point]] of the [[yolks]] to about 190°F/88˚C, helping to prevent [[curdling]] when heated.

[[Butter]] itself is an [[emulsion]] of about 2% [[milk solids]], 80% [[milk fats]] (like [[clarified butter]]), and about 18% [[water]]. At 160˚F/71˚C, [[butter]] normally breaks down into its separate components, but in a [[beurre monté]], the [[butter]] is heated in such a way that it holds an [[emulsion]] up to 180-190˚F/ 82-88˚C due to careful [[whisking]] and [[temperature control]].

## How to Do It

### General Emulsion Sauces (Hollandaise/Béarnaise)
1.  Combine [[egg yolks]] with a small amount of [[liquid]] (e.g., [[water]], [[wine reduction]]).
2.  Gradually add [[melted butter]] or [[oil]] in a slow, steady stream while rapidly [[whisking]]. This is often done over a [[double boiler]] or in a [[bain-marie]] to provide gentle heat.
3.  Continue [[whisking]] until the [[fat]] is fully incorporated and the sauce thickens.
4.  Season and add [[acid]] (e.g., [[lemon juice]], [[wine vinegar]]) to taste.
5.  Maintain a serving temperature of 120-130°F/50-55˚C, ideally using an [[insulated thermos]] for best results, and serve as close to preparation as possible.

### Rescuing a Broken Emulsion
1.  Start with a clean [[bowl]] and add one fresh [[egg yolk]] per two original [[yolks]] used in the broken sauce, along with 1 Tb. [[water]] per new [[yolk]].
2.  [[Whisk]] this mixture in a [[hot water bath]] until it begins to thicken.
3.  Remove from the heat and, using the same initial setup, gradually [[whisk]] the broken sauce into the fresh, cooked [[egg yolks]].
4.  The sauce may become thick, so more [[water]] may be needed to adjust consistency. More [[butter]] may also be needed to adjust the ratio of [[egg]] to [[butter]].

### Beurre Blanc Sauce
1.  Prepare a [[reduction]] of [[wine]], [[wine vinegar]], and [[shallots]].
2.  Optionally, add [[reduced cream]] to the [[reduction]] as a [[stabilizer]].
3.  Over low heat, gradually [[whisk]] diced [[cold butter]] into the [[reduction]], one piece at a time, until it is fully [[emulsified]] into the sauce.
4.  Hold in a [[thermos]] at 120°F/50˚C.

### Beurre Monté
1.  Boil a very small quantity of [[water]] (1-2 oz. /30-60 ml).
2.  Once the [[water]] has come to a boil, turn the heat down to very low.
3.  Start [[whisking]] [[cold butter]] into the [[water]], one or two chunks at a time.
4.  Add more [[butter]] whenever the chunks have melted. Once the [[emulsion]] is started, more [[butter]] can be added at a time.
5.  Continue adding [[butter]] while [[whisking]] until the desired quantity of [[beurre monté]] is achieved.
6.  To avoid breaking the sauce, the [[beurre monté]] must then be held warm, but below 190°F/88˚C.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Overheating:** [[Emulsion sauces]] are sensitive to heat. Holding above 130°F/55˚C (or 190°F/88˚C for [[beurre monté]]) can cause the [[emulsion]] to break due to [[protein denaturation]] and [[fat]] separation. Use a [[double boiler]] for gentle heat and an [[insulated thermos]] for holding.
*   **Underheating/Chilling:** If the sauce gets too cold, the [[fat]] can solidify, causing the [[emulsion]] to break. Maintain recommended holding temperatures.
*   **Adding Fat Too Quickly:** When making the initial [[emulsion]], adding [[melted butter]] or [[oil]] too rapidly can overwhelm the [[emulsifier]] ([[lecithin]]) and prevent proper [[emulsification]]. Add gradually while [[whisking]] vigorously.
*   **Insufficient Whisking:** Not [[whisking]] vigorously enough prevents the [[fat]] from breaking into small enough droplets to be suspended, leading to a broken sauce. A [[whisk]] or [[immersion blender]] is crucial.
*   **Incorrect Ratio:** An imbalance between [[fat]] and [[emulsifier]] ([[egg yolks]]) can lead to instability. The rescue method helps re-establish this balance.

## When to Use It
[[Emulsion sauces]] like [[Hollandaise]] and [[Béarnaise]] are classic accompaniments for [[eggs benedict]], [[asparagus]], [[fish]], and [[steak]]. [[Mayonnaise]] is a versatile cold sauce used in [[sandwiches]], [[salads]], and as a base for other [[dressings]]. [[Beurre Blanc]] is often paired with [[fish]], [[seafood]], and [[poultry]]. [[Beurre Monté]] serves as a rich sauce for [[poached fish]], a [[resting medium]] for [[cooked meats]] (like [[steak]]), or as an ingredient in other [[sauces]]. It can also be used for [[poaching]] delicate items.
