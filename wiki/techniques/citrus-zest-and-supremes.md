## Overview
Zesting and supreming are two fundamental [[Knife Skills]] for preparing [[Citrus Fruit]] like [[Lemon]], [[Lime]], [[Orange]], and [[Grapefruit]]. Zesting is the process of scraping or cutting the outermost layer of the peel, which is rich in aromatic oils, to add intense citrus flavor to dishes without adding acidity. Supreming is the technique of segmenting the fruit by cutting away the peel, pith, and membrane, yielding pure, jewel-like segments of citrus flesh. These techniques are essential in everything from [[Baked Goods]] and [[Vinaigrettes]] to elegant [[Salads]] and [[Garnishes]].

## The Science
The bright, potent aroma of citrus zest comes from [[Essential Oils]], such as [[Limonene]], which are stored in the colourful outer layer of the peel known as the [[Flavedo]]. This layer is distinct from the white, spongy layer beneath it called the [[Pith]] or albedo. The pith contains bitter-tasting [[Flavonoids]] and should be avoided during zesting to prevent imparting bitterness to the final dish.

When supreming, the goal is to remove the tough membranes that separate the fruit's segments (carpels). These membranes are primarily composed of [[Cellulose]] and [[Pectin]], which give them a chewy and sometimes unpleasant texture. By carefully cutting the segments away from the membrane, the resulting supremes offer a much softer, more refined mouthfeel.

## How to Do It
**Zesting**
1.  Thoroughly wash and dry the [[Citrus Fruit]].
2.  Using a [[Microplane]], gently grate the surface of the fruit, rotating it as you go. Be careful to only remove the coloured [[Flavedo]] and not the white [[Pith]] underneath.
3.  Alternatively, use a channel [[Zester]] to create long, thin strips for [[Garnishes]], or a [[Vegetable Peeler]] to remove wider strips that can be minced.

**Supreming**
1.  Place the fruit on a [[Cutting Board]]. Using a sharp [[Chef's Knife]] or [[Paring Knife]], slice off the top and bottom of the fruit to create two flat surfaces.
2.  Stand the fruit on one of its flat ends. Carefully slice downwards, following the curve of the fruit, to remove the peel and all of the white pith. Rotate the fruit and repeat until it is completely peeled.
3.  Hold the peeled fruit in your non-dominant hand over a bowl to catch any juice. Make a V-shaped cut on either side of each membrane to release the segment, letting it fall into the bowl.
4.  Once all segments are removed, squeeze the remaining membrane core over the bowl to extract the last of the juice.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Zesting too deep:** Scraping into the white [[Pith]] will introduce a bitter flavour. Use a light touch and stop as soon as you see white.
*   **Using a dull knife for supreming:** A dull [[Knife]] will crush the delicate fruit segments and tear the membranes, resulting in a messy final product and loss of juice.
*   **Leaving membrane attached:** Failing to remove all the membrane from the supremes results in a tough, chewy texture.
*   **Wasting the juice:** The juice from the leftover core is flavourful and perfect for use in a [[Vinaigrette]] or [[Sauce]]. Always work over a bowl to collect it.

## When to Use It
Zest is used to impart a concentrated citrus flavour and aroma in recipes where extra liquid or acidity is undesirable. It is common in [[Baked Goods]] like [[Lemon Tart]] and [[Scones]], as well as in [[Marinades]], rubs, and as a finishing garnish on dishes like [[Risotto]].

Supremes are used when presentation and texture are paramount. They elevate [[Fruit Salads]], provide a burst of juicy flavour in green [[Salads]], and serve as an elegant component in [[Desserts]] and savoury dishes like [[Duck à l'Orange]].
