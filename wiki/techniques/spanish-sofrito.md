## Overview
Sofrito is a foundational aromatic base used extensively in [[Spanish Cuisine]]. It typically consists of [[Onion]] and [[Garlic]] slow-cooked in [[Olive Oil]], with the addition of [[Red Bell Pepper]] or [[Green Bell Pepper]] and [[Tomato]]. This fragrant mixture serves as the first layer of flavor in countless dishes, including the classic rice dish [[Paella]], various [[Stew]]s, [[Tomato Sauce]]s, and soups like [[Gazpacho]]. It is distinct from the Italian [[Soffritto]], which uses carrot and celery instead of peppers and tomato. Depending on the regional tradition, a sofrito may also include [[Bay Leaf]], [[Smoked Paprika]] (pimentón), or [[Rosemary]].

## The Science
The science of sofrito lies in the slow, low-heat extraction of flavor compounds. Cooking the vegetables gently in [[Olive Oil]] breaks down their cellular structure, releasing aromatic molecules. The oil acts as a solvent, capturing fat-soluble flavors from the [[Garlic]], [[Onion]], and peppers, and distributing them throughout the dish. The slow cooking process encourages a mild [[Caramelization]] of the natural sugars in the vegetables, developing a deep, sweet, and complex flavor profile without the browning associated with the [[Maillard Reaction]]. Spices like [[Smoked Paprika]] release their fat-soluble carotenoid pigments and flavor compounds into the oil, infusing the entire base with color and taste.

## How to Do It
1.  Begin by finely dicing [[Onion]] and [[Bell Pepper]]. Mince the [[Garlic]] and grate or finely dice the [[Tomato]].
2.  Heat a generous amount of [[Olive Oil]] in a heavy-bottomed [[Pan]] or [[Pot]] over low to medium-low heat.
3.  Add the onion and bell pepper to the pan. Cook slowly, stirring occasionally, for 15-20 minutes or until they are very soft and translucent.
4.  Add the minced garlic and cook for another 1-2 minutes until fragrant, being careful not to let it burn.
5.  Stir in the tomato, along with any spices like [[Smoked Paprika]] or herbs like a [[Bay Leaf]].
6.  Continue to cook, stirring periodically, until the mixture has reduced, darkened in color, and the oil begins to separate from the vegetable solids. This can take anywhere from 20 to 40 minutes. The final consistency should be jam-like.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Cooking too quickly:** Using high heat will sauté or brown the vegetables instead of "sweating" them. This creates a harsher, less complex flavor and prevents the deep melding of aromas.
*   **Burning the garlic:** [[Garlic]] has a low sugar content and burns quickly, turning bitter. It should always be added after the onions and peppers have softened significantly.
*   **Under-cooking the mixture:** A rushed sofrito will taste of raw vegetables. The goal is a complete breakdown of the ingredients into a cohesive, jammy base. Patience is key.
*   **Using poor-quality tomatoes:** The flavor of the [[Tomato]] is central to a good sofrito. Using underripe or flavorless tomatoes will result in an acidic or watery base.

## When to Use It
Sofrito is the essential starting point for a vast array of dishes in [[Spanish Cuisine]].
*   It is the non-negotiable first step in building the flavor of an authentic [[Paella]].
*   It serves as the base for countless Spanish [[Stew]]s (guisos), bean dishes, and braised meats.
*   It is used to create rich [[Tomato Sauce]]s for fish, meat, or pasta.
*   It is a key component in soups, from the cold [[Gazpacho]] to heartier lentil or chickpea soups.
