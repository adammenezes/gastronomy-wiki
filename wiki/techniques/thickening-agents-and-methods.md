## Overview
The goal of using [[Thickening Agents]] is to add [[Viscosity]], texture, and [[Mouthfeel]] to a sauce, helping it to linger on the taste buds and deliver a sustained flavor experience. Un-thickened sauces, while offering an immediate flavor punch, may not cling to the tongue unless naturally rich in [[Gelatin]] and [[Glutamates]], often found in [[Meats]], [[Poultry]], and [[Fish]]. Common methods for thickening include [[Reductions]], [[Emulsions]], and [[Suspensions]]. [[Starches]], such as [[Flour]] or [[Cornstarch]], are widely used to add body, though they can sometimes mask flavors, requiring additional [[Seasoning]].

## The Science
Thickening relies on various physical and chemical processes. [[Reductions]] concentrate liquids and flavors by [[Evaporation]], with naturally occurring [[Gelatin]] in [[Meat-based Sauces]] contributing to body. [[Emulsions]], or [[Colloids]], are formed when [[Wheat]] or refined [[Starches]] swell and [[Gelatinize]] in hot liquids, or when [[Liquids]] and [[Fats]] are combined and agitated, as seen in [[Mayonnaise]] or [[Hollandaise]].

In the case of a [[Roux]], [[Fat]] surrounds [[Starch Granules]] from [[Flour]], preventing clumping and allowing for even [[Gelatinization]] when added to hot liquids. The cooking time of a [[Roux]] affects its thickening power; prolonged heat causes the [[Flour]] grains to swell and then collapse, reducing their ability to absorb liquid, which is why darker rouxs require a higher [[Flour]]-to-[[Fat]] ratio. [[Refined Starches]] like [[Arrowroot]], [[Cornstarch]], [[Potato Starch]], and [[Rice Flour]] are effective because they swell quickly and have a neutral flavor, making them suitable for [[Gluten-intolerant Diets]]. However, their thickening power can be weakened by prolonged heat.

[[Warm Emulsions]] involve rapidly [[Whisking]] a liquid while slowly incorporating [[Fat]], causing [[Fat Molecules]] to disperse into tiny droplets that adhere to the liquid, creating [[Viscosity]]. These are often stabilized by ingredients like [[Egg Yolks]] (e.g., in [[Hollandaise]], [[Béarnaise]]), reduced [[Cream]], or [[Spices]]. A [[Liaison]] of [[Egg Yolks]] and [[Cream]] thickens through the [[Coagulation]] of [[Egg Proteins]], but must not be boiled to prevent [[Protein Denaturation]] and curdling. [[Butter]] and [[Cream]] also contribute to thickening by increasing [[Fat Globules]] and [[Viscosity]], especially when [[Cream]] is reduced.

[[Suspensions]] are created by blending various ingredients into fine particles to form a homogenous mixture, where the suspended solids contribute to the overall thickness, as in a [[Tomato Sauce]] or [[Bean Soup]].

## How to Do It
**Reductions:** Simply [[Simmer]] a liquid to reduce its volume through [[Evaporation]], concentrating flavors and naturally thickening it.

**Roux (Wheat Flour):**
1.  **Melt [[Fat]]** (e.g., [[Clarified Butter]], [[Vegetable Oils]], [[Animal Fats]]) in a [[Saucepan]].
2.  **Add [[Flour]]**: Typically equal parts by weight for lighter rouxs. For darker rouxs, adjust to a 3:2 ratio of [[Flour]] to [[Fat]] due to reduced thickening power.
3.  **Cook and Stir**:
    *   **White Roux**: Cook for about 5 minutes, stirring, without allowing it to color. Used for [[Cream Soup]], [[Béchamel Sauce]].
    *   **Blond Roux**: Cook for about 10 minutes until it achieves a gold color. Used for [[Velouté Sauce]], [[Soups]], [[White Stews]].
    *   **Brown Roux**: Cook for about 30 minutes, stirring, until a rich brown color develops. Can also be baked in a 350˚F (177˚C) [[Oven]] with a lid. Used for [[Espagnole Sauce]], [[Brown Stews]], [[Braises]].
    *   **Black Roux**: Cook for about 60 minutes, stirring, until very dark brown to black. Can also be baked. Used in [[Gumbo]], [[Cajun Cuisine]].
4.  **Combine with Liquid**: Add cold or warm [[Roux]] to hot liquid, or hot [[Roux]] to cold liquid, [[Whisking]] continuously to prevent lumps. Allow about 20 minutes of simmering for the [[Flour]] to fully swell and thicken.

**Other Wheat-Based Options:**
*   **Brown Flour (Dry Roux)**: Toast [[Flour]] in a [[Cast Iron Skillet]] or on a [[Sheet Pan]] in a 350°F (175˚C) [[Oven]], stirring frequently, until browned. It can be added directly to liquid or combined with [[Fat]].
*   **[[Beurre Manié]]**: Work equal parts softened [[Butter]] and [[Flour]] into a smooth paste. Add small pieces at a time to simmering [[Sauces]], [[Soups]], or [[Stews]] at the end of cooking, allowing a few minutes to cook out the raw [[Flour]] taste.
*   **[[Bread Crumbs]]**: Pulverized cooked [[Wheat]] products like [[Bread]], [[Crackers]], or [[Cookies]] can be added to thicken.

**Refined Vegetable Starches (Slurry):**
1.  **Prepare a [[Slurry]]**: Combine equal parts by volume of [[Starch]] ([[Arrowroot]], [[Cornstarch]], [[Potato Starch]], [[Rice Flour]]) with [[Cool Water]].
2.  **Heat Liquid**: Bring the liquid to be thickened to a [[Simmer]].
3.  **Combine**: While [[Whisking]] continuously, slowly pour the [[Slurry]] into the hot liquid.
4.  **Simmer and Adjust**: Return the mixture to a [[Boil]] (for about 1 minute) until it thickens. Adjust with more [[Slurry]] if needed.

**Warm Emulsions:**
*   **[[Hollandaise]]/[[Béarnaise]]**: Over a [[Double Boiler]] or very low heat, rapidly [[Whisk]] [[Egg Yolks]] while slowly drizzling in melted [[Butter]] until thick and emulsified.
*   **[[Beurre Blanc]]**: Gradually [[Whisk]] cold [[Butter]] into a reduction of [[Wine]] or [[Vinegar]] and [[Shallots]], often stabilized with a touch of [[Cream]].
*   **Holding**: These sauces are time and temperature-sensitive. Keep them in a warm bath or insulated container at 120°F/49˚C and discard after a maximum of 4 hours.

**Liaison of Egg Yolks and Cream:**
*   **Ratio**: Use 4 parts [[Cream]] to 1 part [[Egg Yolks]].
*   **Temper**: Gradually [[Whisk]] a small amount of the hot liquid into the [[Liaison]] mixture to temper the [[Egg Yolks]] before adding the tempered [[Liaison]] back into the main liquid.
*   **Do Not Boil**: After adding the [[Liaison]], never allow the liquid to [[Boil]], as the [[Egg Yolks]] will curdle.

**Dairy Products:**
*   **[[Monter au Beurre]]**: Swirl cold [[Butter]] into a sauce at the end of cooking to add richness and a slight thickening.
*   **[[Cream]]**: Reduce [[Cream]] by about one-third of its volume to concentrate [[Fat Globules]] and increase [[Viscosity]].

**Suspensions:**
*   **Pureeing**: Blend [[Grains]] (e.g., [[Raw Rice]] in [[Bisque]]), [[Legumes]], [[Root Vegetables]] (e.g., [[Diced Potatoes]] in [[Cream Soups]]), [[Nuts]], [[Seeds]], [[Fruits]], or [[Vegetables]] (e.g., [[Pureed Fruits]] and [[Vegetables]] for [[Tomato Sauces]] or [[Coulis]]) into a smooth consistency.

## Common Mistakes
*   **[[Roux]]**: Not cooking long enough can leave a raw [[Flour]] taste. Using the wrong [[Flour]]-to-[[Fat]] ratio for darker rouxs will result in insufficient thickening. Adding hot [[Roux]] to hot liquid can cause lumps; ensure one component is cooler. Not allowing sufficient simmering time (20 minutes) for [[Flour]] to fully [[Gelatinize]].
*   **Refined [[Starches]]**: Adding [[Starch]] directly to hot liquid without first creating a [[Slurry]] will result in lumps. Prolonged boiling after adding a [[Slurry]] can weaken its thickening power.
*   **Warm [[Emulsions]]**: Overheating or insufficient [[Whisking]] can cause sauces like [[Hollandaise]] to separate.
*   **[[Liaison]]**: Allowing a sauce to [[Boil]] after adding an [[Egg Yolk]] and [[Cream]] [[Liaison]] will cause the [[Egg Yolks]] to curdle, resulting in a grainy texture.

## When to Use It
*   **[[Reductions]]**: Ideal for creating intensely flavored [[Jus]] and [[Glace]] from [[Meat-based Sauces]] where a pure, unobstructed flavor is desired.
*   **[[Roux]]**: A foundational thickener in [[French Cuisine]]. Lighter rouxs are used in [[White Sauces]] like [[Béchamel]] and [[Velouté]], and in [[Cream Soups]]. Darker rouxs are essential for [[Brown Sauces]], [[Brown Stews]], [[Braises]], and [[Southern American Cuisines]] like [[Creole]] and [[Cajun Cooking]] (e.g., [[Gumbo]]).
*   **[[Beurre Manié]]**: Perfect for last-minute adjustments to the consistency of [[Sauces]], [[Soups]], and [[Stews]] when a quick, controlled thickening is needed.
*   **[[Bread Crumbs]]**: An economical and traditional method, dating back to [[Ancient Roman]] times, for thickening [[Soups]], [[Sauces]], and [[Stews]], especially useful for repurposing stale [[Bread]].
*   **Refined Vegetable [[Starches]]**: Preferred for lighter, neutral-flavored sauces, quick thickening, and as a [[Gluten-free]] alternative. They are typically added at the end of the cooking process.
*   **Warm [[Emulsions]]**: Used for rich, luxurious sauces like [[Hollandaise]], [[Béarnaise]], and [[Beurre Blanc]], which are often prepared just before service due to their delicate nature. [[Mayonnaise]] is a classic uncooked emulsion.
*   **[[Liaison]]**: Employed to enrich and delicately thicken [[Soups]], [[Sauces]], and [[Stews]] at the very end of cooking, adding a creamy texture without heavy starch.
*   **[[Monter au Beurre]]**: Used to finish sauces, adding gloss, richness, and a subtle thickening.
*   **[[Cream]]**: When reduced, [[Cream]] adds significant body and richness to sauces.
*   **[[Suspensions]]**: Versatile for [[Gluten-free]], ethnic, and [[Vegetarian Cooking]]. Examples include [[Raw Rice]] in [[Bisque]], [[Diced Potatoes]] in [[Cream Soups]], [[Pureed Fruits]] and [[Vegetables]] in [[Italian Cuisine]] ([[Tomato Sauces]]) and [[French Cuisine]] ([[Coulis]]), and [[Nuts]] and [[Seeds]] in [[African Cuisine]].
