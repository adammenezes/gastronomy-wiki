## Overview
[[Steaming]] is a gentle cooking technique that uses the heat of [[steam]] to cook food. Unlike [[boiling]] or [[poaching]], the food does not come into direct contact with [[water]], preserving more of its natural flavors, textures, and [[nutrients]]. It's a cornerstone of many healthy [[cuisines]], particularly prevalent in [[Asian Cuisine]] for dishes like [[Dim Sum]] and [[Steamed Fish]], but also used widely for [[vegetables]] and [[poultry]].

## The Science
At its core, [[steaming]] relies on the [[phase transition]] of [[water]] into [[steam]]. When [[water]] is heated to its [[boiling point]] (100°C or 212°F at sea level), it transforms into [[steam]], which carries a significant amount of [[latent heat of vaporization]]. This hot vapor then surrounds the food, transferring its energy efficiently and gently. The moist environment prevents food from drying out, and because the [[water-soluble vitamins]] and [[minerals]] don't leach into cooking [[water]], [[nutrient retention]] is generally higher than with [[boiling]]. The heat causes [[protein denaturation]] in [[meat]] and [[fish]], and softens [[cellulose]] in [[vegetables]], making them tender and digestible.

## How to Do It
To steam food, you'll need a [[pot]] with a tight-fitting [[lid]] and a [[steamer basket]] or [[rack]] that fits inside, keeping the food above the [[water]] level.
1.  Fill the [[pot]] with about 1-2 inches of [[water]], ensuring it won't touch the bottom of the [[steamer basket]].
2.  Bring the [[water]] to a rolling [[boil]] over high heat.
3.  Place the food, such as [[fish fillets]] or [[broccoli florets]], in a single layer in the [[steamer basket]]. Avoid overcrowding to allow for even [[steam]] circulation.
4.  Carefully place the [[steamer basket]] into the [[pot]] and immediately cover it with the [[lid]].
5.  Reduce the heat to medium-high to maintain a steady, vigorous [[steam]].
6.  Cook until the food is tender and cooked through. Cooking times vary greatly depending on the food's type and size. For example, thin [[fish fillets]] might take 5-8 minutes, while denser [[vegetables]] like [[carrots]] could take 10-15 minutes.
7.  Carefully remove the [[lid]] (away from your face to avoid [[steam burns]]) and then remove the [[steamer basket]]. Serve immediately. Specialized tools like a [[bamboo steamer]] or an [[electric steamer]] can also be used.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Overcrowding the [[steamer basket]]:** Leads to uneven cooking as [[steam]] cannot circulate properly. Cook in batches if necessary.
*   **Not enough [[water]]:** The [[pot]] can boil dry, potentially damaging the [[pot]] or [[steamer]] and stopping the cooking process. Always ensure sufficient [[water]] for the cooking time.
*   **Lifting the [[lid]] too often:** Each time the [[lid]] is lifted, a significant amount of [[steam]] escapes, dropping the temperature and extending cooking time.
*   **Overcooking:** [[Steamed vegetables]] can become mushy, and [[fish]] can become dry and flaky if cooked too long. Monitor closely for desired doneness.

## When to Use It
[[Steaming]] is ideal for delicate foods that benefit from gentle, moist heat. It's perfect for [[fish]] and [[seafood]] like [[Steamed Salmon]] or [[shrimp]], as it keeps them tender and moist. Most [[vegetables]], from [[asparagus]] to [[potatoes]], are excellent when steamed, retaining their vibrant color and crisp-tender texture. It's also the preferred method for many [[dumplings]], [[buns]], and certain [[custards]] in [[Asian Cuisine]], ensuring a light and airy result.

*   [[Boiling]]: A more aggressive moist-heat method where food is fully submerged in rapidly bubbling water, often used for pasta or blanching.
*   [[Braising]]: A combination cooking method that starts with searing and finishes with slow cooking in liquid, ideal for tougher cuts of meat.
*   [[Sous Vide]]: A precise cooking method that uses a temperature-controlled water bath to cook food gently and evenly in a vacuum-sealed bag.
*   [[Blanching]]: A quick boiling or steaming process followed by an ice bath, often used to pre-cook vegetables or loosen skins.
