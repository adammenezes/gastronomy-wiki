## Overview
Sweating is a gentle cooking technique used to soften vegetables and release their aromatic compounds without browning them. Typically applied to aromatics like [[Onion]], [[Carrot]], and [[Celery]] (a combination known as [[Mirepoix]]), it involves cooking them in a small amount of fat over low heat in a covered pan. The goal is to create a deep, mellow flavor foundation for dishes such as soups, stews, sauces, and [[Risotto]]. Unlike [[Sautéing]], which uses high heat to create color and a more robust flavor, sweating builds a subtle, sweet, and complex base.

## The Science
The process of sweating relies on controlling heat to manage chemical reactions. The low temperature gently breaks down the [[Pectin]] in the vegetables' cell walls, causing them to soften. As the cells break down, they release water via [[Osmosis]], which is encouraged by the early addition of [[Salt]]. By covering the pan, this moisture is trapped as steam, which keeps the surface temperature of the vegetables below the threshold for the [[Maillard Reaction]] and [[Caramelization]] (approximately 300°F / 150°C). The released aromatic compounds, many of which are fat-soluble, then infuse the cooking [[Fat]], creating a flavorful base that permeates the entire dish.

## How to Do It
1.  **Preparation:** Using proper [[Knife Skills]], chop your aromatic vegetables into a small, uniform dice. Consistency is key for even cooking. Common bases include [[Mirepoix]], the Spanish [[Sofrito]], or Italian [[Battuto]].
2.  **Heating the Fat:** In a heavy-bottomed pan, such as a [[Dutch Oven]] or [[Sauté Pan]], melt a small amount of [[Butter]] or heat [[Oil]] over low to medium-low heat.
3.  **Cooking:** Add the vegetables to the pan along with a pinch of [[Salt]]. Stir to coat them evenly with the fat.
4.  **Cover and Sweat:** Cover the pan with a tight-fitting lid. Let the vegetables cook gently, stirring occasionally, until they are very soft, translucent, and have released their liquid. This can take anywhere from 10 to 20 minutes depending on the vegetable and dice size. Do not let them brown.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Heat is too high:** This is the most common error, leading to [[Browning]] instead of sweating. This changes the flavor profile entirely, moving into the territory of [[Sautéing]]. Fix this by keeping the heat low and monitoring the vegetables closely.
*   **Uneven vegetable cuts:** Inconsistent knife work means smaller pieces will overcook or burn while larger pieces remain raw. Practice consistent [[Knife Skills]] for an even result.
*   **Overcrowding the pan:** Too many vegetables in the pan will lower the temperature and release excess moisture, resulting in [[Steaming]] rather than sweating. Use a larger pan or cook in batches if necessary.
*   **Cooking uncovered:** The lid is essential for trapping steam, which regulates temperature and prevents moisture from evaporating too quickly, thus preventing browning.

## When to Use It
Sweating is the foundational first step in countless recipes where a deep, mellow, and sweet flavor base is desired without the roasted notes of browned vegetables. It is fundamental in [[French Cuisine]] and Italian cooking.
*   **Dishes:** Use it to start soups ([[Minestrone]]), stews, braises ([[Coq au Vin]]), and sauces like a classic [[Tomato Sauce]]. It is the essential first step for making a creamy [[Risotto]].
*   **Ingredients:** It works best for aromatic vegetables like [[Onion]], [[Garlic]], [[Shallot]], [[Leek]], [[Celery]], [[Carrot]], and [[Bell Pepper]].
