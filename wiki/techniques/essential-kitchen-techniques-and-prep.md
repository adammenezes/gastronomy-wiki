## Overview
Classic Knife Cuts are a set of standardized, foundational techniques for processing ingredients, primarily vegetables, into uniform shapes and sizes. Mastery of these cuts is a cornerstone of professional cooking and the principle of [[Mise en Place]], ensuring ingredients cook evenly, are texturally consistent, and present beautifully. The most common cuts include the [[Dicing|Dice]], [[Julienne]], [[Mincing|Mince]], and [[Chiffonade]]. These techniques are fundamental across global cuisines, from the [[Mirepoix]] in [[French Cuisine]] to the finely chopped aromatics in a [[Stir-fry]].

## The Science
The primary scientific principle behind uniform knife cuts is consistent [[Heat Transfer]]. When pieces of an ingredient like a [[Carrot]] or [[Potato]] are the same size, they cook at the same rate, preventing a mix of overcooked and undercooked pieces in the final dish. Smaller cuts, such as a [[Mincing|Mince]], dramatically increase the surface area of an ingredient. This accelerates cooking and maximizes flavour extraction by rupturing more cell walls, which is especially important for aromatics like [[Garlic]] and [[Ginger]]. For delicate herbs, a sharp [[Chiffonade]] cut minimizes bruising and cellular damage, preserving fresh flavour and preventing premature [[Enzymatic Browning]].

## How to Do It
All cuts should be performed with a sharp [[Chef's Knife]] on a stable [[Cutting Board]]. Use a "claw grip" with your non-dominant hand to protect your fingertips.

1.  **Julienne (Matchstick Cut):**
    *   First, square off the vegetable (e.g., a [[Carrot]] or [[Potato]]) by trimming the sides to create a stable, rectangular block.
    *   Slice the block lengthwise into thin planks of the desired thickness (typically 1-3mm).
    *   Stack the planks and slice them lengthwise again into fine matchsticks.

2.  **Dice (Cube Cut):**
    *   Begin by creating a [[Julienne]] of the ingredient.
    *   Gather the matchsticks into a neat pile, aligning the ends.
    *   Cut crosswise through the matchsticks to create uniform cubes.
    *   Common sizes include *Brunoise* (fine dice, ~1.5mm), *Small Dice* (~6mm), and *Medium Dice* (~12mm), often used for a [[Mirepoix]].

3.  **Mince (Finely Chopped):**
    *   For aromatics like [[Garlic]] or shallots, first create a small dice.
    *   Then, using a rocking motion with your [[Chef's Knife]], repeatedly chop through the dice until the pieces are extremely fine and uniform.

4.  **Chiffonade (Ribbon Cut):**
    *   This cut is used for leafy greens and herbs like [[Basil]] or [[Spinach]].
    *   Stack several leaves on top of one another.
    *   Roll the stack tightly into a cigar-like cylinder.
    *   Slice thinly across the cylinder to produce fine, elegant ribbons.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Using a Dull Knife:** A dull blade crushes cell walls instead of slicing cleanly, leading to bruised herbs, weeping onions, and a greater risk of the knife slipping. Regular [[Honing]] is essential.
*   **Inconsistent Sizes:** The most common error, which results in unevenly cooked food. Focus on uniformity over speed.
*   **Unstable Cutting Surface:** Failing to create a flat base on round vegetables like potatoes or onions before slicing can cause them to roll, leading to inaccurate cuts and potential injury.
*   **Improper Grip:** Not using the "claw grip" on the guide hand exposes fingertips to the blade.

## When to Use It
*   **[[Dicing|Dice]]:** The workhorse cut for building flavour bases like [[Mirepoix]] and [[Sofrito]]. Essential for soups, stews, sauces, and preparations like [[Tomato Concassee]].
*   **[[Julienne]]:** Best for dishes that cook quickly, such as a [[Stir-fry]], or when a delicate texture is desired in salads and slaws.
*   **[[Mincing|Mince]]:** Used for potent aromatics ([[Garlic]], [[Ginger]], chiles) to ensure their flavour is evenly distributed throughout a sauce, dressing, or marinade.
*   **[[Chiffonade]]:** Primarily used for garnishing with fresh herbs like [[Basil]] or [[Mint]], or for wilting leafy greens like [[Spinach]] into a [[Pasta Carbonara]] or soup.
