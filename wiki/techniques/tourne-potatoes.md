## Overview
The Tourné (pronounced toor-NAY) is a classic knife cut from [[French Cuisine]] that shapes vegetables, most commonly a [[Potato]], into a seven-sided barrel or football shape. While primarily used for elegant presentation, the uniform shape also promotes even cooking. This cut is considered a benchmark of a chef's [[Knife Skills]] and is famously used to make dishes like [[Pommes Château]]. It can be applied to a variety of firm vegetables, including [[Carrot|Carrots]], [[Turnip|Turnips]], and [[Zucchini]]. The technique was codified and popularised by chefs like [[Auguste Escoffier]] as a hallmark of haute cuisine.

## The Science
The primary scientific advantage of the tourné cut lies in the physics of [[Heat Transfer]]. The smooth, curved surfaces and lack of sharp edges or thin corners ensure that heat penetrates the vegetable uniformly. This prevents the points from overcooking, burning, or turning to mush before the dense center is cooked through. This is especially important in slow-cooking methods like braising or roasting. The consistent surface area also allows for an even [[Maillard Reaction]] when browning or a uniform coating when glazing, resulting in a superior texture and appearance.

## How to Do It
Executing a proper tourné requires practice and a specific tool, ideally a [[Tourné Knife]] (also known as a bird's beak knife), though a sharp [[Paring Knife]] can suffice.

1.  **Prepare the Vegetable**: Peel the vegetable and cut it into uniform rectangular blocks, approximately 2 inches (5 cm) long.
2.  **Grip**: Hold the vegetable block firmly in your non-dominant hand. Hold the knife in your dominant hand with a secure but relaxed grip.
3.  **First Cut**: Place the blade at the top corner of the block. In a single, sweeping motion, turn the vegetable towards the blade while cutting downwards, creating a smooth, convex facet from one end to the other. The motion is more about turning the vegetable into the knife than moving the knife around the vegetable.
4.  **Rotate and Repeat**: Rotate the vegetable slightly and make an identical cut, creating the second of seven facets. The edge of this new cut should meet the edge of the previous one cleanly.
5.  **Complete the Shape**: Continue this process of rotating and cutting until you have created seven equal sides.
6.  **Finish**: Trim the two ends so they are flat and blunt. The final product should be a distinct, seven-sided football shape. Save the trimmings for making [[Stock]] or [[Purée|Purées]].

## Common Mistakes
*   **Uneven or Flat Sides**: This occurs from an inconsistent cutting angle or not turning the vegetable smoothly. The resulting shape will be hexagonal or octagonal instead of having seven sides. *Fix:* Practice slow, deliberate movements. Focus on a fluid wrist motion in your non-dominant hand.
*   **Concave Facets**: Cutting too deeply into the vegetable creates a scooped-out appearance rather than a rounded barrel. *Fix:* Maintain a shallow angle with the blade and use a peeling motion.
*   **Excessive Waste**: While some waste is inevitable, inexperienced cooks often trim away too much. *Fix:* Start with slightly larger blocks of vegetable and aim to remove only thin layers with each cut. Always repurpose the trimmings.
*   **Using a Dull Knife**: A dull blade is dangerous as it can slip, and it will tear the vegetable instead of slicing cleanly. *Fix:* Ensure your [[Tourné Knife]] or [[Paring Knife]] is properly sharpened before you begin.

## When to Use It
The tourné cut is used when both presentation and cooking precision are critical.

*   **Classic French Dishes**: It is essential for traditional preparations like [[Pommes Château]] (potatoes roasted in [[Clarified Butter]]) and [[Glazed Carrots]].
*   **Elegant Garnishes**: Tournéed vegetables serve as a sophisticated garnish for roasted meats and fish.
*   **Stews and Braises**: In dishes like [[Beef Bourguignon]], tournéed vegetables hold their shape well during long cooking times.
*   **Suitable Vegetables**: Best suited for firm, starchy, or fibrous vegetables like [[Potato|Potatoes]], [[Carrot|Carrots]], [[Turnip|Turnips]], parsnips, [[Zucchini]], and [[Cucumber]].
