## Overview
Lamination is the process of creating a dough with hundreds of thin, alternating layers of dough and fat. This is achieved by repeatedly rolling and folding a block of fat, known as the *beurrage*, into a base dough, or *detrempe*. During baking, the fat melts and its water content turns to steam, pushing the dough layers apart to create an exceptionally light, flaky, and buttery texture.

This technique is fundamental to classic [[French Cuisine]] and is used to make iconic pastries. Yeast-leavened examples include the [[Croissant]] and [[Danish Pastry]]. The primary unleavened example is [[Puff Pastry]], also known as [[Pâte Feuilletée]], which forms the basis for dishes like the [[Mille Feuille]]. Famous variations include [[Pain au Chocolat]] and [[Amadine]] (almond), as well as savory versions filled with [[Cheese]], [[Spinach]], or [[Ham]].

## The Science
The magic of laminated dough lies in a physical leavening process. As the pastry bakes, the solid fat layers, typically [[Butter]], melt. The water trapped within the butter quickly turns into steam. This steam expands rapidly, forcing the adjacent, pliable dough layers to separate and rise. The fat also serves to waterproof the dough layers, preventing them from fusing together and ensuring a distinct, flaky structure.

The initial dough, or *detrempe*, is mixed minimally to control [[Gluten]] development. Over-developed gluten would make the dough too elastic and resistant to rolling, causing it to shrink back and potentially tear, which would destroy the delicate layers. Temperature control is critical; the fat must remain solid and pliable, not so cold that it shatters or so warm that it melts into the dough.

## How to Do It
The process of creating laminated dough involves several distinct stages, known as *tourage* (the turns or folds).

**1. Prepare the Dough (Detrempe)**
The base dough composition varies. [[Puff Pastry]] uses a simple dough of [[Flour]], [[Water]], and [[Salt]]. [[Croissant]] dough is enriched with [[Sugar]], [[Yeast]], and [[Milk]], while [[Danish Pastry]] dough is further enriched with [[Eggs]]. A blend of [[Bread Flour]] and [[Cake Flour]] can be used, or simply [[All-Purpose Flour]]. Mix ingredients until a sticky mass forms, but avoid over-mixing to limit [[Gluten]] development.

**2. Ferment the Dough**
For yeast-leavened doughs, shape the detrempe into a rectangle on a [[Sheet Pan]] and chill for 12-24 hours. This cold [[Fermentation]] develops flavour and relaxes the gluten.

**3. Lock in the Fat (Beurrage)**
Roll the chilled detrempe into a large rectangle. The fat—ideally high-quality [[Butter]], though [[Lard]], [[Margarine]], or [[Shortening]] are sometimes used—should be pliable but cold, around 55°F/13°C. Pound and roll the fat between sheets of [[Parchment Paper]] into a rectangular slab. Place this beurrage onto the detrempe and fold the dough over to completely enclose it. Common folds for this step include the [[Envelope Fold]], [[Single Fold]], [[Letter Fold]], or [[Book Fold]].

**4. Turn and Roll the Dough**
This is the core of lamination. The dough package is rolled out into a long rectangle, folded, and then rested in the refrigerator.
- Turn the dough 90 degrees from its previous orientation.
- Roll it out evenly.
- Perform a fold (typically a [[Letter Fold]] or [[Book Fold]]).
- Wrap the dough and chill it on a parchment-lined [[Baking Sheet]] for 30-45 minutes to allow the gluten to relax and the butter to firm up.
- Repeat this process, called a "turn," several times (typically 3-6 turns) to create the desired number of layers.

**5. Final Shaping and Baking**
After the final turn and rest, roll the dough to its final thickness.
- Cut and shape as desired. For croissants, cut the dough into long triangles and roll from the wide end. For other pastries, cut into rectangles and fill with [[Chocolate]], [[Almond Paste]], or savory ingredients.
- For yeast-leavened doughs, this is followed by [[Proofing]] to allow the dough to rise.
- Brush with an [[Eggwash]] for a golden, shiny crust.
- The final step is [[Baking]] in a hot oven, which activates the steam and creates the puff.

## Common Mistakes
- **Fat is too warm:** The [[Butter]] will melt into the dough rather than forming distinct layers, resulting in a greasy, dense pastry. Always chill the dough thoroughly between turns.
- **Fat is too cold:** The butter will be brittle and can shatter, tearing through the dough layers instead of spreading evenly.
- **Over-working the dough:** Developing too much [[Gluten]] makes the dough tough and difficult to roll out, causing it to shrink and tear.
- **Insufficient resting:** Skipping the chilling periods between folds prevents the gluten from relaxing, making the dough fight back during rolling and leading to torn layers.
- **Using too much flour for dusting:** Excess flour can be incorporated into the dough, making the final product tough and dry. Always brush off excess flour before folding.

## When to Use It
Lamination is used to create pastries where a light, airy, and flaky texture is desired. It is the foundation for a wide range of sweet and savory baked goods.
- **Breakfast Pastries:** The quintessential application, including the [[Croissant]] and [[Danish Pastry]].
- **Classic Desserts:** Essential for [[Mille Feuille]] ("thousand-layer pastry"), turnovers, and palmiers. Filled pastries like [[Pain au Chocolat]] also rely on this technique.
- **Savory Dishes:** Used for vol-au-vents, sausage rolls, beef wellington, or as a flaky crust for pot pies. It can be filled with ingredients like [[Cheese]], [[Spinach]], or [[Ham]].
