## Overview
Clarified Butter is pure butterfat, produced by removing the water and milk solids from whole [[Butter]]. This process significantly raises the butter's smoke point, making it an exceptional fat for high-heat cooking methods like [[Sautéing]] and [[Searing]]. Because the milk solids, which burn easily, have been removed, it imparts a clean, rich butter flavour without the risk of browning or scorching. It is a foundational ingredient in many classical sauces, most notably [[Hollandaise Sauce]] and [[Béarnaise Sauce]], and is the precursor to [[Ghee]].

## The Science
Standard [[Butter]] is an emulsion composed of approximately 80% butterfat, 15-18% water, and 1-2% milk solids. These milk solids consist of proteins, primarily [[Casein]] and [[Whey]], and milk sugars. When heated, it is these solids that begin to burn at a relatively low temperature (around 350°F / 177°C), creating a bitter taste and dark colour.

The process of clarifying butter is a physical separation based on density. Gentle heating breaks the emulsion, causing the components to stratify:
1.  **Water:** Evaporates as steam.
2.  **Whey Proteins:** Coagulate and rise to the surface as a white foam.
3.  **Butterfat:** The pure, clear liquid fat forms the middle layer.
4.  **Casein Proteins:** Heavier than the fat, these sink to the bottom of the pan.

By removing the water and milk solids, the resulting pure butterfat has a much higher [[Smoke Point]] of around 450°F (232°C), preventing the undesirable browning and burning associated with whole butter in high-heat applications. This process is distinct from chemical changes like the [[Maillard Reaction]], which occurs when milk solids are intentionally browned to make ghee.

## How to Do It
1.  Place unsalted [[Butter]] in a heavy-bottomed [[Saucepan]] or a [[Double Boiler]]. Using a double boiler provides the most gentle, even heat and prevents scorching.
2.  Melt the butter slowly over low heat without stirring. As it melts, it will separate into three distinct layers.
3.  A layer of white foam (whey proteins) will form on the surface. Use a [[Ladle]] or a fine-mesh [[Skimmer]] to gently remove and discard this foam.
4.  Once all the foam has been removed, you will see the clear, golden butterfat in the middle and the milky solids settled at the bottom.
5.  Carefully pour or ladle the clear butterfat into a clean, dry storage container, being sure to stop before the milk solids at the bottom are disturbed.
6.  For maximum purity, you can line a [[Strainer]] with [[Cheesecloth]] and pour the butterfat through it to catch any remaining small particles.
7.  The clarified butter can be stored at room temperature for several weeks or refrigerated for several months.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Heating too quickly:** This is the most common error. High heat will cause the milk solids to burn before they have a chance to fully separate, resulting in a bitter, scorched flavour. This is [[Browning]] the solids, which is desired for ghee but not for pure clarified butter.
*   **Stirring the butter:** Stirring will re-emulsify the separating components, defeating the purpose of the clarification process. Let it separate naturally.
*   **Incomplete skimming:** Leaving foam on the surface means leaving behind milk solids, which will lower the final product's [[Smoke Point]].
*   **Pouring carelessly:** Allowing the milk solids from the bottom of the pan to pour into the final container contaminates the pure butterfat.
*   **Using salted butter:** While possible, the salt becomes concentrated in the milk solids. Using unsalted [[Butter]] provides better control over the final seasoning of your dish.

## When to Use It
Clarified butter is a versatile fat used across many culinary applications, especially in [[French Cuisine]].
*   **High-Heat Cooking:** Its high smoke point makes it ideal for [[Sautéing]] vegetables and [[Searing]] proteins like [[Steak]] or [[Scallops]] to achieve a perfect crust without burning.
*   **Emulsified Sauces:** It is the essential fat for creating stable, classic warm emulsions like [[Hollandaise Sauce]] and [[Béarnaise Sauce]]. The absence of water and milk solids prevents the [[Emulsion]] from breaking easily.
*   **Dipping Sauce:** It is famously served as a simple dipping sauce for steamed seafood such as [[Lobster]] and [[Crab]].
*   **Roasting:** It can be used to baste poultry or roast vegetables, providing a rich flavour without the risk of burning that whole butter presents.
