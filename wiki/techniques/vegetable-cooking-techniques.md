## Overview
[[Vegetables]] are a cornerstone of modern [[Cuisine]], driven by demand for healthier options and the rise of [[Vegetarian]] and [[Mediterranean Diets]]. Due to their delicate nature, proper [[Vegetable Cookery]] is crucial to maintain integrity, develop optimal [[Color]], [[Texture]], and [[Flavor]], and preserve [[Nutrients]]. This technique encompasses a wide array of methods, from initial [[Mise en Place]] steps like [[Blanching]] and [[Par-cooking]] in [[Boiling]] salted [[Water]], often followed by [[Shocking]] in an [[Ice Water Bath]] to set color, to finishing methods like [[Sautéing]], [[Frying]], [[Roasting]], [[Grilling]], [[Braising]], and [[Steaming]]. The chosen method depends heavily on the specific [[Vegetable]] and the desired culinary outcome, whether it's tenderizing [[Root Vegetables]] or quickly setting the color of [[Tender Leafy Greens]].

## The Science
Cooking [[Vegetables]] initiates several physical and chemical transformations. Heat activates [[Flavor Molecules]], enhancing their perception, and causes significant [[Textural Changes]]. For instance, [[Starch Gelatinisation]] occurs in starchy [[Vegetables]] like [[Potatoes]], making them tender and digestible. [[Blanching]] and [[Shocking]] are vital for preserving vibrant colors, especially in green [[Vegetables]], by deactivating [[Enzymes]] that cause softening and discoloration. Overcooking can lead to the development of bitter or unpleasant flavors, particularly in the [[Cabbage Family Vegetables]], while [[Onion Family Vegetables]] mellow and sweeten with heat. Dry heat methods like [[Roasting]] and [[Grilling]] promote [[Caramelization]] of natural sugars, concentrating flavors and creating a sweet, nutty taste. The stability of [[Pigment Colors]] also influences method choice; for example, [[Root Vegetables]] with stable pigments can withstand longer, gentler cooking like [[Poaching]].

## How to Do It

### Moist Heat Cooking Methods

#### Blanching / Par-cooking (A l’anglaise)
This method, often called "Big Pot Blanching" by chefs like [[Thomas Keller]] of [[The French Laundry]] and [[Per Se]], involves briefly cooking [[Vegetables]] in a large quantity of rapidly [[Boiling]] highly salted [[Water]].
1.  Submerge [[Vegetables]] in a large pot of rapidly [[Boiling]] salted [[Water]].
2.  Cook for 30 seconds to two minutes for [[Blanching]] (e.g., [[Tomato Concassé]], [[Peaches]], [[Spinach]], [[Basil]]) or longer for [[Par-cooking]] until partially tender.
3.  Drain immediately and plunge into an [[Ice Water Bath]] to stop the cooking process and set color.

#### Simmering
A gentler moist heat method, ideal for [[Vegetables]] that might disintegrate under a rolling boil.
1.  Place [[Potatoes]] or other suitable [[Vegetables]] in cold [[Water]].
2.  Slowly bring the [[Water]] to a [[Simmer]] (just below [[Boiling]] point).
3.  Cook until tender, ensuring even cooking and preventing breakdown.

#### Poaching
Cooking at a low, controlled temperature (180-190˚F/82-88˚C) in aromatic liquids.
1.  Prepare [[Aromatic Liquids]] such as [[Court Bouillon]], [[Stock]], [[Broth]], [[Coconut Milk]], [[Juice]], [[Butter]], or [[Olive Oil]].
2.  Submerge hearty [[Root Vegetables]] like [[Carrots]], [[Parsnips]], [[Rutabagas]], [[Turnips]], [[Fennel]], [[Onions]], [[Artichokes]], or [[Leeks]].
3.  Cook slowly, from 10 minutes up to an hour, depending on the [[Vegetable]] and size, until tender. This method is similar to [[Confit]] when using [[Butter]] or [[Olive Oil]].

#### Sous Vide
A precise method using a [[Vacuum Package]] and an [[Immersion Circulator]] or [[Hot Water Bath]].
1.  Clean and cut [[Vegetables]] into uniform shapes.
2.  Place in a [[Vacuum Pouch]]; add [[Seasoning]] and [[Olive Oil]] or [[Butter]].
3.  [[Vacuum Seal]] the pouch.
4.  Set the [[Thermal Circulator]] or [[Hot Water Bath]] to 190 °F / 88 °C.
5.  Immerse the [[Vegetables]] for the desired time.
6.  Serve immediately or [[Shock]] in an [[Ice Water Bath]] to hold. Reheat in the [[Thermal Circulator]].

#### Steaming
Cooking with moist heat from [[Boiling]] [[Water]] vapor.
1.  Clean and cut [[Vegetables]] into uniform shapes.
2.  Prepare a [[Steamer]] in a pot with [[Water]] and a [[Perforated Pan]].
3.  Bring the [[Water]] to a [[Boil]].
4.  Add the [[Vegetable]] to the [[Perforated Pan]] and cover with a [[Lid]].
5.  Cook to the desired degree of doneness.
6.  [[Season]] and serve immediately, or [[Shock]] in an [[Ice Water Bath]] for later use.

#### Glazing (Pan-Steaming)
Cooking in a shallow pan with a small amount of liquid, [[Butter]], and [[Sugar]] to create a glossy coating.
1.  In a [[Shallow Pan]], combine [[Water]], [[Butter]] (or [[Olive Oil]]), [[Sugar]] (or [[Honey]], [[Maple Syrup]]), and [[Salt]].
2.  Add [[Vegetables]] (raw or [[Par-cooked]]) and desired [[Herbs]] and [[Spices]].
3.  Cook, allowing the liquid to reduce and coat the [[Vegetables]] in a shiny glaze. [[Wine]], [[Stock]], or [[Juice]] can be incorporated.

#### Braising / Stewing
Combination cooking methods starting with [[Searing]] or [[Sweating]] in fat, then finishing in liquid.
1.  Clean and prep all ingredients.
2.  Begin by [[Sweating]] [[Aromatics]] (e.g., [[Onions]], [[Shallots]], [[Garlic]]) in a [[Braising Pan]] with fat (e.g., [[Bacon]], [[Ham]], [[Salt Pork]]).
3.  Add other [[Vegetables]] from least tender to most tender.
4.  [[Season]] with [[Salt]], [[Pepper]], and [[Spices]].
5.  Add liquids like [[Stock]], [[Wine]], [[Juice]], [[Coconut Milk]], or [[Tomato Sauce]].
6.  Cover and [[Stew]] or [[Braise]] on the stovetop or in an [[Oven]].
7.  Finish by [[Reducing]] or [[Thickening]] the liquid if needed. Examples include [[Ratatouille]] or [[Moroccan Vegetable Tagine]].

### Dry Heat Cooking Methods

#### Sautéing
Quickly cooking [[Vegetables]] in a hot [[Sauté Pan]] with a small amount of fat.
1.  Clean and cut [[Vegetables]] into uniform shapes. [[Blanch]] or [[Par-cook]] as needed.
2.  Heat a [[Sauté Pan]] to medium-high temperature.
3.  Add [[Olive Oil]] or [[Butter]].
4.  Add [[Aromatics]] (e.g., [[Shallots]], [[Garlic]]) if desired.
5.  Add [[Vegetables]] (e.g., [[Spinach]], [[Green Beans]], [[Carrots]]), avoiding overloading the pan.
6.  Cook, tossing occasionally to heat and cook evenly. [[Stir-Frying]] is a similar process often done in a [[Wok]].
7.  [[Season]] with [[Salt]], [[Pepper]], [[Herbs]], and [[Spices]]. Serve immediately.

#### Frying (Pan-Frying & Deep-Frying)
Cooking in hot fat to produce a crisp texture and caramelized taste.
*   **Pan-Frying:**
    1.  Prep ingredients; optionally [[Bread]] with [[Flour]], [[Egg Wash]], and [[Breadcrumbs]].
    2.  Heat a [[Sauté Pan]] or [[Cast Iron Skillet]] with [[Oil]] to a depth of ⅛-¼”/3-6mm, heated to 325-350˚F/160-180˚C.
    3.  Add items, avoiding crowding, and cook to a golden brown.
    4.  Turn as needed, drain on [[Paper Towels]], and [[Season]].
*   **Deep-Frying:**
    1.  Submerge [[Vegetables]] (e.g., [[Onion Rings]], [[Eggplant Fries]], [[Croquettes]], [[Chips]], [[Fritters]], [[Tempura]]) in hot [[Oil]] at 325-375˚F/160-190°C.
    2.  Items can be [[Breaded]] (using the [[Basket Method]]) or [[Batter-Dipped]] with [[Buttermilk]], [[Beer]], or light [[Tempura Batter]] (using the [[Swimming Method]] with [[Tongs]], [[Spider]], or [[Skimmer]]).
    3.  Cook until crisp and golden, with a moist interior.

#### Grilling / Broiling
Cooking with direct radiant heat, creating char and smoky flavors.
1.  Pre-heat [[Grill]] or [[Broiler]] to 375-450 °F/190-230˚C.
2.  Clean and cut [[Vegetables]] (e.g., [[Asparagus]], [[Corn]], [[Eggplant]], [[Mushrooms]], [[Peppers]], [[Onions]], [[Tomatoes]], [[Summer Squash]], [[Winter Squash]], [[Radicchio]], [[Endive]], [[Romaine]]) into uniform shapes.
3.  [[Season]] with [[Salt]] and [[Pepper]], brush or toss with [[Olive Oil]] or [[Marinade]].
4.  Place on [[Grill]] grates or a [[Broiler Pan]] (under the heating element).
5.  Turn [[Vegetables]] to brown and score uniformly. Brush with additional [[Marinade]] or [[Olive Oil]] to prevent drying.
6.  Remove when skin blisters and the middle softens. Small [[Vegetables]] can be placed on [[Skewers]] or in a [[Grilling Basket]].

#### Roasting / Baking
Cooking in an [[Oven]] with dry heat, concentrating flavors and promoting [[Caramelization]].
1.  Choose suitable [[Vegetables]] (e.g., [[Artichokes]], [[Beans]], [[Cauliflower]], [[Winter Squash]], [[Asparagus]], [[Zucchini]]).
2.  Cut [[Vegetables]]; smaller cuts require a hotter [[Oven]]. Halved [[Winter Squash]] at 350˚F/175˚C; smaller cuts like [[Asparagus]] at 425˚F/220˚C.
3.  Toss with [[Salt]], [[Pepper]], and [[Olive Oil]]. Add additional [[Spices]] as desired.
4.  Spread in a single layer on a baking sheet.
5.  Roast until tender and caramelized.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Overcooking:** Leads to mushy textures, loss of vibrant color, and the development of bitter or unpleasant flavors, especially in [[Cabbage Family Vegetables]]. Always monitor closely.
*   **Overloading the Pan:** When [[Sautéing]] or [[Frying]], too many [[Vegetables]] in the pan drops the temperature, causing them to steam rather than brown, resulting in a soggy product. Cook in batches.
*   **Incorrect Oil Temperature for Frying:** Too low, and [[Vegetables]] absorb excess [[Oil]], becoming greasy and soggy. Too high, and the exterior burns while the interior remains raw. Use a [[Thermometer]] to maintain the ideal temperature (325-375˚F/160-190°C).
*   **Boiling Starchy Vegetables:** [[Potatoes]] should be [[Simmered]], not [[Boiled]], as rapid boiling can cause them to break down and disintegrate unevenly.
*   **Not Shocking After Blanching:** Failing to plunge blanched [[Vegetables]] into an [[Ice Water Bath]] allows residual heat to continue cooking them, leading to overcooked, dull-colored results.

## When to Use It
*   **[[Blanching]]/[[Par-cooking]]**: Essential for [[Tomato Concassé]], loosening skins of [[Peaches]], setting the vibrant green color of [[Spinach]] or [[Basil]], and as a preparatory step for many [[Vegetables]] before [[Sautéing]] or [[Frying]].
*   **[[Simmering]]**: Best for [[Potatoes]] and other starchy [[Root Vegetables]] that require gentle, even cooking to prevent disintegration.
*   **[[Poaching]]**: Ideal for hearty [[Root Vegetables]] like [[Carrots]], [[Parsnips]], [[Rutabagas]], [[Turnips]], [[Fennel]], [[Artichokes]], [[Onions]], and [[Leeks]] when a tender, infused flavor is desired.
*   **[[Sous Vide]]**: Excellent for any [[Vegetable]] where precise temperature control and locking in flavors and [[Nutrients]] are paramount.
*   **[[Steaming]]**: A healthy, gentle method suitable for most [[Vegetables]], preserving their natural flavors and textures.
*   **[[Glazing]]**: Used for a wide variety of [[Vegetables]] when a slightly sweet, glossy finish is desired, enhancing their visual appeal and flavor.
*   **[[Braising]]/[[Stewing]]**: Perfect for developing deep, complex flavors in [[Vegetables]] like [[Leeks]], [[Celery]], [[Cabbage]], [[Sauerkraut]], and in dishes like [[Ratatouille]] or [[Moroccan Vegetable Tagine]].
*   **[[Sautéing]]**: Quick cooking for tender [[Vegetables]] like [[Spinach]], [[Green Beans]], or [[Carrots]], often finished à la minute. [[Stir-Frying]] is a variant for [[Asian Cuisine]].
*   **[[Frying]]**: For creating crisp textures and [[Caramelization]] in [[Vegetables]] prepared as [[Onion Rings]], [[Eggplant Fries]], [[Croquettes]], [[Chips]], [[Fritters]], or [[Tempura]].
*   **[[Grilling]]/[[Broiling]]**: Adds a rustic, smoky flavor and char to [[Asparagus]], [[Corn]], [[Eggplant]], [[Mushrooms]], [[Peppers]], [[Onions]], [[Tomatoes]], and various [[Squash]] varieties, often used for [[Kabobs]] or charring [[Peppers]] for [[Sauces]] and [[Salsa]].
*   **[[Roasting]]/[[Baking]]**: Brings out the natural sweetness and [[Caramelization]] in almost any [[Vegetable]], including [[Artichokes]], [[Beans]], [[Cauliflower]], and [[Winter Squash]].
