## Overview
Knife skills are the foundational set of techniques for cutting and preparing ingredients. Mastery of these skills is essential for ensuring ingredients cook evenly, improving the final texture and presentation of a dish, and working safely and efficiently in the kitchen. Core techniques include classic cuts like [[Dicing]], [[Julienne]], [[Mincing]], and [[Chiffonade]]. More advanced skills involve specific preparations for vegetables like [[Tomato|Tomatoes]], [[Bell Pepper|Peppers]], [[Artichoke|Artichokes]], and [[Fava Bean|Fava Beans]], as well as the classic [[Tourné]] cut used in [[French Cuisine]].

## The Science
The primary scientific principle behind precise knife skills is consistent [[Heat Transfer]]. When ingredients are cut to a uniform size, they cook at the same rate, preventing some pieces from being overcooked while others remain raw. The type of cut also affects flavour release. For example, [[Mincing]] [[Garlic]] ruptures more cell walls, releasing the enzyme alliinase which converts alliin into [[Allicin]], resulting in a much sharper, more pungent flavour than a simple slice. A sharp [[Chef's Knife]] makes cleaner cuts, causing less cellular damage to delicate herbs like [[Basil]] or leafy greens. This reduces bruising and slows enzymatic browning, a form of [[Oxidation]], which is especially important when preparing a [[Chiffonade]].

## How to Do It
While specific cuts have their own methods, universal principles apply to all knife work.

1.  **Secure Your Station:** Place a damp paper towel under your [[Cutting Board]] to prevent it from slipping.
2.  **The Knife Grip:** Use a "pinch grip" on your [[Chef's Knife]], pinching the base of the blade between your thumb and forefinger and wrapping your remaining fingers around the handle. This provides maximum control.
3.  **The Guide Hand:** Form your non-knife hand into a "claw grip," tucking your fingertips under and guiding the side of the blade against your knuckles. This protects your fingers and ensures precise, consistent cuts.
4.  **Create a Stable Base:** For round ingredients like an [[Onion]] or [[Potato]], slice a small piece off one side to create a flat, stable surface to work from.
5.  **Execute the Cut:** Use a smooth, rocking motion, starting with the tip of the knife on the board and pushing forward and down. The primary cuts include:
    *   **[[Julienne]]:** Cutting vegetables into long, thin "matchsticks."
    *   **[[Dicing]]:** Cutting ingredients into uniform cubes, often starting from a julienne.
    *   **[[Mincing]]:** Chopping an ingredient, typically an aromatic, into the smallest possible pieces.
    *   **[[Chiffonade]]:** Stacking and rolling leafy greens or herbs to slice them into fine ribbons.
    *   **[[Tourné]]:** A decorative, seven-sided cut resembling a small football, applied to root vegetables like [[Carrot|Carrots]] and potatoes.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Using a Dull Knife:** This is the most common and dangerous mistake. A dull blade requires more pressure, increasing the chance of it slipping and causing injury. It also crushes ingredients rather than slicing them. See [[Knife Sharpening]].
*   **Improper Grip:** Holding the knife only by the handle reduces control. Failing to use the "claw grip" with the guide hand exposes fingertips to the blade.
*   **Unstable Cutting Surface:** A rocking or sliding [[Cutting Board]] is an accident waiting to happen. Always secure it before starting.
*   **Inconsistent Sizes:** Cutting ingredients into various sizes will cause them to cook unevenly, negatively impacting the final dish's texture.
*   **Rushing:** Speed comes from practice and muscle memory. Focus on proper, safe technique first, and speed will follow naturally.

## When to Use It
Knife skills are a universal requirement for nearly all cooking, but specific cuts are chosen to achieve different outcomes.
*   **[[Dicing]]** is essential for foundational aromatic bases like a [[Mirepoix]] ([[Onion]], [[Carrot]], and [[Celery]]) or for dishes where ingredients are combined, like a [[Ratatouille]].
*   **[[Julienne]]** is ideal for creating texture in salads like [[Coleslaw]] or for quick-cooking preparations like a [[Stir-Fry]].
*   **[[Mincing]]** is used for aromatics like [[Garlic]], [[Ginger]], and shallots to ensure they distribute evenly and release maximum flavour in sauces, dressings, and marinades like a [[Gremolata]].
*   **[[Chiffonade]]** is used almost exclusively for herbs like [[Basil]] and [[Mint]] or leafy greens like [[Spinach]], often as a garnish or for gentle wilting.
*   **[[Tourné]]** is a classic cut from [[French Cuisine]], used primarily for presentation in braises or roasts to create an elegant appearance.
