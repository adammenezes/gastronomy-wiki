## Overview
A cartouche (French for "cartridge" or "scroll") is a lid made from a circle of [[Parchment Paper]] that is placed directly on the surface of food being cooked. Its primary purpose is to control [[Evaporation]], keep ingredients submerged in their cooking liquid, and prevent a skin from forming on the surface of sauces or stocks. This simple but effective tool is a cornerstone of classic [[French Cuisine]], used extensively in gentle cooking methods like [[Braising]], [[Poaching]], and making a [[Confit]]. It ensures food cooks evenly and gently, retaining moisture and flavour.

## The Science
The cartouche works by trapping a thin layer of [[Steam]] between the paper and the surface of the liquid. This creates a very humid environment that gently cooks the tops of ingredients not fully submerged, preventing them from drying out or oxidizing. By slowing down [[Evaporation]], the cartouche allows for longer, slower cooking without the liquid reducing too quickly, which is crucial for tenderizing tough cuts of meat in a [[Braise]]. For sauces and stocks held at a simmer, it prevents the formation of a skin, which is caused by the [[Protein Denaturation]] and drying of proteins at the surface. The small vent hole cut in the center allows excess steam to escape, preventing pressure from building up and ensuring the liquid remains at a gentle simmer rather than a rolling boil.

## How to Do It
Creating a cartouche is a simple and quick process that requires only [[Parchment Paper]] and [[Kitchen Shears]].

1.  Cut a square of parchment paper slightly larger than the diameter of the [[Pot]] or [[Pan]] you are using.
2.  Fold the square in half to form a rectangle.
3.  Fold the rectangle in half again to form a smaller square.
4.  Fold the square diagonally, from the corner with the loose edges to the corner with the original center of the paper, to form a triangle.
5.  Repeat the diagonal fold two more times to create a narrow, pointed cone.
6.  Hold the point of the cone over the center of the pot and trim the wide end of the cone so its length matches the radius of the pot (from the center to the edge).
7.  Snip off the very tip of the cone (about 1/4 inch or 1 cm). This will create a vent hole in the center.
8.  Unfold the paper. You will have a perfect circle that fits inside your pot, with a small hole in the middle.
9.  Place the cartouche directly onto the surface of the food and liquid.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Forgetting the vent hole:** Without a vent, too much steam is trapped, which can raise the temperature and cause the liquid to boil aggressively instead of simmer gently.
*   **Using the wrong material:** Never use wax paper, as the wax will melt into your food. [[Aluminum Foil]] can be used in a pinch, but it can react with acidic ingredients like [[Wine]] or [[Tomato]]es, imparting a metallic taste. [[Parchment Paper]] is the ideal material.
*   **Making it too small:** An undersized cartouche will not effectively cover the surface, allowing for uneven cooking and excessive evaporation around the edges.
*   **Not placing it directly on the food:** A cartouche is not a standard lid. If it rests on the rim of the pot, it fails to keep the food's surface moist and simply traps heat like a loose-fitting lid.

## When to Use It
A cartouche is invaluable for any slow, moist-heat cooking method where gentle, even cooking and moisture retention are desired.

*   **Braising:** Essential for dishes like [[Braised Short Ribs]], [[Coq au Vin]], or [[Osso Buco]] to keep the exposed surface of the meat from drying out during the long cooking time.
*   **Poaching:** Perfect for [[Poached Pears]] or [[Apples]], as it keeps the fruit submerged and prevents the surface from oxidizing and turning brown.
*   **Sweating Vegetables:** When making an [[Onion Confit]] or slowly sweating a [[Mirepoix]], a cartouche traps moisture, helping the vegetables soften without browning.
*   **Sauces and Stocks:** Use a cartouche to cover a simmering [[Velouté]], [[Béchamel]], or stock to prevent a skin from forming while it reduces or is being held for service.
