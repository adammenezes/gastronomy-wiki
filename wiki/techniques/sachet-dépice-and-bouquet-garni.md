## Overview
A Sachet d'Épices (French for "bag of spices") and a Bouquet Garni ("garnished bouquet") are bundles of aromatics used to infuse flavor into simmering liquids like [[Stock]], [[Stew]], and [[Sauce]]. A core technique in [[French Cuisine]], their primary purpose is to add depth from herbs and spices which can then be easily removed before serving, ensuring a clear, refined final product. A sachet typically contains dried herbs and spices like [[Parsley]], [[Thyme]], [[Bay Leaf]], and [[Peppercorns]] enclosed in a [[Cheesecloth]] bag. A bouquet garni consists of fresh herbs, often the same trio, tied together with twine and sometimes wrapped in a [[Leek]] leaf.

## The Science
The process of flavoring a liquid with a sachet or bouquet garni is a form of [[Infusion]]. As the liquid simmers, the heat and water act as a solvent, breaking down the cell walls of the plant materials. This process releases volatile aromatic compounds and essential oils into the surrounding liquid. The gentle, sustained heat of [[Simmering]] is crucial for effective flavor extraction without introducing the bitterness that can result from boiling herbs and spices too aggressively. The porous nature of the [[Cheesecloth]] or the loose tying of the bouquet allows for maximum surface area contact between the aromatics and the liquid, facilitating an efficient transfer of flavor.

## How to Do It
**To make a Sachet d'Épices:**
1.  Cut a small square of [[Cheesecloth]].
2.  Place your aromatics in the center. A classic combination includes dried [[Parsley]] stems, a few sprigs of [[Thyme]], one or two [[Bay Leaf|Bay Leaves]], and a teaspoon of whole [[Peppercorns]].
3.  Gather the corners of the cheesecloth to form a pouch and tie it securely with kitchen twine. Leave a long tail on the twine to tie to the pot handle for easy retrieval.

**To make a Bouquet Garni:**
1.  Gather your fresh herbs. The classic trio is several sprigs of fresh [[Parsley]], a few sprigs of fresh [[Thyme]], and a [[Bay Leaf]].
2.  Optionally, wrap the herbs in the dark green outer leaf of a [[Leek]] to contain them.
3.  Using kitchen twine, tie the bundle securely.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Using ground spices:** Powdered spices will escape through the [[Cheesecloth]], resulting in a cloudy liquid. Always use whole or lightly cracked spices like [[Peppercorns]].
*   **Tying the bundle too loosely:** If not secured properly, the bundle can come apart during cooking, forcing you to strain the entire dish to remove the aromatics.
*   **Over-infusing:** Leaving the sachet or bouquet in the liquid for too long can lead to an overpowering or bitter taste, particularly from potent ingredients like [[Bay Leaf]].
*   **Using old aromatics:** Stale, old herbs and spices have lost their volatile oils and will contribute very little flavor to the dish.

## When to Use It
This technique is ideal for any dish that involves long, slow simmering and requires a clear, refined finish. It is a foundational step in making classic [[Stock]], broths, and [[Consommé]]. It is indispensable in many [[French Cuisine]] preparations, from a simple [[Chicken Fricassee]] to a complex [[Boeuf Bourguignon]]. The aromatics can be customized to suit the main ingredients; for example, adding [[Rosemary]] to a sachet for a [[Lamb Stew]] or [[Tarragon]] to a bouquet garni for a chicken dish.
