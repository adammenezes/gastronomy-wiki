## Overview
Mirepoix is a foundational [[aromatic]] [[flavor base]] used extensively in [[French Cuisine]]. It consists of a specific [[ratio]] of [[diced]] [[vegetables]]: two parts [[Onion]], one part [[Carrots]], and one part [[Celery]]. This [[combination]] is slowly [[cooked]], often in [[Butter]] or [[oil]], to create a deep, complex background [[flavor]] for a wide variety of [[dishes]], most notably [[Stocks]], [[Soups]], [[Stews]], and [[braises]]. A well-made [[mirepoix]] adds [[savory]] [[depth]] without overpowering the main [[ingredients]]. A common [[variation]] is [[Matignon]], which includes [[Bacon]] or [[Ham]] for additional [[richness]].

## The Science
The purpose of a [[mirepoix]] is to [[extract]] and [[build]] layers of [[flavor]] through the slow [[breakdown]] of [[vegetable]] [[cell walls]]. As the [[vegetables]] [[cook]] gently, their [[volatile aromatic compounds]] are released into the [[cooking fat]] and subsequently into the larger [[dish]]. The [[Onion]] provides a [[pungent]], [[sulfurous]] [[base]], the [[Carrots]] contribute [[sweetness]] and [[color]] through their [[carotenoids]], and the [[Celery]] adds a slightly [[bitter]], [[vegetal]] [[note]]. When [[browned]] for a *[[mirepoix au gras]]* (a [[brown mirepoix]]), the [[Maillard Reaction]] and [[Caramelization]] occur, creating hundreds of new, complex [[flavor compounds]] that lend a deep, [[roasted]] [[flavor]] to [[dishes]] like [[Brown Stock]].

## How to Do It
1.  **Gather Ingredients:** Adhere to the classic 2:1:1 [[ratio]] by [[weight]]: two parts [[Onion]], one part [[Carrots]], and one part [[Celery]].
2.  **Prepare the Vegetables:** [[Wash]] and [[peel]] the [[vegetables]] as needed.
3.  **Cut to Size:** The [[size]] of the [[dice]] depends on the total [[cooking time]].
    *   **Long Cooking (2+ hours):** For [[dishes]] like [[Brown Stock]], use a large, [[rough chop]] (1-2 inch [[pieces]]). The [[vegetables]] can even be left [[whole]].
    *   **Short Cooking (under 1 hour):** For [[Soups]] or quick [[sauces]], use a smaller, more [[precise dice]] (¼ to ½ inch).
4.  **Cook:** Gently [[cook]] the [[mirepoix]] in a suitable [[fat]] (like [[Butter]] or [[oil]]) over [[low to medium heat]]. For a "[[white mirepoix]]" used in [[light-colored dishes]], [[sweat]] the [[vegetables]] until [[soft]] and [[translucent]] without [[browning]]. For a "[[brown mirepoix]]," continue [[cooking]] until the [[vegetables]] are deeply [[caramelized]].
5.  **Variations:** For different [[flavor profiles]], [[Leeks]] can [[substitute]] for [[onions]], or [[Celeriac]] can be used in place of [[celery]]. Adding [[Garlic]] towards the [[end of cooking]] provides another [[layer]] of [[aroma]]. For a [[Matignon]], add [[diced]] [[Bacon]] or [[Ham]] and [[cook]] with the [[vegetables]].

## Common Mistakes
*   **Incorrect Ratio:** Using too much [[carrot]] can make the final [[dish]] overly [[sweet]]. [[Sticking]] to the 2:1:1 [[ratio]] by [[weight]] is [[crucial]] for a [[balanced]] [[flavor foundation]].
*   **Uneven Cuts:** If the [[vegetable]] [[pieces]] are of [[inconsistent sizes]], they will [[cook]] [[unevenly]]. Smaller [[pieces]] may [[burn]] before larger ones are [[softened]], resulting in a [[bitter taste]].
*   **Cooking Too Hot:** For a standard ([[white]]) [[mirepoix]], [[high heat]] will cause [[browning]] too quickly, creating a [[roasted]] [[flavor]] that may be [[undesirable]] in a [[delicate]] [[Chicken Stock]] or a [[light vegetable]] [[Soup]].
*   **Cutting Too Small for Long Cooks:** [[Finely dicing]] [[vegetables]] for a [[long-simmering]] [[Stock]] will cause them to [[disintegrate]] completely, potentially making the [[stock]] [[cloudy]].

## When to Use It
[[Mirepoix]] is a [[versatile]] [[base]] used in [[countless]] [[preparations]]. It is the [[starting point]] for almost all [[classic]] [[Stocks]], including [[Vegetable Stock]], [[Chicken Stock]], and [[Brown Stock]]. It is [[essential]] for [[building]] the [[flavor]] of [[hearty]] [[Stews]], [[braises]], and most [[Soups]]. It can also be used as an [[aromatic bed]] on which to [[roast]] [[meats]], [[infusing]] the [[meat]] and the subsequent [[pan sauce]] with its [[flavor]]. Its use is a [[hallmark]] of [[traditional]] [[French Cuisine]].

*   [[Soffritto]]: The [[Italian flavor base]], which typically uses the same [[core ingredients]] but often adds [[garlic]] or [[parsley]] and is [[cooked]] in [[olive oil]].
*   [[Holy Trinity (Cajun)]]: The [[foundational aromatic blend]] in [[Cajun]] and [[Creole cooking]], consisting of [[onion]], [[celery]], and [[bell pepper]].
*   [[Stock]]: [[Mirepoix]] is the [[primary aromatic component]] used to [[flavor]] [[stocks]], the [[building blocks]] of many [[sauces]] and [[soups]].
*   [[Braising]]: This [[slow-cooking technique]] almost always begins with creating a [[flavor base]] from a [[mirepoix]] before [[liquid]] is added.
*   [[Sweating]]: The [[specific cooking technique]] often used to [[soften]] the [[mirepoix]] [[vegetables]] without [[browning]] them.
