## Overview
Tomato concasse (from the [[French]] *concasser*, meaning "to crush or grind") is a fundamental [[preparation technique]] in which a [[Tomato]] is [[peeled]], [[seeded]], and finely [[diced]]. The purpose is to create a refined [[tomato product]] with a uniform [[texture]], free from the toughness of the [[skin]] and the [[bitterness]] or [[watery texture]] of the [[seeds]]. This [[technique]] is a cornerstone of [[French Cuisine]] and is used to prepare bases for [[sauces]], [[soups]], and [[garnishes]]. It is essential for classic [[dishes]] like [[Bruschetta]], [[Gazpacho]], and [[Sauce Vierge]]. The principles of removing the [[skin]] and [[dicing]] the [[flesh]] can also be applied to other [[vegetables]], such as [[Bell Pepper]]s, though the [[method]] for [[skin removal]] may differ (e.g., [[charring]] instead of [[blanching]]).

## The Science
The process begins with [[Blanching]], a quick immersion in [[boiling water]]. This application of high [[heat]] causes the [[water]] in the [[cells]] just under the [[skin]] to turn to [[steam]], creating a [[pressure]] that separates the [[skin]] from the [[flesh]]. The subsequent "shock" in an [[Ice Bath]] halts the [[cooking process]], preventing the [[tomato]]'s [[pectin]] [[structure]] from breaking down further, which would result in a [[mushy]] [[texture]]. This rapid [[temperature change]], known as [[Thermal Shock]], makes the [[skin]] easy to [[peel]] away. Removing the [[seeds]] and the surrounding [[gelatinous pulp]] eliminates components that can add [[bitterness]] and excess [[water]], resulting in a purer, more concentrated [[tomato flavour]] and a more refined final [[texture]] in a [[dish]].

## How to Do It
1.  **Prepare:** Using a [[Paring Knife]], [[score]] a shallow "X" on the bottom of each [[Tomato]]. This will help the [[skin]] [[peel]] back easily. Prepare a large [[bowl]] of [[Ice Bath|ice water]] and bring a [[pot]] of [[water]] to a [[rolling boil]].
2.  **Blanch:** Carefully place the [[tomatoes]] into the [[boiling water]] using a [[Slotted Spoon]]. Leave them for only 15-30 seconds, or just until you see the [[skins]] at the "X" begin to [[curl]] away.
3.  **Shock:** Immediately transfer the [[tomatoes]] from the [[boiling water]] to the [[Ice Bath]]. This [[stops the cooking process]] and [[cools]] them for safe handling.
4.  **Peel & Seed:** Once [[cool]], remove the [[tomatoes]] from the [[water]]. The [[skins]] should [[slip off]] easily. [[Cut]] each [[tomato]] in half horizontally and gently [[squeeze]] or use a small [[spoon]] to remove the [[seeds]] and [[watery pulp]].
5.  **Dice:** Lay the [[peeled]] and [[seeded]] [[tomato flesh]] on a [[Cutting Board]] and [[chop]] or [[dice]] it to the desired [[size]]. For a very [[fine]] result, this [[cut]] is often referred to as a [[Brunoise]].

## Common Mistakes
*   **Over-blanching:** Leaving the [[Tomato|tomatoes]] in [[boiling water]] for too long will [[cook]] the [[flesh]], making it [[soft]] and difficult to [[dice]] neatly. The [[goal]] is only to [[loosen]] the [[skin]].
*   **Skipping the Ice Bath:** Failing to [[shock]] the [[tomatoes]] allows [[residual heat]] to continue [[cooking]] them, resulting in a [[mushy]] [[texture]].
*   **Using the Wrong Tomato:** For the best results, use [[firm]], [[ripe]] [[tomatoes]] with lower [[water content]], such as [[Roma Tomato|Roma]] or [[Plum Tomato|plum tomatoes]]. Overly [[ripe]] or [[juicy]] [[varieties]] can [[fall apart]].
*   **Incomplete Seeding:** Leaving behind [[seeds]] or the [[watery gel]] can negatively affect the final [[texture]] and [[flavour]] of the [[dish]], defeating the purpose of the [[technique]].

## When to Use It
This [[technique]] is ideal for [[dishes]] where a [[smooth]], [[refined]] [[texture]] is paramount. Use tomato concasse as a base for [[elegant sauces]] like a classic [[Tomato Sauce]] or a [[French]] [[Sauce Vierge]]. It is the required [[preparation]] for [[cold soups]] like [[Gazpacho]] and [[toppings]] like [[Bruschetta]], where [[tough skin]] would be [[unpleasant]]. It is also used to create a [[garnish]] for [[clear soups]] like [[Consommé]] or to add [[clean tomato flavour]] to [[sautés]] and [[stews]] without altering the [[texture]] with [[skins]] and [[seeds]].
