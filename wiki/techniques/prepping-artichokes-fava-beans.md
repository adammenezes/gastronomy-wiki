## Overview
Blanching is a cooking process wherein a food, usually a vegetable or fruit, is scalded in boiling water or steam for a short, timed interval. It is immediately followed by "shocking," the practice of plunging the food into an [[Ice Bath]] to halt the cooking process. This technique is fundamental in professional kitchens for preserving color, texture, and flavor. It is used to prepare vegetables like [[Green Beans]] for a [[Crudités]] platter, loosen the skins of [[Tomatoes]] or [[Almonds]], or prepare items like [[Corn]] and [[Winter Squash]] for [[Freezing]]. It is also essential for ingredients like [[Artichokes]] and [[Fava Beans]] to make them more palatable and easier to peel.

## The Science
The primary scientific purpose of blanching is [[Enzyme]] deactivation. Enzymes like lipoxygenase and polyphenol oxidase cause deterioration in quality, leading to off-flavors, discoloration, and loss of nutrients over time, especially during freezing. The brief exposure to high heat denatures these enzymes, preserving the food's integrity. For green vegetables, blanching helps to expel intercellular gases that cloud the vibrant color of [[Chlorophyll]], resulting in a brighter green appearance. The subsequent [[Shocking]] is critical; it rapidly cools the food, preventing [[Carryover Cooking]] that would soften the [[Cellulose]] in the plant's cell walls too much, leading to a mushy texture.

## How to Do It
1.  **Prepare the Water:** Bring a large [[Pot]] of water to a rolling boil. Salt the water generously (it should taste like the sea); this seasons the vegetable from the inside out.
2.  **Prepare the Ice Bath:** Fill a large [[Bowl]] with equal parts ice and cold [[Water]]. This is for [[Shocking]] the vegetables after they are boiled.
3.  **Cook:** Add the prepared vegetables, such as [[Fava Beans]] or chopped [[Artichokes]], to the boiling water. Do not overcrowd the pot. Cook for a brief, specific period, typically 30 seconds to 3 minutes, depending on the item's size and density.
4.  **Shock:** Using a [[Spider Strainer]] or [[Slotted Spoon]], quickly remove the vegetables from the boiling water and immediately plunge them into the prepared [[Ice Bath]].
5.  **Drain:** Once completely cool, remove the vegetables from the ice bath and drain them thoroughly on a clean kitchen towel or paper towels. They are now ready for use or storage.

## Common Mistakes
*   **Overcrowding the Pot:** Adding too many vegetables at once drastically lowers the water temperature, causing them to simmer or stew rather than blanch. This results in a soggy texture and dull color.
*   **Incorrect Timing:** Over-cooking, even by a minute, can turn crisp vegetables mushy. Under-cooking will fail to deactivate the necessary [[Enzyme]]s for long-term storage.
*   **Skipping the Salt:** Unsalted water can leach flavor and nutrients from the vegetables. Properly salted water seasons them effectively.
*   **Not Using an Ice Bath:** Omitting the [[Shocking]] step allows [[Carryover Cooking]] to continue, which will overcook the vegetables and negate the benefits of blanching.

## When to Use It
Blanching is a versatile preparatory technique used in a wide range of applications:
*   **Preservation:** It is an essential step before [[Freezing]] most vegetables, including [[Peas]], [[Corn]], [[Winter Squash]], and [[Broccoli]], to preserve their quality.
*   **Color Enhancement:** To make green vegetables like [[Asparagus]], [[Green Beans]], or [[Edamame]] exceptionally vibrant for use in [[Salads]] or as a garnish.
*   **Peeling:** To easily remove the skins from produce like [[Tomatoes]], [[Peaches]], and [[Almonds]]. It is also key for peeling the tough outer layer of [[Fava Beans]].
*   **Flavor Modification:** To reduce the bitterness or raw intensity of certain vegetables, such as [[Cabbage]] or [[Onions]], before incorporating them into a dish.
*   **Pre-cooking:** To partially cook vegetables before they are finished with another method, such as a [[Sauté]] or a [[Stir-fry]], ensuring everything cooks evenly.
