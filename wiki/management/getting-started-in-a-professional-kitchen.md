# Yield Testing

## Overview
Yield Testing is the process of measuring the amount of usable product (Edible Portion or EP) obtained from a raw ingredient after it has undergone fabrication or preparation (the As Purchased or AP amount). This is a fundamental practice in kitchen management, as it provides the critical data needed for accurate [[Food Costing]], effective [[Purchasing]], and consistent [[Portion Control]]. By understanding the true yield of an ingredient, a chef can determine its actual cost per usable ounce or pound, leading to smarter menu pricing and better control over inventory.

## Key Concepts
- **As Purchased (AP) Weight:** The total weight of an ingredient as it is received from the supplier, before any trimming, cleaning, or cooking.
- **Edible Portion (EP) Weight:** The weight of an ingredient after it has been processed and is ready to be used in a recipe. This is also known as the "fabricated weight" or "usable yield."
- **Trim Loss:** The weight of all material removed during processing, such as peels, seeds, bones, fat, or inedible leaves. It is the difference between AP weight and EP weight.
- **Yield Percentage (%):** The ratio of the Edible Portion weight to the As Purchased weight, expressed as a percentage. This is the most important metric derived from a yield test.

## Calculations & Formulas

### 1. Yield Percentage
This formula calculates the percentage of the raw product that is usable after trimming.

**Formula:** `Yield % = (EP Weight / AP Weight) * 100`

**Example:**
You purchase a 10 lb case of [[Winter Squash]] (AP Weight). After peeling, seeding, and dicing, you are left with 7.5 lbs of usable squash cubes (EP Weight).
- **Calculation:** `(7.5 lbs / 10 lbs) * 100 = 75%`
- **Result:** The yield for this winter squash is 75%.

### 2. Edible Portion (EP) Cost
This formula determines the actual cost of the usable ingredient, which is always higher than the purchase price per unit.

**Formula:** `EP Cost per Unit = AP Cost per Unit / Yield %`

**Example:**
The 10 lb case of squash cost $15.00, making the AP cost $1.50 per pound. The yield is 75% (or 0.75).
- **Calculation:** `$1.50 / 0.75 = $2.00`
- **Result:** The true cost of the usable squash is $2.00 per pound, not $1.50. This is the number you must use for accurate [[Food Costing]].

### 3. As Purchased (AP) Quantity Needed
This formula helps you determine how much of a raw ingredient to order to meet a specific recipe requirement.

**Formula:** `AP Quantity Needed = EP Quantity Needed / Yield %`

**Example:**
A banquet recipe requires 40 lbs of usable, diced winter squash (EP Quantity). You know your yield is 75%.
- **Calculation:** `40 lbs / 0.75 = 53.33 lbs`
- **Result:** You must purchase at least 54 lbs of whole winter squash to ensure you have 40 lbs for your recipe.

## Implementation
Follow these steps to conduct a reliable yield test in your kitchen:

1.  **Gather Materials:** You will need the raw ingredient, a calibrated [[Digital Scale]], cutting board, [[Chef's Knife]], and containers for both the usable product and the trim.
2.  **Record AP Weight:** Weigh the ingredient in its original, "as purchased" state. Record this weight.
3.  **Fabricate the Product:** Process the ingredient exactly as you would for a menu item. This includes washing, peeling, trimming, seeding, deboning, etc. Be consistent with your technique.
4.  **Weigh the Edible Portion:** Place all the usable product in a clean container and weigh it. Record this as the EP Weight.
5.  **Calculate the Yield:** Use the formulas above to calculate the Yield Percentage and the EP Cost.
6.  **Document Results:** Log the results in a "Yield Book" or a shared spreadsheet. Include the item name, supplier, date, AP weight, EP weight, and the final Yield %.
7.  **Repeat:** Perform yield tests multiple times for the same ingredient, especially if sourced from different suppliers or during different seasons, to establish a reliable average.

## Industry Standards
Yields are not universal and can be affected by the quality of the product, the season, the supplier, and the skill of the cook performing the fabrication. However, established industry guides like *The Book of Yields* provide reliable averages that can be used as a baseline.

**Typical Yield Percentage Ranges:**
- **Beef Tenderloin, PSMO:** 70-75% (trimmed to steak-ready)
- **Whole [[Chicken]], Roaster:** 65-70% (meat only, deboned)
- **Whole Round Fish (e.g., Snapper):** 40-48% (skinless fillets)
- **[[Onion|Onions]], Yellow:** 88-92% (peeled, topped, and tailed)
- **[[Carrot|Carrots]], Jumbo:** 80-85% (peeled and trimmed)
- **[[Asparagus]]:** 55-65% (trimmed)
- **[[Corn]], on the cob:** 50-55% (kernels only)
