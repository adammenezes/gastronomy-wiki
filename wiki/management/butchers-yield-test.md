# Butcher's Yield Test

## Overview
A Butcher's Yield Test is a systematic process used to determine the true, usable cost of a product after it has been trimmed and fabricated. By measuring the difference between the "As-Purchased" (AP) state of a raw product (like a sub-primal cut of [[Beef Tenderloin]] or a whole [[Fish]]) and its final "Edible Portion" (EP) state, chefs and managers can calculate the actual cost per portion. This is a critical tool for accurate [[Menu Pricing]], effective [[Portion Control]], vendor comparison, and evaluating the skill of kitchen staff performing [[Fabrication]].

## Key Concepts
- **As-Purchased (AP):** The weight, volume, or count of a product as it is received from the supplier, before any in-house processing. The AP Cost is the price paid for the item in this state.
- **Edible Portion (EP):** The weight, volume, or count of a product after it has been cleaned, trimmed, cooked, and portioned, ready for service. The EP Cost is the calculated true cost of this final, sellable product.
- **Yield Percentage:** The percentage of the As-Purchased weight that remains after fabrication, representing the usable or sellable portion of the product.
- **Trim:** The material removed during fabrication, such as fat, bone, sinew, or unusable scraps. Trim can be further categorized as:
    - **Usable Trim:** Byproducts that can be repurposed for other preparations, such as bones for [[Stock]], fat for rendering, or meat scraps for [[Ground Meat]]. Some operations assign a value to this trim to further reduce the primary item's EP cost.
    - **Unusable Trim:** Waste that has no value and must be discarded.
- **Cost Factor (Yield Multiplier):** A number calculated by dividing 1 by the yield percentage (in decimal form). This factor can be multiplied by the AP price per unit to quickly determine the EP price per unit.

## Calculations & Formulas
The core goal is to transfer the total cost of the raw item to the final, sellable portions. All weight measurements should be in the same unit (e.g., ounces or grams) for accuracy.

- **Yield Percentage**
  `Yield % = (Edible Portion Weight / As-Purchased Weight) * 100`

- **Edible Portion (EP) Cost per Unit**
  `EP Cost = As-Purchased Cost per Unit / Yield % (as a decimal)`
  *or*
  `EP Cost = Total AP Cost / Edible Portion Weight`

---
### Example: Fabricating a Beef Tenderloin

Let's say we purchase a PSMO (Peeled, Side Muscle On) [[Beef Tenderloin]] to cut 8 oz [[Filet Mignon]] steaks.

**Given Data:**
- **Item:** PSMO Beef Tenderloin
- **As-Purchased (AP) Weight:** 8 lbs (128 oz)
- **As-Purchased (AP) Cost:** $18.00 per lb

**Step 1: Calculate Total AP Cost**
`Total AP Cost = AP Weight * AP Cost per lb`
`8 lbs * $18.00/lb = $144.00`

**Step 2: Fabricate and Weigh the Results**
After trimming the fat, silver skin, and side muscle (chain), and portioning the steaks, we have:
- **Edible Portion (EP) Weight (Steaks):** 6 lbs (96 oz)
- **Trim Weight (Fat, Chain, etc.):** 2 lbs (32 oz)

**Step 3: Calculate Yield Percentage**
`Yield % = (EP Weight / AP Weight) * 100`
`Yield % = (6 lbs / 8 lbs) * 100 = 75%`

**Step 4: Calculate Edible Portion (EP) Cost per Pound**
This is the true cost of the steak meat.
`EP Cost per lb = Total AP Cost / EP Weight`
`EP Cost per lb = $144.00 / 6 lbs = $24.00 per lb`

*Alternatively, using the yield percentage:*
`EP Cost per lb = AP Cost per lb / Yield %`
`EP Cost per lb = $18.00 / 0.75 = $24.00 per lb`

The true cost of the usable filet mignon is $24.00/lb, not the $18.00/lb we paid for the whole tenderloin. This is the figure that must be used for accurate [[Food Cost]] calculations.

## Implementation
Follow these steps to conduct a butcher's yield test in a professional kitchen:

1.  **Select the Item:** Choose the product to be tested. Ensure it is a representative sample.
2.  **Record AP Data:** Before any work begins, weigh the item in its "as-purchased" state. Record this as the **AP Weight**. Note the invoice cost for the item as the **AP Cost**.
3.  **Set Up for Fabrication:** Prepare a clean and sanitized work station with separate, tared containers for the finished portions and the trim.
4.  **Fabricate the Product:** Trim, bone, and portion the item according to the standard specifications for your menu. Place the finished, sellable portions in one container and all trim (fat, bones, sinew, scraps) in another.
5.  **Weigh the Components:**
    - Weigh the container with the finished portions. Subtract the container's tare weight to get the **EP Weight**.
    - Weigh the container with the trim. Subtract the tare weight to get the **Trim Weight**.
    - **Verification:** The EP Weight + Trim Weight should equal the original AP Weight. A small discrepancy due to moisture loss is acceptable.
6.  **Perform Calculations:** Use the formulas above to calculate the Yield Percentage and the EP Cost per unit (e.g., per pound, per ounce, or per portion).
7.  **Document and Analyze:** Record the results in a log or spreadsheet. Use this data to:
    - Set or verify menu prices.
    - Compare the value of products from different suppliers or with different specifications (e.g., PSMO vs. a fully trimmed tenderloin).
    - Establish a performance benchmark for training kitchen staff.
    - Make informed "make vs. buy" decisions (e.g., cutting steaks in-house vs. buying pre-portioned steaks).

## Industry Standards
There are no universal yield percentages, as yields vary significantly based on:
- **The specific product:** A [[Striploin]] will have a different yield than a [[Ribeye]].
- **Supplier specifications:** The level of trim performed by the purveyor before delivery directly impacts in-house yield.
- **Butcher's skill:** A skilled butcher will maximize the yield, while an inexperienced one may produce more waste.

The primary goal of the Butcher's Yield Test is to establish a consistent **in-house standard** for each specific product you purchase. This internal benchmark becomes the standard against which you measure future deliveries, employee performance, and cost-effectiveness. Many suppliers can provide "spec sheets" that list an expected yield range for their products, which can be a useful starting point.
