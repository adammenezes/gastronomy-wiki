# Culinary Math

## Overview
Culinary math is the system of basic and advanced calculations used in professional kitchens to manage and control resources effectively. It is a fundamental skill for chefs and managers, essential for tasks ranging from scaling [[Standardized Recipe|Standardized Recipes]] and managing [[Portion Control]] to calculating [[Food Cost]] and establishing budgets. Mastery of culinary math ensures accuracy in production, consistency in product quality, and profitability in the foodservice operation.

## Key Concepts
- **Measurement Systems**:
    - **U.S. System**: Measures weight in [[Ounce|ounces]] and [[Pound|pounds]]; volume in [[Cup|cups]], [[Pint|pints]], [[Quart|quarts]], and [[Gallon|gallons]]; and temperature in Fahrenheit.
    - **Metric System**: A decimal-based system measuring weight in [[Gram|grams]] and [[Kilogram|kilograms]]; volume in [[Milliliter|milliliters]] and [[Liter|liters]]; and temperature in Celsius. It is the global standard and preferred for its precision, especially with small quantities.
- **Weight vs. Volume**:
    - **Weight**: An absolute measure of mass. It is the professional standard for measuring dry ingredients (e.g., [[Flour]], [[Sugar]]) because it is far more accurate and consistent than volume.
    - **Volume**: A measure of the space an ingredient occupies. It is reliable for liquids but can be highly inaccurate for compressible dry ingredients.
- **As-Purchased (AP)**: The weight, volume, or count of a product as it is received from the supplier, before any trimming, cleaning, or cooking. Also referred to as As-Purchased Quantity (APQ).
- **Edible Portion (EP)**: The weight, volume, or count of a product after it has been cleaned, trimmed, and prepared for use. This is the usable amount of the ingredient. Also referred to as Edible Portion Quantity (EPQ).
- **Yield Percentage**: The percentage of an As-Purchased ingredient that remains as the Edible Portion after preparation. `Yield % = EP Weight / AP Weight`.
- **Recipe Conversion Factor (RCF)**: A multiplier used to increase or decrease the yield of a [[Standardized Recipe]]. It is calculated by dividing the desired yield by the original recipe yield.
- **[[Butcher's Yield Test]]**: A specific type of [[Yield Testing]] performed on large cuts of meat to determine the precise cost of individual portions, usable trim, fat, and bone, thereby establishing an accurate [[Plate Cost]].

## Calculations & Formulas

### Measurement Conversions

**Converting to Smaller Units (Multiplying):**
- **Pounds to Ounces**: `Pounds × 16 = Ounces`
    - *Example*: `3 lb × 16 oz/lb = 48 oz`
- **Quarts to Fluid Ounces**: `Quarts × 32 = Fluid Ounces`
    - *Example*: `2 qt × 32 fl oz/qt = 64 fl oz`
- **Cups to Fluid Ounces**: `Cups × 8 = Fluid Ounces`
    - *Example*: `3 cups × 8 fl oz/cup = 24 fl oz`
- **Gallons to Quarts**: `Gallons × 4 = Quarts`
    - *Example*: `5 gal × 4 qt/gal = 20 qt`

**Converting to Larger Units (Dividing):**
- **Ounces to Pounds**: `Ounces / 16 = Pounds`
    - *Example*: `56 oz / 16 oz/lb = 3.5 lb` (or 3 lb, 8 oz)
- **Fluid Ounces to Quarts**: `Fluid Ounces / 32 = Quarts`
    - *Example*: `80 fl oz / 32 fl oz/qt = 2.5 qt`

### Yield Percentage & Edible Portion

**Calculating Yield Percentage:**
- **Formula**: `EP Weight / AP Weight = Yield Percentage`
- **Example**: You purchase 5 lbs of green beans (AP). After trimming the ends, you have 4 lbs of usable beans (EP).
    - `4 lb (EP) / 5 lb (AP) = 0.80`
    - The yield percentage is 80%.

**Calculating As-Purchased Quantity (APQ) Needed:**
This is used to determine how much raw product to buy to get a specific amount of usable product.
- **Formula**: `EPQ Needed / Yield Percentage = APQ to Purchase`
- **Example**: You need 80 portions of a vegetable, each weighing 4 oz. The vegetable has a known yield of 70% (0.70).
    1.  **Calculate total EPQ needed**: `80 portions × 4 oz/portion = 320 oz`
    2.  **Convert EPQ to pounds**: `320 oz / 16 oz/lb = 20 lb`
    3.  **Calculate APQ**: `20 lb (EPQ) / 0.70 (Yield %) = 28.57 lb`
    4.  **Action**: You must purchase at least 28.57 lbs. It is standard practice to round up to the next whole unit, so you would order 29 or 30 lbs.

## Implementation

1.  **Standardize Measurement**: Equip the kitchen with accurate digital scales and enforce a "weight, don't measure" policy for all dry ingredients in [[Standardized Recipe|Standardized Recipes]].
2.  **Conduct Yield Tests**: Perform regular [[Yield Testing]] on key ingredients, especially produce and proteins, as yields can vary by supplier, season, and quality. Do not rely solely on published figures like those in *The Book of Yields*.
3.  **Scale Recipes Accurately**: When changing recipe yields, calculate the Recipe Conversion Factor (RCF) and apply it to every ingredient. Avoid simple doubling or halving for non-standard amounts to maintain recipe integrity.
4.  **Integrate into Purchasing**: Use yield data to inform purchasing decisions. Calculate the APQ needed for your menu forecast to avoid over-ordering or running short.
5.  **Manage Trim and Waste**: A key part of yield management is finding value in trim. Use vegetable trim for [[Stock]], meat scraps for ground applications, and compost unusable organic matter to reduce waste removal costs and improve profitability.

## Industry Standards
- **Measurement by Weight**: The universal standard in professional baking, pastry, and high-volume kitchens is to measure all critical ingredients by weight for maximum accuracy and consistency.
- **In-House Yield Data**: While industry guides provide average yields, the professional standard is to establish and maintain your own yield data through in-house testing. This accounts for variations in product quality, fabrication skill, and specific supplier products.
- **Standardized Recipes**: The use of detailed [[Standardized Recipe|Standardized Recipes]] that include weights, yields, portion sizes, and costs is a fundamental operational standard for any professional foodservice business.
