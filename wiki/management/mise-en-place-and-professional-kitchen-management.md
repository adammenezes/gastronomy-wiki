# Mise en Place

## Overview
Mise en place is a French culinary phrase meaning "everything in its place." It refers to the comprehensive process of gathering, preparing, and organizing all the ingredients and tools required for service before cooking begins. This foundational principle of kitchen management encompasses everything from chopping [[vegetables]] and butchering proteins to setting up a cook's station with all necessary equipment and smallwares. Proper mise en place is critical for ensuring a smooth, efficient, and successful service, as it minimizes delays, reduces stress, and promotes consistency and quality in a high-pressure kitchen environment.

## Key Concepts
- **Prep List:** A detailed, itemized checklist of all tasks that must be completed before service. It is derived by breaking down each menu item into its components and using sales data to forecast quantities.
- **Par-cooking:** The technique of partially cooking food which will be finished to order during service. This is a key time-saving element of mise en place for items like [[risotto]] or blanched vegetables.
- **Station Setup:** The ergonomic and logical arrangement of a cook's workspace. All tools, equipment, and ingredients should be within arm's reach to minimize movement and maximize efficiency during service.
- **Workflow:** The logical sequence of movement and tasks within a station or the kitchen as a whole. An efficient workflow is designed to execute menu items smoothly with minimal wasted steps or cross-traffic.
- **Consolidated Prep:** The practice of preparing ingredients common to multiple menu items or stations at the same time. For example, dicing a large batch of [[onions]] for [[soup]], [[sauce]], and a sauté station simultaneously improves overall kitchen efficiency.

## Calculations & Formulas
A common guideline for determining prep quantities is the "125% Rule," which helps prevent running out of an item during a busy service while minimizing waste from over-prepping.

**125% Prep Rule**
This rule suggests preparing 25% more of an item than the forecasted sales quantity.

- **Formula:** `Prep Quantity = Forecasted Sales Quantity × 1.25`

**Example:**
- A [[POS System|POS report]] forecasts that you will sell 32 orders of Pan-Seared Salmon on Friday night.
- **Calculation:** `32 portions × 1.25 = 40 portions`
- **Implementation:** You would prep, portion, and have 40 portions of salmon ready on the line to safely cover service and any unexpected demand.

## Implementation
Applying mise en place is a daily discipline that involves several key steps:

1.  **Develop a Prep List:**
    *   Arrive early and review the prep list for your station. If one doesn't exist, create one.
    *   Analyze menu recipes and use sales forecasts to determine the quantity of each item to be prepped.
    *   Prioritize tasks based on complexity and cooking/cooling time.

2.  **Organize the Workstation:**
    *   Gather all necessary [[cutlery]], tools (e.g., [[cutting board]], [[sharpening steel]]), and equipment.
    *   Arrange ingredients and tools in a logical, continuous flow (e.g., left to right), moving from raw product to finished prep.
    *   Set up containers for finished product, food waste, and tasting spoons (one for clean, one for used).
    *   Place a [[sanitizing solution|sani-bucket]] on a lower shelf, away from food contact surfaces.

3.  **Execute Preparation:**
    *   Work systematically through the prep list.
    *   Consolidate tasks by prepping common ingredients like [[garlic]] or [[herbs]] for multiple recipes at once.
    *   Communicate with other cooks to coordinate shared prep items and avoid duplication of effort.
    *   Label and date all prepared items clearly before storing them in their designated place.

4.  **Maintain Cleanliness and Order:**
    *   Clean as you go. Keep your cutting board and work surfaces free of clutter.
    *   Periodically stop to wipe down your station and reorganize your tools.
    *   Return shared equipment and bulk ingredients to their proper storage location immediately after use.

5.  **Finalize Station Setup for Service:**
    *   Stock the line station with all portioned ingredients, sauces, and garnishes.
    *   Check temperatures of all hot and cold holding units.
    *   Do a final check of the station against a service checklist to ensure nothing is missing.

## Industry Standards
- **State of Readiness:** The professional standard is to have 100% of mise en place completed before the first order is fired. Running out of a prepped item during service is considered a significant operational failure.
- **Cleanliness:** A "clean as you go" approach is a non-negotiable standard. A clean, organized station is directly linked to [[Food Safety|food safety]], efficiency, and professionalism.
- **Ergonomics:** While setups vary, the principle of minimizing steps and movement is universal. A well-organized station where a cook can pivot rather than walk is the industry benchmark for an efficient line setup.
- **Teamwork:** Cooks are expected to manage their own mise en place but also to assist others and communicate effectively about shared prep items, equipment, and space.
