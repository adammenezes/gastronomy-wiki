# Culinary Ratios

## Overview
[[Culinary ratios]] are a foundational [[tool]] in [[professional kitchens]] that express the proportional relationship between [[ingredients]] by parts, rather than by specific [[measurements]] like [[grams]] or [[cups]]. This [[method]] allows for rapid and consistent [[scaling]] of [[preparations]], encourages [[creativity]], and provides a deeper understanding of the [[scientific principles]] behind [[cooking]]. Unlike a static [[recipe]], a [[ratio]] is a flexible [[formula]] that, when combined with proper [[technique]], enables a [[chef]] to efficiently produce a wide range of [[dishes]] from [[stocks]] and [[sauces]] to [[doughs]] and [[braises]].

## Key Concepts
- **Ratio:** A fixed proportion of [[ingredients]] relative to one another, defined in "parts." For example, a 1:1 [[ratio]] for a [[roux]] means one part [[fat]] to one part [[flour]] by [[weight]].
- **Ratio by Weight:** The most accurate and preferred [[method]] in [[professional kitchens]]. [[Ingredients]] are measured using a [[scale]], ensuring [[consistency]] regardless of [[density]] or [[preparation]].
- **Ratio by Volume:** A less precise [[method]] where [[ingredients]] are measured by [[volume]] (e.g., [[cups]], [[liters]]). Commonly used for [[liquids]] or [[preparations]] where minor [[variations]] are acceptable, like a 2:1 [[ratio]] for [[rice pilaf]] (2 parts [[liquid]] to 1 part [[rice]]).
- **Ratio by Count:** A [[method]] used for [[ingredients]] measured in whole units, such as 6 [[egg yolks]] per 1 [[lb]] of [[butter]] for a [[Hollandaise Sauce]].
- **[[Baker's Percentage]]:** A distinct [[system]] used in [[baking]] where [[flour]] is always 100%, and all other [[ingredients]] are expressed as a [[percentage]] of the [[flour]]'s [[weight]]. This is different from a simple [[ratio]].
- **Simple Percentage:** A [[method]] where all [[ingredients]] in a [[formula]] add up to 100%. A classic [[mirepoix]] can be expressed as 50% [[onion]], 25% [[carrot]], and 25% [[celery]].

## Calculations & Formulas

[[Ratios]] are [[scaled]] by assigning a [[weight]] or [[volume]] to one "part" and multiplying accordingly.

### Example 1: White Stock (by Weight)
- **Ratio:** `3 parts [[water]] : 2 parts [[bones]]`
- **Goal:** Produce approximately 1.5 [[gallons]] of [[stock]].
- **Calculation:**
    1.  Let's define 1 part as 4 [[lbs]].
    2.  **Water:** 3 parts × 4 [[lbs]]/part = 12 [[lbs]] of [[water]] (approx. 1.5 [[gallons]])
    3.  **Bones:** 2 parts × 4 [[lbs]]/part = 8 [[lbs]] of [[bones]]
    4.  **Note:** A standard addition, separate from the core [[ratio]], is 1 [[lb]] of [[mirepoix]] for every [[gallon]] of finished [[stock]].

### Example 2: Basic Braise (by Weight)
- **Ratio:** `10 parts [[protein]] : 1 part [[mirepoix]] : 1-2 parts [[liquid]]`
- **Goal:** [[Braise]] a 5 [[lb]] (80 [[oz]]) [[beef chuck roast]].
- **Calculation:**
    1.  The [[protein]] is the known quantity: 10 parts = 80 [[oz]].
    2.  Calculate the size of one part: 80 [[oz]] / 10 parts = 8 [[oz]]/part.
    3.  **Mirepoix:** 1 part × 8 [[oz]]/part = 8 [[oz]] of [[mirepoix]].
    4.  **Liquid:** 1 to 2 parts × 8 [[oz]]/part = 8 to 16 [[oz]] of [[stock]], [[wine]], or other [[braising liquid]].

### Example 3: Classic Vinaigrette
- **Ratio:** `3 parts [[oil]] : 1 part [[vinegar]]`
- **Goal:** Make 1 [[quart]] (32 [[fl oz]]) of [[vinaigrette]].
- **Calculation:**
    1.  Total parts in the [[ratio]]: 3 + 1 = 4 parts.
    2.  Calculate the [[volume]] of one part: 32 [[fl oz]] / 4 parts = 8 [[fl oz]]/part.
    3.  **Oil:** 3 parts × 8 [[fl oz]]/part = 24 [[fl oz]] of [[oil]].
    4.  **Vinegar:** 1 part × 8 [[fl oz]]/part = 8 [[fl oz]] of [[vinegar]].

## Implementation
Applying [[culinary ratios]] effectively requires a combination of [[calculation]] and [[technique]].

1.  **Identify the Core Ratio:** Select the appropriate foundational [[ratio]] for the desired [[preparation]] (e.g., [[stock]], [[sauce]], [[dough]]).
2.  **Determine Desired Yield:** Decide the total final [[quantity]] needed for [[service]] or [[production]].
3.  **Calculate Part Size:** Based on the desired [[yield]] and the total number of parts in the [[ratio]], calculate the [[weight]] or [[volume]] of a single "part."
4.  **Scale Ingredients:** Use the calculated part size to accurately measure each [[ingredient]] for your [[Mise en Place]].
5.  **Execute with Proper Technique:** Follow the correct [[culinary procedures]] for the [[preparation]]. The [[ratio]] provides the "what," but the [[chef]]'s [[skill]] provides the "how."
6.  **Adjust and Finalize:** [[Taste]] and [[adjust]] the final [[product]]. [[Ratios]] are a starting point; factors like [[ingredient quality]] ([[acidity]] of [[vinegar]], [[salt content]] of [[stock]]) and [[cooking variables]] ([[evaporation]]) require a [[chef]]'s [[judgment]] to [[season]] and [[finish]] the [[dish]] perfectly.

## Industry Standards
- **Weight is the Standard:** For [[professional accuracy]] and [[consistency]], [[ratios]] are almost always calculated by [[weight]]. This eliminates [[inconsistencies]] from [[variations]] in [[ingredient density]] or how an [[item]] is packed into a [[measuring cup]].
- **Foundational Knowledge:** [[Chefs]] and [[cooks]] are expected to know common [[culinary ratios]] by [[heart]] as part of their [[fundamental skill set]]. This includes [[ratios]] for [[roux]], [[vinaigrette]], [[stocks]], [[basic doughs]], and [[custards]].
- **A Guideline, Not a Rule:** [[Ratios]] are understood to be a [[scientific starting point]]. The "[[art]]" of [[cooking]] comes from the [[chef]]'s [[ability]] to [[adjust]] a [[ratio]] based on the specific [[ingredients]] being used and the desired [[outcome]]. For example, a [[vinaigrette ratio]] may shift from 3:1 to 4:1 if using a very [[sharp]], [[acidic vinegar]] with a [[delicate oil]].
