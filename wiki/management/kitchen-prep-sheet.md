# Yield Testing

## Overview
Yield Testing is the process of determining the net or usable weight (Edible Portion) of a food item from its initial, unprocessed weight (As Purchased). This is a fundamental practice in professional kitchens for achieving accurate [[Food Costing]], effective [[Portion Control]], and intelligent purchasing. By understanding the true yield of an ingredient after trimming, deboning, or cooking, a chef can calculate the actual cost of the usable product, which is essential for precise recipe costing and menu pricing.

## Key Concepts
- **As Purchased (AP) Weight:** The total weight of an ingredient as it is received from the supplier, before any fabrication or preparation.
- **Edible Portion (EP) Weight:** The weight of an ingredient after it has been cleaned, trimmed, fabricated, and is ready for use in a recipe. This is the usable amount.
- **Trim Loss:** The weight of all waste generated during fabrication, such as peels, seeds, bones, fat, and unusable scraps. It is the difference between AP Weight and EP Weight.
- **Yield Percentage (%):** The ratio of the Edible Portion weight to the As Purchased weight, expressed as a percentage. This metric indicates how much of the initial product is usable.
- **As Purchased (AP) Cost:** The price paid to the supplier for the ingredient in its AP state (e.g., cost per case, per pound).
- **Edible Portion (EP) Cost:** The true cost of the usable portion of an ingredient. It is calculated by dividing the AP Cost by the EP Weight, which accounts for the cost of the trim loss. Also known as "Fabricated Cost" or "Cost per Usable Pound."

## Calculations & Formulas
The core purpose of a yield test is to calculate the Yield Percentage and the Edible Portion (EP) Cost.

1.  **Yield Percentage Formula:**
    ```
    (Edible Portion Weight / As Purchased Weight) * 100 = Yield %
    ```

2.  **Edible Portion Cost Formula:**
    ```
    As Purchased Cost / Edible Portion Weight = Edible Portion Cost per Unit
    ```

**Example: Yield Test for Corn on the Cob**

Let's use the data for corn from the source content.

- **Given:**
    - AP Weight: 50 lbs
    - Trim Loss (husks, silks, ends): 27.5 lbs
    - AP Cost: $25.00

- **Step 1: Calculate EP Weight**
    - `AP Weight - Trim Loss = EP Weight`
    - `50 lbs - 27.5 lbs = 22.5 lbs`

- **Step 2: Calculate Yield Percentage**
    - `(EP Weight / AP Weight) * 100 = Yield %`
    - `(22.5 lbs / 50 lbs) * 100 = 45%`
    - *The yield for corn on the cob is 45%.*

- **Step 3: Calculate EP Cost per Pound**
    - `AP Cost / EP Weight = EP Cost per lb`
    - `$25.00 / 22.5 lbs = $1.11 per lb`
    - *While the AP cost was $0.50/lb ($25/50lbs), the true cost of the usable corn kernels is $1.11/lb.*

## Implementation
Follow these steps to conduct a yield test in a professional kitchen:

1.  **Gather Materials:** You will need the raw ingredient, a receiving [[scale]], cutting boards, appropriate [[knives]], and containers for the finished product and the trim.
2.  **Record AP Weight:** Before any processing, weigh the ingredient in its "as purchased" state. Record this as the AP Weight.
3.  **Fabricate the Item:** Process the ingredient exactly as you would for its intended recipe. For example, peel and dice a [[butternut squash]], shuck corn, or trim a [[beef tenderloin]].
4.  **Separate and Weigh:** Keep all trim separate from the usable product. Weigh the final Edible Portion and record this as the EP Weight. As a cross-check, you can also weigh the trim; AP Weight minus Trim Loss should equal EP Weight.
5.  **Calculate Results:** Use the formulas above to determine the Yield Percentage and the EP Cost per unit (e.g., per pound, ounce, or kilogram).
6.  **Document:** Record the results in a "Yield Book" or a digital spreadsheet. This creates a reference for your specific operation, suppliers, and staff skill level, which can be used for future [[Recipe Costing]] and purchasing decisions.

## Industry Standards
There are no universal, fixed yield percentages, as they can vary significantly based on:
- **Product Quality & Size:** A larger, higher-grade vegetable may yield more than a smaller, lower-grade one.
- **Supplier Specifications:** Different suppliers may provide products with different levels of initial trim.
- **Skill of the Cook:** A skilled butcher or prep cook will produce less waste and a higher yield.
- **Intended Use:** Dicing an onion will have a different yield than slicing it for onion rings.

Because of this variability, most professional kitchens develop their own yield standards by consistently performing tests on their regularly purchased items. A typical food cost target for a restaurant is **28-35%**, and accurate yield data is critical to achieving this goal.
