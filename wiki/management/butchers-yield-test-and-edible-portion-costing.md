# Butcher's Yield Test

## Overview
A [[Butcher's Yield Test]] is a systematic process used to determine the actual usable amount, or [[Edible Portion (EP)]], of a product after it has been fabricated from its raw, [[As-Purchased (AP)]] state. This is especially critical for meat, poultry, and fish that require trimming, deboning, or portioning. The test reveals the true cost of the servable product by accounting for trim and waste, which is essential for accurate [[menu-pricing]], effective [[purchasing]] decisions, evaluating supplier quality, and as a training tool for kitchen staff to ensure consistency and minimize waste.

## Key Concepts
- **As-Purchased (AP) Weight:** The total weight of a product as it is received from the supplier, before any trimming or fabrication.
- **Edible Portion (EP) Weight:** The weight of the product after it has been cleaned, trimmed, and portioned, ready for cooking and serving. This is the "servable" yield.
- **Yield Percentage:** The percentage of the AP weight that remains as the EP weight after fabrication. A lower yield percentage means a higher cost for the final edible product.
- **Trim:** The material removed from the AP product during fabrication, such as fat, bones, sinew, or skin. Trim can be categorized as:
    - **Usable Trim:** Parts that can be repurposed in other preparations, such as bones for [[stock]], fat for [[rendering]], or meat scraps for [[ground meat]].
    - **Waste:** Parts with no further use in the kitchen that must be discarded.
- **PSMO (Peeled, Side Muscle On):** An industry-specific term for a cut of [[beef tenderloin]] that has been peeled of surface fat but retains the side muscle. This is an example of a specific AP trim level that will have a different yield than a fully trimmed tenderloin.

## Calculations & Formulas

### 1. Calculating Yield Percentage

This formula determines what percentage of the initial product is servable after fabrication.

**Formula:**
`Edible Portion (EP) Weight / As-Purchased (AP) Weight = Yield Percentage (%)`

**Example:**
You purchase a whole [[beef tenderloin]] with an AP weight of 8 lbs. After trimming and portioning [[filet mignons]], the total weight of the steaks (EP weight) is 6 lbs.
- `6 lbs (EP) / 8 lbs (AP) = 0.75`
- **Yield Percentage = 75%**

### 2. Calculating Edible Portion (EP) Cost

This calculation reveals the true cost per pound (or ounce) of the final, servable product.

**Formula:**
`As-Purchased (AP) Cost per Pound / Yield Percentage = Edible Portion (EP) Cost per Pound`

**Example:**
The 8 lb tenderloin from the previous example was purchased at $18.00/lb. The AP cost is `8 lbs * $18.00/lb = $144.00`. The yield is 75%.
- `$18.00 (AP Cost/lb) / 0.75 (Yield %) = $24.00`
- **EP Cost = $24.00 per pound**

### 3. Calculating AP Quantity to Purchase

This formula is used to determine how much of a raw product to order to meet a specific menu demand.

**Formula:**
`Required Edible Portion Quantity (EPQ) / Yield Percentage = As-Purchased Quantity (APQ) to Order`

**Example:**
You need 80 portions of a 6 oz fish fillet for an event. Through yield testing, you know the whole fish has a 70% yield.
1.  **Calculate Total EPQ Needed:**
    `80 portions * 6 oz/portion = 480 oz`
2.  **Convert EPQ to Pounds:**
    `480 oz / 16 oz/lb = 30 lbs`
3.  **Calculate APQ to Order:**
    `30 lbs (EPQ) / 0.70 (Yield %) = 42.86 lbs`
- **You must order at least 43 lbs (rounding up) of the whole fish to get the 30 lbs of fillets you need.**

## Implementation

1.  **Establish a Baseline:** Select a specific product from a supplier (e.g., 1079 Beef Ribeye, PSMO Tenderloin, Whole Salmon).
2.  **Weigh the AP Product:** Before any work begins, weigh the item on a calibrated scale. Record this as the **AP Weight**.
3.  **Fabricate the Item:** Process the product exactly as you would for service. Trim, debone, and portion the item according to your restaurant's specifications.
4.  **Separate and Weigh Components:**
    - Weigh all the final, servable portions. Record this as the **EP Weight**.
    - Weigh all the usable trim (bones, fat, etc.).
    - Weigh all the unusable waste.
    - *Verification: The sum of EP Weight + Usable Trim + Waste should equal the original AP Weight.*
5.  **Calculate and Record:** Use the formulas above to calculate the Yield Percentage and the EP Cost per unit.
6.  **Analyze and Repeat:** Keep a detailed log of all yield tests. Use this data to compare suppliers, track the consistency of your butchers, make informed purchasing decisions (e.g., pre-portioned vs. whole), and accurately cost menu items.

## Industry Standards
There are no universal yield standards, as yields vary dramatically based on:
- **The specific product:** A whole chicken will have a different yield than a boneless pork loin.
- **The grade and quality:** A [[USDA Prime]] cut may have more marbling and a different trim yield than a [[USDA Choice]] cut.
- **The AP trim level:** A PSMO tenderloin will have a higher yield than an untrimmed tenderloin.
- **The skill of the butcher:** An experienced butcher will maximize the EP yield and minimize waste.

The industry standard is for an operation to perform its own yield tests to establish internal benchmarks for its specific products and recipes. This data is crucial for maintaining consistent [[food-cost]].
