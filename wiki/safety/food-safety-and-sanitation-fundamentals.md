# Foodservice Sanitation

## Overview
[[Foodservice sanitation]] encompasses the [[principles]] and [[practices]] required to provide safe [[food]] and maintain a [[clean environment]] in a professional [[kitchen]]. Proper [[sanitation]] is critical for preventing [[foodborne illness]], which can be dangerous for [[customers]] and financially devastating for a [[business]]. It requires comprehensive [[knowledge]] of [[food hazards]], safe [[food handling]] and [[storage]] procedures, and diligent [[personal hygiene]] to minimize and eliminate potential [[risks]] of [[contamination]].

## Key Hazards
There are three primary categories of [[food hazards]] that can occur in a [[foodservice operation]]:
- **[[Biological Hazards]]:** [[Microorganisms]] that can cause [[foodborne illness]]. These are the most common [[threats]] to [[food safety]].
    - **[[Bacteria]]:** Single-celled [[organisms]] that can [[multiply]] rapidly in [[TCS Foods]] under the right [[conditions]]. Some are [[infectious]], while others produce harmful [[toxins]].
    - **[[Viruses]]:** Small [[life forms]] that require a [[living host]] to [[multiply]] but can survive on [[surfaces]] and use [[food]] as a [[vehicle]] for [[transmission]] (e.g., [[Hepatitis A]], [[Norovirus]]).
    - **[[Parasites]]:** [[Organisms]] that require a [[host]] to survive, such as [[Trichinella Spiralis]] in [[pork]] or [[Anisakis]] roundworms in [[fish]].
    - **[[Fungi]]:** Includes [[molds]] and [[yeasts]]. While some are [[beneficial]], others cause [[food spoilage]] or produce dangerous [[toxins]].
- **[[Chemical Hazards]]:** [[Contamination]] from [[chemical substances]], such as [[cleaning solutions]], [[sanitizers]], or [[pesticides]].
- **[[Physical Hazards]]:** [[Contamination]] by [[foreign objects]], such as [[glass shards]], [[metal shavings]], or [[plastic fragments]].

## Prevention & Controls
The most common [[factors]] contributing to [[foodborne illness]] [[outbreaks]] can be managed through specific [[controls]]:
- **[[Sourcing]] from [[Approved Suppliers]]:** Only [[purchase]] [[food]] from reputable [[suppliers]] who meet [[state]] and [[federal inspection standards]]. Never use "[[homemade]]" or [[uninspected]] [[food products]] in a [[commercial kitchen]].
- **Practicing Proper [[Personal Hygiene]]:**
    - Implement a strict [[handwashing]] policy, especially after using the [[restroom]], before starting [[work]], and after handling [[raw meat]], [[poultry]], or [[fish]].
    - Prohibit [[sick employees]] from handling [[food]].
    - Ensure [[employees]] avoid [[coughing]] or [[sneezing]] on [[food]] and do not touch [[wounds]] while working.
- **[[Time and Temperature Control]]:**
    - Identify and properly [[handle]] all [[TCS Foods]] ([[Time/Temperature Control for Safety]]), including [[dairy]], [[meat]], [[poultry]], [[fish]], [[eggs]], and [[cooked vegetables]].
    - [[Cook]] [[foods]] to their required minimum [[internal temperatures]] to kill [[pathogens]].
    - [[Hold]] [[hot foods]] hot and [[cold foods]] cold, outside of the [[Temperature Danger Zone]].
    - [[Cool]] [[hot foods]] rapidly using [[approved methods]].
    - [[Reheat]] [[foods]] to the correct [[temperature]] quickly.
- **Preventing [[Cross-Contamination]]:**
    - Separate [[raw animal products]] from [[ready-to-eat foods]] during [[storage]] and [[preparation]].
    - Use separate [[equipment]], such as [[color-coded]] [[cutting boards]], for different [[food types]] (e.g., [[red]] for [[raw meat]], [[green]] for [[vegetables]]).
    - [[Clean]] and [[sanitize]] all [[work surfaces]], [[tools]], and [[smallwares]] between [[tasks]].
- **Controlling [[Conditions for Pathogen Growth]] ([[FAT TOM]]):**
    - **[[Food]]:** Limit access to [[nutrient-rich]] [[TCS Foods]].
    - **[[Acidity]]:** Be aware that most [[bacteria]] thrive in a [[neutral]] [[pH]] [[environment]] and will not [[grow]] in highly [[acidic conditions]] (below 4.6 [[pH]]).
    - **[[Temperature]]:** Keep [[food]] out of the [[Temperature Danger Zone]] (41°F to 135°F or 5°C to 57°C).
    - **[[Time]]:** Minimize the [[time]] [[TCS Foods]] spend in the [[danger zone]].
    - **[[Oxygen]]:** Control [[oxygen levels]] where appropriate (e.g., [[sous vide]] [[packaging]]).
    - **[[Moisture]]:** Be aware that [[pathogens]] need [[moisture]] to [[grow]].

## Regulations & Standards
[[Professional kitchens]] must adhere to local [[health department regulations]], which are typically based on the **[[FDA Food Code]]**. These [[codes]] provide the [[legal framework]] for [[food safety]], including [[requirements]] for [[personal hygiene]], [[time/temperature control]], and preventing [[contamination]]. Systems like **[[HACCP]] ([[Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points]])** provide a [[systematic approach]] to identifying and controlling [[hazards]]. [[Certifications]] like **[[ServSafe]]** are an [[industry standard]] for [[food safety training]].

## Best Practices
- Implement a [[color-coded]] [[cutting board]] [[system]] to prevent [[cross-contamination]]:
    - **Red:** [[Raw meat]]
    - **Yellow:** [[Raw poultry]]
    - **Blue:** [[Raw fish]]/[[seafood]]
    - **Green:** [[Vegetables]] and [[fruits]]
- Use the [[FAT TOM]] [[acronym]] as a regular [[training tool]] for all [[kitchen staff]] to reinforce the [[conditions]] required for [[pathogen growth]].
- Maintain [[logs]] for [[temperature checks]] of [[refrigerators]], [[freezers]], and [[hot-holding equipment]].
- Establish and enforce a clear "when to wash" [[policy]] for [[handwashing]].
- Develop a [[master cleaning schedule]] to ensure all [[equipment]] and [[surfaces]] are regularly [[cleaned]] and [[sanitized]].
