## Overview
[[Biological Hazards]] such as [[bacteria]], [[viruses]], [[parasites]], and [[fungi]] are the primary cause of [[foodborne illnesses]]. These [[pathogens]] or the [[toxins]] they produce can contaminate food, often without visible signs, leading to significant health risks. Understanding these microorganisms and their modes of transmission is fundamental to ensuring [[food safety]] in any culinary environment.

## Types of Foodborne Pathogens
*   **[[Bacteria]]**: Single-celled organisms, bacteria are the most common foodborne hazard. While some are beneficial (e.g., in [[fermenting]] [[pickles]]), many are infectious, multiplying rapidly and sometimes producing toxins that cause [[infectious disease]].
*   **[[Viruses]]**: Simple life forms that require a living host but can survive on inanimate objects like [[foodservice equipment]]. They are resistant to temperature extremes and use food as a transport mechanism. Examples include [[Hepatitis A]] and [[Norovirus]].
*   **[[Parasites]]**: Organisms that need a host to survive. Notable examples include [[Trichinella Spiralis]] (causing [[Trichinosis]]) found in [[pork]], and [[Anisakis]] ([[roundworms]]) found in [[fish]]. Proper [[cooking]] can eliminate some parasites.
*   **[[Fungi]]**: This category includes [[molds]] and [[yeasts]]. While some are beneficial (like yeast in [[bread]]), others cause [[food spoilage]] and can produce toxic effects.

## Common Causes of Foodborne Illnesses
[[Foodborne illnesses]] typically stem from a combination of factors, with the most common being [[receiving food from unsafe sources]], [[time and temperature abuse]], [[poor personal hygiene]], [[contaminated equipment]], and [[cross-contamination]].
*   **Receiving Food from Unsafe Food Sources**: Contamination can occur at any point in the [[supply chain]]. It is crucial to use [[approved food suppliers]] who meet [[food processing]] and [[inspection standards]]. "Homemade" foods are generally not permitted in [[commercial foodservice operations]].
*   **Poor Personal Hygiene**: Employees failing to practice good [[handwashing]] after using the bathroom, handling [[potentially hazardous foods]] (such as [[raw meats]], [[poultry]], and [[fish]]) without washing hands, working while sick, or touching [[wounds]] can spread pathogens.
*   **Time and Temperature Abuse**: This is critical for [[TCS Foods]] (Time/Temperature Control for Safety). These include [[milk]] and [[dairy products]], [[meats]], [[poultry]], [[fish]], [[shellfish]], [[eggs]], [[baked potatoes]], [[cooked rice]], [[beans]], [[vegetables]], [[tofu]], [[sprouted seeds]], [[sliced melons]], [[diced tomatoes]], and [[garlic-and-oil mixtures]]. Failure to cook to proper doneness, hold at safe temperatures, or adequately [[reheated]] foods promotes [[foodborne pathogens]] growth.
*   **Cross-Contamination**: Occurs when pathogens transfer from one food or surface to another. Examples include mixing improperly cooked foods, [[TCS foods]] (like [[raw meats]], [[poultry]], [[fish]]) contaminating [[ready-to-eat foods]] like [[salads]], using unsanitized equipment for different food types (e.g., raw meat and [[lettuces]]), or using contaminated towels.
*   **Contaminated Equipment**: Surfaces, [[tools]], and [[small wares]] that are not properly [[cleaned]] and [[sanitized]] can transfer pathogens to food. Regular [[cleaning]] and [[sanitizing]] of [[work surfaces]] are essential.

## Prevention Principles
Effective prevention of [[foodborne illnesses]] relies on strict adherence to [[food safety]] protocols. Key principles include:
*   **Source Control**: Always procure ingredients from [[approved food suppliers]] to ensure the integrity of the [[supply chain]].
*   **Personal Hygiene**: Implement rigorous [[handwashing]] policies and ensure all staff understand the importance of hygiene, especially when handling [[potentially hazardous foods]] or after using the restroom.
*   **Time and Temperature Control**: Maintain [[TCS Foods]] within safe temperature ranges. Ensure proper [[cooking]] temperatures are reached and foods are adequately [[reheated]]. Avoid leaving foods in the [[Temperature Danger Zone]].
*   **Preventing Cross-Contamination**: Use separate cutting boards and [[tools]] for [[raw meats]] and [[ready-to-eat foods]]. Thoroughly [[clean]] and [[sanitized]] all [[work surfaces]] and [[equipment]] between uses.
*   **Equipment Sanitation**: Regularly [[clean]] and [[sanitize]] all [[food preparation surfaces]], [[tools]], and [[small wares]] to prevent the growth and spread of [[pathogens]].
