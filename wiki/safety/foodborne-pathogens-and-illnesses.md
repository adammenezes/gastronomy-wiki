# Foodborne Pathogens

## Overview
Foodborne pathogens are biological hazards, including bacteria, viruses, parasites, and fungi, that can contaminate food and cause foodborne illness. These microorganisms, or the toxins they produce, are a primary cause of food safety incidents in professional kitchens. Since many pathogens cannot be seen, smelled, or tasted, it is critical for culinary professionals to understand the risks they pose and the control measures required to prevent contamination and illness.

## Key Hazards
- **Bacteria:** Single-celled organisms that are the most common foodborne hazard. Pathogenic bacteria can multiply rapidly under the right conditions (see [[FATTOM]]) and may cause infection or produce toxins that lead to illness.
- **Viruses:** Small life forms that require a living host to reproduce but can survive on food and equipment, using them as a vehicle for transmission. Common foodborne viruses include [[Hepatitis A]] and [[Norovirus]]. They are often resistant to extreme heat and cold.
- **Parasites:** Organisms that require a host to live and reproduce. Examples include *Trichinella spiralis* in [[pork]] and *Anisakis* roundworms in [[fish]]. Proper [[cooking]] is a primary control for killing parasites.
- **Fungi:** Includes molds and yeasts. While some are beneficial in food production (e.g., yeast for [[bread]], mold for cheese), others cause food spoilage and can produce harmful toxins.

## Prevention & Controls
The most common failures leading to foodborne illness can be prevented by implementing the following controls:
- **Source from Approved Suppliers:** Only purchase food from reputable suppliers who meet state and federal food processing and inspection standards. Never use "homemade" or uninspected foods in a commercial operation.
- **Maintain Strict Personal Hygiene:** Implement and enforce a rigorous [[personal hygiene]] program, including proper handwashing procedures, especially after using the restroom or handling raw meat. Staff must not work while sick.
- **Practice Time and Temperature Control:** Foods designated as [[TCS Foods]] (Time/Temperature Control for Safety) must be kept out of the [[Temperature Danger Zone]] (41°F to 135°F / 5°C to 57°C). This includes proper [[cooking]], holding, cooling, and [[reheating]] procedures.
- **Prevent Cross-Contamination:** [[Cross-contamination]] occurs when pathogens are transferred from one surface or food to another. Prevent it by separating raw and ready-to-eat foods, using separate equipment (e.g., color-coded cutting boards), and ensuring proper sanitation between tasks.
- **Ensure Proper Cleaning and Sanitizing:** All food-contact surfaces, equipment, tools, and smallwares must be thoroughly cleaned and sanitized on a regular schedule. Train staff on correct procedures for washing and sanitizing.

## Regulations & Standards
- **FDA Food Code:** Provides guidance for food safety regulations in the United States. Local health codes are often based on this model.
- **HACCP (Hazard Analysis Critical Control Point):** A systematic approach to identifying, evaluating, and controlling food safety hazards. Many operations are required to have a [[HACCP]] plan.
- **ServSafe:** A food and beverage safety training and certificate program administered by the U.S. National Restaurant Association.

## Best Practices
- Implement a strict handwashing policy and provide accessible, well-stocked handwashing stations.
- Use color-coded [[cutting boards]] and utensils for different food categories (e.g., red for raw meat, green for produce) to prevent [[cross-contamination]].
- Maintain temperature logs for all refrigerators, freezers, and hot-holding units to ensure equipment is functioning correctly.
- Train all staff to identify [[TCS Foods]] and understand the specific handling requirements for each.
- Develop and follow a Master Cleaning Schedule for all equipment and surfaces.
