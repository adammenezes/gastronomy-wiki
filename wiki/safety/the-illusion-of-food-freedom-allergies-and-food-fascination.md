# Food Allergies

## Overview
A food allergy is an immune system reaction that occurs soon after eating a certain food. For guests with severe allergies, the need to control their food intake is not a lifestyle choice but a medical necessity. In a professional kitchen, understanding and managing food allergies is a critical responsibility. Failure to do so can lead to severe health consequences for the guest, including life-threatening [[anaphylaxis]], and significant legal and reputational risk for the establishment. Meticulous control over ingredients and processes is paramount to ensure the safety of all diners.

## Key Hazards
*   **Allergen Exposure:** The primary hazard is the ingestion of an allergenic protein by a sensitive individual.
*   **[[Cross-Contamination]]:** The unintentional transfer of an allergen from one food, surface, or piece of [[equipment]] to another. This is a major risk in shared kitchen spaces.
*   **Anaphylaxis:** A severe, potentially fatal allergic reaction that can occur within seconds or minutes of exposure.
*   **Misinformation:** Inaccurate menu descriptions, ingredient lists, or communication between Front of House and Back of House staff, leading to accidental exposure.

## Prevention & Controls
*   **Ingredient Verification:** Always know the exact ingredients in all products received from suppliers. Maintain a binder or digital record of specification sheets.
*   **Strict Segregation:**
    *   Store allergenic ingredients separately, in sealed and clearly labeled containers, preferably on lower shelves to prevent spills onto other foods.
    *   Prepare allergen-free meals in a designated, sanitized area.
    *   Use dedicated, color-coded [[equipment]] (e.g., purple for allergens) such as [[cutting boards]], [[knives]], and [[tongs]].
*   **Sanitation:** Thoroughly clean and sanitize all food-contact surfaces, including prep tables, [[cookware]], and utensils, before preparing an allergen-free order.
*   **Communication Protocol:** Implement a clear, documented system for communicating allergy information from the server to the kitchen. Use designated markers on tickets (e.g., "ALLERGY ALERT") and have a manager or lead [[chef]] oversee the order.
*   **Personal Hygiene:** Staff must wash hands and change gloves after handling a known allergen and before preparing an allergen-free dish.

## Regulations & Standards
*   **FDA Food Code:** Identifies the most common food allergens. As of 2023, the "Big 9" in the U.S. are: milk, eggs, fish, crustacean shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans, and sesame.
*   **Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act (FALCPA):** Requires food labels to clearly identify the food source of any ingredients that are—or contain protein from—a major food allergen.
*   **[[HACCP]] Plans:** Allergen control should be integrated into a facility's [[HACCP]] plan, identifying allergens as a chemical hazard and establishing critical control points for their management (e.g., receiving, storage, preparation).

## Best Practices
*   **"When in doubt, throw it out":** If there is any uncertainty about whether a dish has been contaminated with an allergen, do not serve it. Remake the dish following all safety protocols.
*   **Allergen Matrix:** Maintain a comprehensive chart or database that lists every menu item and indicates which of the major allergens it contains. This should be readily accessible to both FOH and BOH staff.
*   **Staff Training:** Regularly train all staff on the "Big 9" allergens, the symptoms of an allergic reaction, protocols for handling allergy orders, and the emergency response plan for anaphylaxis.
*   **Adopt "Food Fascination":** Cultivate a professional culture of deep interest in and respect for ingredients and their effects on the body. This mindset shifts allergy management from a burden to an integral part of culinary excellence and guest care.
