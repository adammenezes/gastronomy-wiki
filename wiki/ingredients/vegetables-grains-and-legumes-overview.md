## Overview
[[Gratin Dauphinoise]] is a classic [[French Cuisine]] dish originating from the [[Dauphiné]] region in southeastern [[France]]. It features thinly sliced [[Potato]]es baked in [[Heavy Cream]] or milk, often infused with [[Garlic]] and [[Nutmeg]], until tender and golden brown. Unlike many gratins, traditional Gratin Dauphinoise does not include [[Cheese]], though modern variations often do. It's celebrated for its comforting richness and simple elegance, showcasing the humble potato in a luxurious way.

## Ingredients
*   1.5 kg [[Russet Potato]]es or [[Yukon Gold Potato]]es, peeled
*   2 cloves [[Garlic]], peeled and halved
*   600 ml [[Heavy Cream]] (or a mix of cream and [[Whole Milk]])
*   1/2 tsp freshly grated [[Nutmeg]]
*   [[Kosher Salt]] to taste
*   Freshly ground [[Black Pepper]] to taste
*   1 tbsp [[Unsalted Butter]] for greasing
*   (Optional) 100g [[Gruyère Cheese]], grated, for a cheesier version

## Method
1.  **Prepare the Potatoes:** Preheat your oven to 180°C (350°F). Lightly grease a 2-liter [[Baking Dish]] with [[Unsalted Butter]]. Using a [[Mandoline Slicer]] or a sharp [[Chef's Knife]], thinly slice the [[Potato]]es into uniform rounds, about 2-3mm thick. Immediately place them in a large bowl of cold water to prevent [[Oxidation]] and rinse off excess [[Starch]]. Drain thoroughly and pat dry with a clean kitchen towel.
2.  **Infuse the Cream:** In a [[Saucepan]], combine the [[Heavy Cream]] (or cream/milk mixture), halved [[Garlic]] cloves, [[Nutmeg]], a generous pinch of [[Kosher Salt]], and [[Black Pepper]]. Bring to a gentle [[Simmer]] over medium heat, then remove from heat and let it steep for 10-15 minutes to allow the garlic flavor to infuse. Remove the garlic cloves before proceeding.
3.  **Layer the Gratin:** Arrange a single layer of sliced [[Potato]]es in the prepared [[Baking Dish]], slightly overlapping. Season lightly with [[Kosher Salt]] and [[Black Pepper]]. Pour a small amount of the infused [[Heavy Cream]] over the potatoes, just enough to moisten them. Repeat the layering process, building up the gratin in even layers, seasoning each layer, and pouring cream over it, until all potatoes are used and the cream almost covers the top layer. If using [[Gruyère Cheese]], sprinkle some between layers or just on the top.
4.  **Bake:** Place the [[Baking Dish]] on a [[Baking Sheet]] (to catch any potential overflows) and bake for 45-60 minutes, or until the potatoes are tender when pierced with a knife and the top is golden brown and bubbling. If the top browns too quickly, loosely cover it with [[Aluminum Foil]].
5.  **Rest and Serve:** Once baked, remove the [[Gratin Dauphinoise]] from the oven and let it rest for at least 10-15 minutes before serving. This allows the cream to set and the flavors to meld. Serve hot as a side dish to roasted meats or poultry.

## Chef's Notes & Variations
*   **Potato Choice:** While [[Russet Potato]]es are common, [[Yukon Gold Potato]]es offer a creamier texture. Avoid waxy potatoes like [[Red Bliss]] as they may not absorb the cream as well.
*   **Preventing Sticking:** Ensure your [[Baking Dish]] is well-greased with [[Unsalted Butter]] or even a thin layer of [[Garlic]] paste to prevent the bottom layer of potatoes from sticking.
*   **Cream vs. Milk:** For a richer gratin, use all [[Heavy Cream]]. For a lighter version, a 50/50 mix of cream and [[Whole Milk]] works well. Some traditional recipes even use only milk.
*   **Flavor Infusion:** Experiment with other aromatics in the cream, such as a [[Bay Leaf]] or a sprig of [[Thyme]].
*   **Cheesy Variation:** While not traditional, adding [[Gruyère Cheese]], [[Comté Cheese]], or even [[Parmesan Cheese]] to the layers or on top creates a delicious variation, sometimes called [[Gratin Savoyard]] if cheese is prominent.
*   **Don't Overcrowd:** Ensure the potatoes are thinly and evenly sliced for consistent cooking. Overcrowding the dish can lead to unevenly cooked potatoes.
*   **Resting is Key:** Allowing the gratin to rest after baking is crucial. It helps the sauce thicken and prevents the dish from falling apart when served.
