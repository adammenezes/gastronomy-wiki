## Overview
Vegetable oils are [[Fat|fats]] refined from plant sources, including seeds, nuts, beans, or fruit. They are a foundational ingredient in global cuisine, essential for cooking and for creating cold sauces like [[Mayonnaise]] and [[Vinaigrette]].

Oils are extracted through several methods. The oldest is the [[Hydraulic Pressing]] method, a natural process used for centuries to produce [[Extra Virgin Olive Oil]] and considered true [[Cold Pressing]]. The [[Expeller Pressing]] method uses a mechanical press, generating some heat from friction but still qualifying as cold-pressed. The most efficient and common industrial method is [[Chemical Extraction]], which uses petroleum-based solvents to recover up to 99% of the oil from plant matter, as is the case with most [[Soybean Oil]]. These oils are then highly refined.

The term "cold-pressed" generally means the oil's temperature never exceeded 120°F (49°C) during extraction, though standards can vary; the [[International Olive Council]] mandates a temperature no higher than 86°F (30°C) for true extra virgin olive oil.

### Olive Oil
[[Olive Oil]] holds a unique status among vegetable oils, with a history dating back thousands of years in the [[Mediterranean]] and Middle East, originating in regions like [[Crete]], [[Syria]], and [[Egypt]]. Today, [[Spain]], [[Italy]], and [[Greece]] are the largest producers. Similar to [[Winemaking]], its quality is influenced by climate, cultivation, and [[Terroir]]. Key taste factors include fruitiness, pungency, and bitterness. The [[International Olive Council]] recognizes ten categories based on production, purity, and oleic acid levels. High-quality bottles often display a harvest date.

-   **Extra Virgin Olive Oil:** The highest quality, from the first mechanical pressing without heat or chemicals. It has a bright, fruity taste when young.
-   **Virgin Olive Oil:** Characterized by a slightly higher oleic acid level.
-   **Olive Oil:** A blend of virgin and refined oils.
-   **Olive Pomace Oil:** A lower grade produced via [[Chemical Extraction]] and blended with other oils.

## Flavour & Texture
The sensory profile of a vegetable oil is determined by its processing. [[Unrefined Oil|Unrefined oils]] are simply filtered and bottled, retaining more nutrients, flavour, and colour. They can have grassy, nutty, or fruity notes but spoil more quickly. [[Refined Oil|Refined oils]] are heated, deodorized, and bleached, a process that strips out flavour and nutrients, resulting in a bland, neutral-tasting oil with a longer shelf life.

Through [[Hydrogenation]], liquid oils are converted into solid or semi-solid fats like [[Shortening]] and [[Margarine]], which provide texture in baked goods as a substitute for [[Butter]]. This process, however, can create unhealthy [[Trans Fat|trans-fatty acids]].

All oils contain a mix of fatty acids:
*   **Saturated Fat:** Predominant in animal fats like [[Lard]] and [[Butter]], but also high in some vegetable oils like [[Coconut Oil]], [[Cottonseed Oil]], and [[Palm Kernel Oil]].
*   **Monounsaturated Fat:** Typically liquid at room temperature and solid when chilled. Examples include [[Olive Oil]], [[Sunflower Oil]], [[Canola Oil]], [[Grapeseed Oil]], [[Peanut Oil]], [[Sesame Oil]], [[Almond Oil]], and [[Avocado Oil]].
*   **Polyunsaturated Fat:** Liquid at both room and cool temperatures, containing beneficial [[Omega-3 Fatty Acid|Omega-3]] and [[Omega-6 Fatty Acid|Omega-6]] fats. Sources include [[Soybean Oil]], [[Corn Oil]], [[Safflower Oil]], as well as fatty fish like [[Salmon]] and [[Mackerel]].

## How to Buy & Store
When selecting high-quality oils like [[Extra Virgin Olive Oil]], look for a harvest date on the label to ensure freshness.

All oils are perishable and will spoil with age or improper storage, a process known as [[Rancidity]]. A rancid oil will have a strong, unpleasant aroma and taste and should be discarded. To maximize shelf life, store oils in a tightly sealed container in a cool, dark place away from heat and light.

## How to Use
Matching the oil to the cooking method is critical, primarily determined by the oil's [[Smoke Point]]—the temperature at which it begins to break down and smoke. [[Refined Oil|Refined oils]] generally have a higher smoke point, making them suitable for high-heat applications like [[Frying]].

Delicate, flavourful [[Unrefined Oil|unrefined oils]] like [[Extra Virgin Olive Oil]] have a lower smoke point. Using them for high-heat cooking destroys their nuanced flavours and is a waste of a premium product. These oils are best used in cold preparations, such as in a [[Vinaigrette]], or as a [[Finishing Oil]] drizzled over a dish just before serving.

## Substitutions
In many applications, particularly baking, other fats can be used in place of liquid vegetable oil to alter texture and flavour.
*   **[[Butter]]**: Provides a rich flavour and can help with leavening in baked goods.
*   **[[Lard]]**: An animal fat that creates exceptionally flaky pastries and crusts.
*   **[[Shortening]]**: A solid, hydrogenated vegetable oil that yields a tender, crumbly texture in baked goods.
*   **[[Margarine]]**: A butter substitute made from vegetable oil that functions similarly to shortening.
