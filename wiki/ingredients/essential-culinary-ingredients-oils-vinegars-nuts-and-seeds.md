## Overview
[[Vegetable Oils]] are essential fats refined from the seeds, nuts, beans, or fruit of various plants. They are extracted either mechanically or chemically and can be further categorized as refined or unrefined, and by their fatty acid composition: polyunsaturated, monounsaturated, or hydrogenated. These versatile oils are fundamental in countless culinary preparations, from enriching cold sauces like [[Mayonnaise]] and [[Vinaigrette]] to facilitating high-heat [[Frying]].

Extraction methods vary significantly. The ancient [[Hydraulic Press Method]] is a traditional and natural approach, recognized as true [[Cold Pressing]], famously used for [[Extra Virgin Olive Oil]]. The [[Expeller Method]] employs a mechanical press, generating some heat (up to 120˚F or 49˚C) but still qualifying as [[Cold Pressing]]. In contrast, [[Chemical Extraction]] utilizes petroleum-based solvents and heat, recovering up to 99% of the oil, making it the most efficient but also the most environmentally impactful method. Much of the [[Soybean Oil]] on the market is produced this way. For [[Cold-Pressed Oils]], the temperature during processing generally must not exceed 120˚F (49˚C), though the [[International Olive Council]] specifies a stricter maximum of 86°F (30°C) for true cold-pressed [[Extra Virgin Olive Oil]].

## Flavour & Texture
The sensory profile of [[Vegetable Oils]] is largely determined by their processing. [[Unrefined Oils]] retain more of their natural flavor, color, and nutrients, often exhibiting fruity, nutty, or herbaceous notes, along with visible impurities. They are generally more aromatic and vibrant. Conversely, [[Refined Oils]] undergo heating (up to 450˚F or 225˚C), deodorization, and bleaching, which strips away unwanted odors, colors, and, unfortunately, much of their inherent flavor and nutrients, resulting in a bland, neutral-tasting product.

[[Hydrogenation]] transforms liquid oils into solid or semi-solid fats, such as [[Shortening]] and [[Margarine]], which are prized in [[Baking]] for their ability to provide texture and act as a substitute for [[Butter]]. However, [[Hydrogenated Oils]] can contain [[Trans-Fatty Acids]], which are known to elevate bad [[Cholesterol]]. All fats and oils contain varying levels of [[Saturated Fats]], [[Monounsaturated Fats]], and [[Polyunsaturated Fats]]. [[Animal Fats]] like [[Lard]], [[Butter]], [[Cream]], and [[Cheese]], along with some [[Vegetable Oils]] such as [[Coconut Oil]], [[Cottonseed Oil]], and [[Palm Kernel Oil]], are high in [[Saturated Fats]]. [[Monounsaturated Oils]], including [[Olive Oil]], [[Sunflower Oil]], [[Canola Oil]], [[Grapeseed Oil]], [[Peanut Oil]], [[Sesame Oil]], [[Almond Oil]], and [[Avocado Oil]], are typically liquid at room temperature. [[Polyunsaturated Fats]], found in [[Soybean Oil]], [[Corn Oil]], [[Safflower Oil]], [[Flaxseed]], [[Walnuts]], and [[Sunflower Seeds]], as well as fatty fish like [[Salmon]], [[Mackerel]], [[Herring]], and [[Trout]], are considered healthier due to their [[Omega-3 Fats]] and [[Omega-6 Fats]] content. A strong, off-putting aroma and taste indicate that an oil has turned [[Rancid]] and should be discarded.

## How to Buy & Store
When purchasing [[Vegetable Oils]], especially specialty varieties like [[Olive Oil]], look for quality indicators such as a harvest date on the label. For [[Extra Virgin Olive Oil]], adherence to standards set by bodies like the [[International Olive Council]] can signify superior quality.

Proper storage is crucial to prevent oils from spoiling. All oils should be kept in appropriately sealed containers in a cool, dark place, away from light and heat, which can accelerate degradation. If an oil develops a strong, unpleasant aroma or taste, it has likely become [[Rancid]] and should be discarded. While [[Unrefined Oils]] offer more flavor and nutrients, they generally have a shorter shelf life and lower [[Smoke Point]] compared to their refined counterparts.

## How to Use
The culinary application of [[Vegetable Oils]] is diverse, but understanding their interaction with heat is paramount. [[Refined Oils]] typically possess a higher [[Smoke Point]] than [[Unrefined Oils]] or minimally processed varieties, making them suitable for high-heat [[Frying]] and [[Sautéing]]. Conversely, delicate oils like [[Extra Virgin Olive Oil]] have a lower [[Smoke Point]]; using them for high-heat [[Cooking]] can destroy their nuanced flavor and nutritional benefits, making them better suited for [[Cold Preparations]] like dressings or as a finishing garnish.

[[Hydrogenation]] is a process that converts liquid [[Vegetable Oils]] into solid or semi-solid fats such as [[Shortening]] and [[Margarine]]. These [[Hydrogenated Oils]] are frequently employed in [[Baking]] as a substitute for [[Butter]], providing specific textures and stability to baked goods. Matching the oil to the intended culinary preparation is vital to ensure both optimal flavor and health outcomes.

## Substitutions
The choice of substitute for a [[Vegetable Oil]] depends heavily on the desired flavor profile, [[Smoke Point]], and intended application.
*   For general-purpose [[Frying]] or [[Baking]], many neutral-flavored oils can be interchanged: [[Soybean Oil]], [[Corn Oil]], [[Safflower Oil]], [[Sunflower Oil]], [[Canola Oil]], [[Grapeseed Oil]], [[Peanut Oil]], [[Sesame Oil]], [[Almond Oil]], or [[Avocado Oil]].
*   In [[Baking]], [[Hydrogenated Oils]] like [[Shortening]] or [[Margarine]] can substitute for [[Butter]], offering different textures and shelf stability.
*   For dressings or finishing, [[Olive Oil]] (especially [[Extra Virgin Olive Oil]]) can be used for its distinct fruity and peppery notes, though its lower [[Smoke Point]] makes it unsuitable for high-heat cooking.
