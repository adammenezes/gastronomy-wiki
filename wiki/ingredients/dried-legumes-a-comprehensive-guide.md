## Overview
[[Dried Legumes]] encompass a wide variety of [[Beans]], [[Peas]], [[Lentils]], and [[Peanuts]] that grow in pods. While many can be enjoyed fresh, they are frequently dried for extended storage and convenience. These versatile ingredients are fundamental in [[Soups]], [[Stews]], side dishes, and [[Salads]], typically requiring rehydration through cooking in liquids. Globally, [[Dried Legumes]] constitute approximately 25% of farmed plant production. They are a highly valued and economical source of [[Protein]], [[Iron]], [[Potassium]], [[Magnesium]], and [[Vitamin B]], while being low in fat and calories, making them a dietary staple across diverse cultures worldwide.

## Flavour & Texture
The inherent flavour profile of [[Dried Legumes]] is often described as [[Starchy]] and earthy, providing a satisfying base for many dishes. When properly cooked, they achieve a tender, creamy texture. This [[Starchy]] quality can be brightened significantly by finishing dishes with a touch of [[Acidity]], such as [[Lemon Juice]] or various [[Vinegar]]s. Their mild flavour also makes them excellent vehicles for absorbing the rich tastes of accompanying ingredients like [[Pancetta]], [[Bacon]], [[Salt Pork]], or [[Ham Hock]]. For vegetarian preparations, [[Olive Oil]] and [[Vegetable Stock]] provide a suitable foundation. Aromatic additions like [[Onions]], [[Celery]], [[Carrots]], [[Garlic]], [[Bay Leaves]], [[Thyme]], [[Parsley]], [[Rosemary]], [[Crushed Peppercorns]], [[Red Chili Flakes]], [[Cumin]], [[Ginger]], [[Green Chili Peppers]], and [[Curry Powder]] can transform their character. Other flavour enhancers include [[Soy Sauce]], [[Kombu]], [[Lemongrass]], [[Tomatoes]], [[Spanish Chorizo]], [[Allspice]], and [[Collard Greens]], each contributing to a complex and deeply satisfying culinary experience.

## How to Buy & Store
When purchasing [[Dried Legumes]], look for whole, unbroken pieces free from debris or discoloration. Before cooking, they should always be thoroughly [[Rinsing|rinsed]] and [[Sorting|sorted]] to remove any small stones or imperfect legumes. Quality indicators include a uniform appearance and a lack of dust. Older legumes tend to be drier and may require longer cooking times, so while they don't spoil quickly, fresher stock is often preferable. For storage, [[Dried Legumes]] will maintain their quality for up to one year when kept in a cool, dry place within a [[Sealed Container]]. This prevents moisture absorption and protects against pests.

## How to Use
Proper preparation of [[Dried Legumes]] is key to their texture and digestibility. While [[Peas]] and [[Lentils]] can often be [[Simmering|simmered]] directly from their dry state, most [[Beans]] benefit from [[Soaking]] to reduce cooking time. There are two primary [[Soaking]] methods:
*   **Long Soak:** [[Rinsing|Rinse]] beans, place in a container with four times their volume of water, and add 1 Tb./15 grams of [[Salt]] per quart/liter. Soak for 4-12 hours at room temperature or refrigerated. Discard the soaking liquid and [[Rinsing|rinse]] beans before cooking. Soaking helps reduce the "gaseous nature" of beans by drawing out [[Lectins]] and [[Phytates]] and activating [[Enzymes]] that break down indigestible starches.
*   **Quick Soak:** [[Rinsing|Rinse]] beans, place in a heavy-bottomed pot with four times their volume of water. Bring to a [[Simmering|simmer]], then remove from heat, cover, and let sit for 1 hour. [[Rinsing|Rinse]] beans before cooking.

[[Pressure Cooking]] significantly reduces cooking time, often allowing beans to cook from dry in under an hour. Pre-soaked beans tend to hold their shape better when [[Pressure Cooking|pressure cooked]]. For 1 lb./450 g of beans, use 2 qt./2 liters of liquid, along with 1/2 lb./450 g of [[Mirepoix]] ([[Onions]], [[Celery]], [[Carrots]], and [[Garlic]]). A sachet of [[Bay Leaves]], [[Thyme]], [[Parsley]], and [[Crushed Peppercorns]] adds seasoning. To prevent foaming and valve clogging, add 1-2 Tb./15-30 g of [[Oil]] per lb./450 g of beans. Cook at 15 psi pressure for the recommended time.

Salting the soaking liquid helps beans retain their shape, though it can slightly slow the tenderization process. Flavor enhancers like a [[Ham Hock]], [[Bacon]], or [[Salt Pork]] can be added during cooking, contributing both [[Salt]] and depth. [[Acids]] (from [[Vinegar]], [[Tomato]], [[Wine]], or [[Fruit]]) and [[Sugar]] can prevent beans from softening, so these should be added towards the end of the cooking process. Finishing with a touch of [[Acidity]] like [[Lemon Juice]] or [[Sherry Vinegar]], [[Rice Vinegar]], or [[Wine Vinegar]] brightens the [[Starchy]] taste.

For soups, use 1-2 lbs./450-900 g of legumes per gallon/4 liters of liquid. For stews and salads, a 2:1 ratio of liquid to legumes is recommended. Legumes will double or triple in volume when rehydrated and cooked (e.g., 8 oz./240 g dry yields about 1 lb. 4 oz./550 g cooked).

**Cooking Tips:**
*   [[Salt]] the soaking liquid for beans, but adjust seasoning with [[Salt]] after they have tenderized in the cooking liquid.
*   [[Lentils]] and [[Peas]] generally do not require [[Soaking]].
*   [[Baking Soda]] (⅛ tsp. per cup of beans) can aid in tenderizing, but too much can impart a soapy taste.
*   [[Hard Water]] (high in calcium) can prevent beans from softening; using [[Distilled Water]] can resolve this.
*   **Red Bean Toxicity:** Raw or improperly cooked [[Red Beans]] (and to a lesser extent, [[White Beans]]) contain [[Phytohaemagglutinin]], a toxin that can cause nausea and vomiting. This toxin is easily deactivated by [[Boiling]] beans for at least ten minutes at 212°F/100°C. The [[USDA]] also recommends [[Soaking]] beans for at least 5 hours and discarding the soaking liquid.

**Braised Legume Dishes:**
*   **[[Tuscan Cannellini Beans]]**: Features [[Cannellini Beans]] braised with [[Mirepoix]], [[Pancetta]], [[Olive Oil]], [[Garlic]], [[Red Chili Flakes]], [[Pork Stock]] or [[Chicken Stock]], and a sachet of [[Bay Leaves]], [[Parsley]], [[Thyme]], [[Rosemary]], and [[Crushed Peppercorns]]. Finished with [[Kale]], [[Balsamic Vinegar]], and [[Parmesan Cheese]]. This dish is a staple of [[Italian Cuisine]].
*   **[[Indian Curried Dal]]**: Made with [[Red Lentils]], [[Coconut Oil]], [[Cumin]], [[Ginger]], [[Green Chili Peppers]], [[Garlic]], [[Curry Powder]], and [[Tomatoes]]. Often served with [[Lamb]], [[Rice]], and [[Raita]], representing [[Indian Cuisine]].
*   **[[Brazilian Feijoada]]**: A hearty stew with [[Black Beans]], [[Onions]], [[Garlic]], [[Bay Leaves]], [[Olive Oil]], [[Spanish Chorizo]], and [[Ham Hock]]. A cornerstone of [[Brazilian Cuisine]].
*   **[[Braised Black-Eyed Peas with Ham Hock and Collard Greens]]**: [[Black-Eyed Peas]] soaked overnight, then braised with [[Onions]], [[Allspice]], [[Red Chili Flakes]], [[Ham Hock]], and [[Chicken Stock]]. Served with sautéed [[Collard Greens]].

## Substitutions
While the specific characteristics of each type of [[Dried Legumes]] vary, some general substitutions can be made depending on the desired outcome:
*   [[Lentils]]: Can often be substituted for [[Peas]] or smaller [[Beans]] in soups and stews, especially when a quicker cooking time is desired as they typically do not require [[Soaking]].
*   [[Peas]]: Similar to [[Lentils]], [[Dried Peas]] can replace other small legumes for faster cooking, offering a slightly different texture and flavour.
*   [[Beans]]: Different varieties of [[Beans]] (e.g., [[Cannellini Beans]], [[Black Beans]], [[Black-Eyed Peas]]) can often be interchanged in recipes, though cooking times and final textures may vary. Consider the size and starchiness when making a substitution.
