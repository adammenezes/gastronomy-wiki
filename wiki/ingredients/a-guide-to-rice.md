## Overview
Upon harvesting, [[Rice]] is [[cleaned]] and [[milled]] to remove the outer [[husk]]. This initial [[process]] leaves the [[Bran]] intact, resulting in [[Brown Rice]]. The [[Bran]] contains beneficial [[nutrients]] but also contributes to quicker [[spoilage]]. [[Brown Rice]] can be further [[processed]] to remove the [[Germ]], yielding [[White Rice]]. Due to the [[loss]] of [[nutrients]] from [[Germ]] removal, [[White Rice]] is often [[Fortification|fortified]] with added [[vitamins]] and [[minerals]]. Both [[Brown Rice]] and [[White Rice]] are frequently [[polished]] to achieve a [[smooth]] appearance and [[shine]]. A [[process]] called [[Parboiling]] involves [[soaking]], [[steaming]], and [[drying]] [[Rice]] before [[hulling]], which gives it a [[golden]] hue when [[polished]] and sold as [[Converted Rice]].

[[Rice]] varieties are broadly categorized by [[grain]] length: [[short]], [[medium]], or [[long]]. Most modern [[Rice]] varieties are [[Hybrid Cultivar|hybrid cultivars]], developed through extensive [[research]] and [[experimentation]] in [[Farming Techniques|farming]] and [[processing]]. [[Wild Rice]], while sharing similar [[culinary characteristics]], is technically a [[wild grass]] and not in the same [[genus]] as true [[Rice]]. It is primarily [[grown]] in the [[Upper Midwest]] and [[Canada]], with most [[commercial production]] occurring in [[California]].

## Flavour & Texture
[[Rice]] [[texture]] is largely determined by its [[starch]] components: [[Amylose]] and [[Amylopectin]]. [[Long Grain Rice]], typically from the [[Indica Cultivar]], has a [[long]], [[cylindrical]] appearance and a higher [[Amylose]] content, which results in [[dry]], [[fluffy]] [[grains]] that hold their [[shape]] well when [[cooked]]. In contrast, [[Short Grain Rice]], often from the [[Japonica Cultivar]], and [[Medium Grain Rice]] have higher [[Amylopectin]] content. When [[cooked]], these varieties are [[moist]], [[tender]], and have a greater tendency to [[cling together]], making them suitable for [[dishes]] like [[Sushi]], [[desserts]], and [[puddings]]. Some [[Short Grain Rice]] varieties are inaccurately called [[Glutinous Rice]] due to their [[sticky]] [[texture]], though they contain no [[Gluten]].

The [[color]] of [[Rice]]—such as [[Brown Rice]], [[Black Rice]], and [[Red Rice]]—comes from natural [[pigmentation]] in the [[plant]], as these varieties are minimally [[processed]] with the [[Bran]] left intact. These [[colored]] varieties generally take longer to [[cook]], maintain their [[shape]] better, and offer a [[chewier]] [[texture]]. However, the presence of [[oil]] in the [[Bran]] makes them more susceptible to [[spoilage]], leading to a shorter [[shelf life]] than [[White Rice]]. Variations in [[translucence]] also affect [[texture]]; [[opaque]] or "[[chalky]]" [[grains]] are [[softer]], [[denser]], and [[heavier]] than [[translucent]] [[grains]], and tend to [[cook]] more quickly. [[Aromatic Rice]] varieties, including [[Basmati]], [[Jasmine]], [[Texmati]], and [[Weihani Rice]], are prized for their distinctive [[nutty]] and [[bold]] [[flavor profiles]]. Leading [[growers]] of these types in the [[United States]] include [[Arkansas]], [[Northern California]], and [[Texas]].

## How to Buy & Store
[[Rice]] should be [[stored]] in [[sealed containers]] at [[Room Temperature]] in a [[cool]] and [[dry place]] to maintain its [[quality]]. Due to the natural [[oils]] present in the [[Bran]], [[Brown Rice]] and other [[colored]] varieties are more prone to becoming [[rancid]] and thus have a shorter [[shelf life]] compared to [[White Rice]]. It is important to [[rotate]] these varieties through [[storage]] more quickly to prevent [[spoilage]].

## How to Use
Modern [[processing techniques]] generally produce [[clean]] [[Rice]], but some [[preparations]] recommend [[Rinsing]] or [[Soaking]] [[Rice]] to remove excess [[starch]] and achieve a less [[sticky]] result. This is particularly common for [[Small Grain Rice]] and [[Medium Grain Rice]] varieties.

[[Rice]] can be [[cooked]] by [[Boiling]] in [[salted water]], similar to [[Pasta]]. [[Braising]] methods, such as those used for [[Pilaf]], [[Paella]], [[Jambalaya]], and [[Risotto]], involve first [[Sautéing]] and sometimes [[Browning]] the [[Rice]] in [[Fat]] before gradually adding [[liquid]]. For [[Long Grain Rice]], it is typically not [[stirred]] after the [[liquid]] is added, allowing it to [[steam]] and [[cook]] evenly. In contrast, [[Short Grain Rice]] varieties used in [[dishes]] like [[Paella]] and [[Risotto]] are traditionally [[stirred]] while [[liquids]] are added, promoting the [[release]] of [[starch]] for a [[creamy]] [[texture]]. [[Sticky Rice]] varieties, commonly used in Asian [[Cuisine]], are usually [[rinsed]] or [[soaked]], combined with [[water]], covered, and gently [[Steaming|steamed]] without [[stirring]].

## Substitutions
*   [[Wild Rice]]: While technically a different [[grass]], [[Wild Rice]] can be used as a [[substitute]] for its similar [[texture]] and [[nutty]] [[flavor]], especially in [[pilafs]] or as a [[side dish]], though it will have a [[firmer]] [[bite]] and distinct [[taste]].
