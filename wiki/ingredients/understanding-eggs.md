## Overview
[[Eggs]] are a fundamental and versatile [[ingredient]], primarily composed of [[water]], with significant amounts of [[Proteins]], [[Fats]], and [[Minerals]]. They are widely used both as a stand-alone [[food]] and as a key component in various [[culinary preparations]], including [[Sauces]], [[Custards]], [[Batters]], and [[Foams]].

The production of [[eggs]] varies significantly. While many [[eggs]] come from large [[Factory Farms]] where [[chickens]] are housed in [[Battery Cages]] under [[artificial light]], there's a growing movement towards more [[humane methods]]. The [[USDA]] defines different categories for [[consumers]]:
*   [[Free Range Eggs]]: From [[birds]] that have access to the [[outdoors]].
*   [[Cage-Free Eggs]]: From [[birds]] not raised in [[cages]], typically in open [[barn floor systems]].
*   [[Organic Eggs]]: From [[chickens]] fed [[organic feed]] and given no [[Antibiotics]].
*   [[Vegetarian Eggs]]: Produced from [[chickens]] fed only [[vegetarian feed]].

## Flavour & Texture
[[Eggs]] offer a neutral yet [[rich]] base, capable of imparting [[creaminess]], [[moisture]], or a [[crisp texture]] depending on [[preparation]]. [[Egg Yolks]] are particularly [[rich]], containing high levels of [[Protein]], [[Fat]], and [[Cholesterol]]. They also contain [[Lecithin]], a natural [[Emulsifier]] vital for [[Sauces]] like [[Hollandaise]] and [[Mayonnaise]], and contribute [[richness]] and [[moisture]] to [[Cakes]], [[Souffles]], and [[Custards]].

[[Egg Whites]], conversely, are about 90% [[moisture]] and 10% [[Protein]]. When [[foamed]], they act as [[Leavening Agents]], providing [[height]] and [[volume]] to [[Cakes]] and [[Meringues]]. Their lack of [[fat]] makes them ideal for achieving a [[Crisp Texture]], as seen in baked [[meringues]], and they are also used in [[Clarifying]] [[Consommés]].

The [[structure]] of raw [[eggs]] is loose, but during [[Cooking]], the [[Proteins]] undergo [[Protein Denaturation]], unfolding and tangling to suspend [[moisture]] in a [[solid form]]. Proper [[cooking]] results in a [[creamy]], [[moist texture]]; [[overcooking]] can lead to a [[Rubbery Mass]] or cause them to [[Curdle|Curdled Sauce]] and leech [[moisture]]. [[Eggs]] generally tolerate lower [[temperatures]] better than [[intense heat]], allowing for greater [[control]] over [[texture]], especially in [[Custards]] and [[Sauces]]. A [[green discoloration]] in [[Egg Yolks]] can occur from [[overcooking]], due to the interaction of [[Iron]] and [[Sulfur]].

## How to Buy & Store
[[Egg Quality Grading]] is a [[voluntary process]], with [[Grade AA]] and [[Grade A]] being the most common for [[retail]]. This [[grading]] is determined by a [[method]] called [[Candling]], where [[eggs]] pass over a [[backlight]] to assess [[internal quality]]. More crucial than [[grading]], however, is [[freshness]], which dictates how well an [[egg]] holds together when [[Poached]], [[Fried]], or [[cooked]] in the [[shell]]. Fresher [[eggs]] have [[thicker whites]] and [[yolks]] that are less prone to [[breaking]].

To mitigate the risk of [[Salmonella]] [[bacteria]], especially in [[Undercooked Eggs]] used in [[Sauces]] like [[Mayonnaise]] and [[Hollandaise]], [[cooking]] to [[140°F (60°C)]] for [[5 minutes]] or [[160°F (71°C)]] for [[one minute]] is recommended. [[Pasteurized Eggs]], heated to [[temperatures]] between [[130-140°F (55–60°C)]] to [[kill bacteria]] without [[coagulating]] the [[proteins]], are also available in [[whole]], [[separated]], or [[dried forms]].

## How to Use
[[Eggs]] are incredibly [[versatile]], acting as [[Emulsifiers]], [[Leavening Agents]], [[binders]], and [[thickeners]].
*   **[[Egg Yolks]]** are essential for creating [[rich]] [[Emulsion Sauces]] such as [[Mayonnaise]], [[Hollandaise]], and [[Crème Anglaise]]. They also add [[richness]] and [[moisture]] to [[Cakes]], [[Souffles]], and [[Custards]].
*   **[[Egg Whites]]** are primarily used for their [[Leavening]] properties when [[Whipped]] into [[Foams]], providing [[height]] and [[Structure]] to [[Cakes]] and [[Meringues]]. They are also crucial for [[Consommé Clarification]].
*   **[[Whole Eggs]]** are the base for [[creamy preparations]] like [[Custard]], [[Flan]], [[Crème Brûlée]], and [[Quiche]].

For [[Poaching]] and [[Frying]], [[fresher eggs]] are preferred due to their [[thicker whites]], which help them [[hold their shape]] better. Conversely, [[older eggs]] are easier to [[peel]] when [[cooked]] in the [[shell]] because the [[egg]] shrinks and loses [[moisture]] as it [[ages]], creating a [[larger air pocket]]. [[Controlling cooking temperature]], especially [[slow cooking]], is key to achieving [[creamy textures]] in [[Custards]] and [[Sauces]], preventing a [[rubbery]] or [[curdled result|Curdled Sauce]].
