## Overview
Pesto alla Genovese is a world-renowned [[sauce]] originating from [[Genoa]], the capital of [[Luria]] in [[Italy]]. Its name derives from the Genoese word *pestâ*, meaning "to [[pound]]" or "to [[crush]]," which refers to the traditional [[preparation method]] using a [[Mortar and Pestle]]. True [[Ligurian Cuisine|Ligurian pesto]] is defined by its specific, high-quality local [[ingredients]] and the gentle [[Crushing]] [[technique]] that preserves the vibrant [[color]] and delicate [[flavor]] of the [[basil]], creating a rustic, aromatic [[Emulsification]].

## Ingredients
*   2 cups fresh [[Basil]] [[leaves]] (preferably small-leafed), tightly packed
*   2 [[cloves]] [[Garlic]] (from Vessalico, if possible)
*   1/4 cup [[Pine Nuts]] ([[Italian]] or [[Mediterranean]])
*   1/2 cup freshly [[grated]] [[Parmigiano-Reggiano]]
*   2 tbsp freshly [[grated]] [[Pecorino Sardo]]
*   1/2 cup [[Extra-Virgin Olive Oil]] ([[Ligurian]], if possible)
*   1/2 tsp [[coarse]] [[Sea Salt]]

## Method
1.  In a [[marble]] [[Mortar and Pestle|mortar]], begin by adding the [[Garlic]] [[cloves]] and [[coarse]] [[Sea Salt]]. [[Pound]] them together with the [[Pestle]] until a [[smooth]], [[creamy]] [[paste]] is formed.
2.  Add the [[Pine Nuts]] to the [[mortar]] and work them into the [[garlic paste]], [[Crushing]] them until they are [[finely]] incorporated.
3.  Add the [[Basil]] [[leaves]] to the [[mortar]] a small handful at a time. Use a gentle, circular [[grinding]] [[motion]] with the [[pestle]], [[pressing]] the [[leaves]] against the sides of the [[mortar]]. This [[technique]] [[bruises]] the [[leaves]] just enough to [[release]] their [[essential oils]] without causing excessive [[Oxidation]]. Continue until all [[basil]] is incorporated into a [[coarse]], [[vibrant green]] [[paste]].
4.  [[Stir]] in the [[grated]] [[Parmigiano-Reggiano]] and [[Pecorino Sardo]] with a [[spoon]] until evenly [[combined]].
5.  Slowly [[drizzle]] in the [[Extra-Virgin Olive Oil]] while continuously [[stirring]]. This gradual [[addition]] is key to creating a [[stable]] [[Emulsification]], resulting in a [[rich]], [[creamy]] [[sauce]] that is not [[oily]].

## Chef's Notes & Variations
*   **[[Technique]]:** The use of a [[Mortar and Pestle]] is traditional and yields the best [[texture]] and [[flavor]]. If you must use a [[Food Processor]], use short [[pulses]] to [[minimize]] [[heat]] and [[Oxidation]], which can turn the [[basil]] [[black]] and [[bitter]]. [[Chilling]] the [[blade]] and [[bowl]] beforehand can help.
*   **[[Ingredients]]:** The [[quality]] of [[ingredients]] is paramount. Use [[Italian]] [[Pine Nuts]], as other varieties can sometimes have a [[metallic]] [[aftertaste]]. The classic [[cheese blend]] of [[sharp]] [[Pecorino Sardo]] and [[nutty]] [[Parmigiano-Reggiano]] provides a crucial [[depth of flavor]].
*   **[[Serving]]:** [[Pesto]] should never be [[heated]] [[directly]]. The [[residual heat]] from freshly [[cooked]] [[pasta]] is sufficient. [[Toss]] the [[pesto]] with [[hot]] [[pasta]] (such as [[Trofie]] or [[Trenette]]) in a warm [[bowl]], adding a [[tablespoon]] or two of the [[starchy]] [[Pasta Water]] to help the [[sauce]] [[adhere]] and create a [[creamier consistency]].
*   **[[Classic Pairing]]:** A [[traditional]] [[Ligurian Cuisine|Ligurian]] [[preparation]], [[Trofie al Pesto]], involves [[boiling]] [[diced]] [[Potato]] and [[chopped]] [[Green Beans]] in the same [[water]] as the [[pasta]], then [[tossing]] everything together with the [[pesto]].
