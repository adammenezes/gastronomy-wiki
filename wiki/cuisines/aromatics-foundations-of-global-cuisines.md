## Overview
In cooking, [[Aromatics]] are combinations of [[vegetable|vegetables]], [[spice|spices]], and [[herb|herbs]] that serve as a flavor foundation for a wide variety of dishes. Used in everything from [[soup|soups]] and [[sauce|sauces]] to [[stew|stews]] and [[stir-fry|stir-fries]], these ingredients are fundamental to different [[Cuisine|cuisines]] around the world. While some aromatics like [[onion]] and [[garlic]] are nearly universal, others like [[lemongrass]] and [[galangal]] are specific to certain geographic regions, defining their unique culinary character.

## French Aromatics
[[French Cuisine]] relies on several key aromatic preparations as a foundation for its dishes.

The most famous is the [[Mirepoix]], a mixture of two parts [[onion]], one part [[carrot]], and one part [[celery]]. This base adds depth to [[soup|soups]], [[stock|stocks]], and [[stew|stews]]. A more complex version, known as a [[Matignon]], is essentially a mirepoix with the addition of [[bacon]] or [[ham]] for a savory boost. A mirepoix can be varied by substituting [[leek|leeks]] for onions or using [[celeriac]] instead of celery. The size of the cut depends on the cooking time; for a long-simmering [[Brown Stock]], vegetables can be left in large pieces, while they are diced smaller for quicker preparations.

Herbs are also central to French flavor bases. A [[Sachet d'Épice]] is a small bag, typically made of [[cheesecloth]], filled with dried herbs like [[parsley]], [[bay leaf|bay leaves]], [[thyme]], and [[peppercorn|peppercorns]]. A [[Bouquet Garni]] is a similar concept but uses a bundle of fresh herbs, often parsley, bay, and thyme, sometimes wrapped in a leek leaf. These bundles can be customized; [[rosemary]] is a common addition for a [[Lamb Stew]], while [[tarragon]] complements a [[Chicken Fricassee]].

## Cajun & Creole Aromatics
The [[Holy Trinity]] is the cornerstone of [[Cajun Cuisine]] and [[Creole Cooking]] in [[Louisiana]]. It consists of [[onion|onions]], [[celery]], and [[bell pepper|bell peppers]]. This combination is believed to have evolved from the French [[Mirepoix]], with the native [[bell pepper]] of the [[Americas]] replacing the [[carrot]]. [[Scallion|Scallions]] are sometimes used in place of onions. The trinity is the essential starting point for classic dishes like [[Gumbo]], [[Jambalaya]], and [[Crawfish Étouffée]].

## Italian Aromatics
In [[Italian Cuisine]], the primary aromatic base is the [[Soffritto]], a blend of [[onion|onions]], [[carrot|carrots]], [[celery]], and [[garlic]]. It is the Italian counterpart to the [[Mirepoix]] and is foundational to classic sauces like [[Marinara]], [[Amatriciana]], and [[Ragù Bolognese]], as well as dishes like [[Risotto]] and [[Minestrone]].

A [[Battuta]] is a soffritto that has been [[Sautéing|sautéed]] in [[olive oil]], often with additional ingredients like [[parsley]], [[pancetta]], or [[lard]]. It is used in soups like [[Zuppa di Verdure]] and stews such as [[Stracotto]]. A [[Trito]] is another aromatic mix, typically featuring finely chopped herbs, garlic, and sometimes onion.

## Spanish Aromatics
[[Spanish Cuisine]] uses a flavor base called a [[Sofrito]]. Similar to its Italian counterpart, it contains [[onion|onions]] and [[garlic]], but it uses [[bell pepper|bell peppers]] and [[tomato|tomatoes]] instead of carrots and celery. This mixture is slow-cooked in [[olive oil]] and may include [[bay leaf|bay leaves]], [[smoked paprika]] (pimentón), or [[rosemary]]. Sofrito is the first step in making [[Paella]] and is a key component in stews, sauces, and soups like [[Gazpacho]]. [[Saffron]] is often added during the cooking of paella to impart its signature color and aroma.

## Caribbean Aromatics
The Caribbean [[Sofrito]] is a product of [[Spanish Cuisine|Spanish]] colonial roots and African influences, adapted with local ingredients. Found in [[Cuban Cuisine]], [[Puerto Rican Cuisine]], and [[Dominican Cuisine]], it typically includes [[onion|onions]], [[garlic]], [[bell pepper|bell peppers]], and [[tomato|tomatoes]] sautéed in [[olive oil]]. Local peppers like [[Ají Dulce]] may be used, along with [[culantro]] (recao), which has a more potent flavor than [[cilantro]] and is used to brighten the mix.

## Signature Dishes
Aromatics are the backbone of countless iconic dishes across various cuisines. In [[French Cuisine]], the [[Mirepoix]] forms the base for classics like [[Coq au Vin]], [[Boeuf Bourguignon]], and rich [[French Onion Soup]]. [[Cajun Cuisine]] and [[Creole Cooking]] rely on the [[Holy Trinity]] for their signature [[Gumbo]], [[Jambalaya]], and [[Crawfish Étouffée]]. [[Italian Cuisine]]'s [[Soffritto]] is essential for foundational dishes such as [[Ragù Bolognese]], [[Marinara Sauce]], [[Risotto]], and hearty [[Minestrone]]. The [[Spanish Sofrito]] is the starting point for the national dish [[Paella]], as well as numerous stews and sauces. In the [[Caribbean]], the local [[Sofrito]] underpins dishes like [[Arroz con Pollo]] and [[Sancocho]], providing depth and vibrant flavor.

## Key Ingredients
The defining ingredients of aromatic bases vary by cuisine but often share common elements that contribute to their foundational flavor. Universal components include [[onion|onions]] and [[garlic]], which provide pungency and sweetness when cooked. [[Carrots]] and [[celery]] are central to [[French Mirepoix]] and [[Italian Soffritto]], offering sweetness and a subtle bitterness, respectively. [[Bell peppers]] are a hallmark of the [[Cajun Holy Trinity]] and both [[Spanish]] and [[Caribbean Sofrito]], adding a fresh, vegetal note. [[Tomatoes]] are crucial for the depth and acidity in [[Spanish Sofrito]] and its Caribbean variations. Beyond these vegetables, various [[herb|herbs]] like [[parsley]], [[thyme]], [[bay leaf|bay leaves]], [[rosemary]], and [[culantro]] (recao) are frequently incorporated, along with [[spice|spices]] such as [[smoked paprika]] and [[saffron]], to further define the aromatic profile of a dish.

## The Role of Aromatics in Cooking
Flavor bases like [[Mirepoix]] and [[Sofrito]] are crucial for building depth and complexity in a dish. Through techniques like [[Sautéing]] or [[Sweating]], aromatics release their natural oils and sugars. This process of [[Caramelization]] creates sweetness and a deep, savory [[Umami]] foundation. Each aromatic contributes a unique quality: [[onion|onions]] provide sweetness, [[garlic]] adds pungency, [[carrot|carrots]] offset acidity, and [[celery]] introduces a subtle bitterness for balance. The choice of fat, such as the [[olive oil]] in an Italian [[Soffritto]], adds another layer of regional character. Ultimately, aromatics define the signature profile of a cuisine and create the base upon which other flavors are layered.
