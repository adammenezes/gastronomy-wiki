## Overview
Cha Gio is a key [[dish]] in [[Vietnamese Cuisine]], with a significant [[history]] in the [[United States]]. According to [[research]] by [[Minh-Ha T. Pham]], the [[dish]]'s presence in [[America]] predates the post-1975 wave of [[refugees]], having been introduced by [[Vietnamese war brides]], [[diplomats]], and [[workers]] between 1945 and 1975. This early [[introduction]] helped establish [[Vietnamese cooking]] within the [[American culinary landscape]] well before the first formal [[restaurants]] appeared.

## Chef's Notes & Variations
The [[historical context]] of Cha Gio in the [[United States]] is deeply connected to the [[social networks]] of early [[Vietnamese immigrants]]. As documented by [[Minh-Ha T. Pham]], [[networks]] of [[mutual aid]] and [[collaboration]] were crucial in cementing [[Vietnamese Cuisine]] in [[America]] during the [[post-World War II era]], a period of significant [[culinary experimentation]].
