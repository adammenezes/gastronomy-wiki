## Overview
Connected Foods is a term proposed by scholar [[Sevgi Mutlu Sirakova]] to describe foods, particularly homemade preserved items, that carry deep relational and microbial connections between people and places. The concept challenges sterile, industrial [[Pasteurian Regimes]] by embracing a [[Post-Pasteurianism]] view of [[Human-Microbial Collaborative Co-existence]]. These foods act as living links to a person's home, culture, and history, often transported by migrants to new environments.

## Core Principles
*   **Relational Quality:** The quality of a connected food is determined not just by its physical properties but by relational processes—familiarity with its producers, ingredients, and context of production. This direct contact imbues the food with meaning and value beyond the plate.
*   **Microbial Carriers:** These foods are living ecosystems, carrying unique [[Microbes]] from their place of origin. When transported, they introduce these microbes to new contexts, shaping local [[Microbial Landscapes]] and contributing to the biodiversity of new marketplaces.
*   **Cultural & Identity:** Connected foods are tangible carriers of cultural identity and memory. For migrants, they provide a taste of home, a way to maintain traditions, and a method for co-creating new cultural spaces, such as [[Migrant Marketplaces]].
*   **Challenge to Control:** The concept inherently opposes the strict controls of modern food systems and border agencies, which often restrict the movement of these [[Informal Foods]] and, by extension, the cultural and microbial connections they represent.

## Applications & Examples
The theory of connected foods is most evident in the context of migration. A migrant carrying a family [[Sourdough Starter]], a jar of pickled vegetables made with a traditional recipe, or a SCOBY for [[Kombucha]] is carrying a connected food. These items are not merely sustenance; they are active cultural and biological agents.

Upon arrival in a new home, these foods can become the foundation for small-scale economies within migrant communities. They can be shared, bartered, or sold, helping to establish marketplaces that reflect the culinary traditions of their home countries. However, this practice is often threatened by border controls and food safety regulations that favor sterilized, mass-produced goods over living, informal ones.

## See Also
*   [[Fermentation]]: The core biochemical process, driven by microbes, that creates many of the preserved foods central to this concept.
*   [[Terroir]]: The idea that a food's environment, including its unique microbiome, gives it a distinct character, a principle embodied by connected foods.
*   [[Sourdough Starter]]: A quintessential example of a connected food—a living microbial culture passed between people and carried across geographies.
*   [[Post-Pasteurianism]]: The broader ideological framework that values, rather than fears, the collaborative relationship between humans and microbes.
*   [[Food Sovereignty]]: This concept aligns with the right of communities to define their own food systems and value culturally significant foods outside of industrial control.
*   [[Microbiome]]: The community of microorganisms that defines the living character of these foods and their ability to influence new environments.
