## Overview
Pâte Brisée is a classic, versatile shortcrust pastry from [[French Cuisine]], made from [[Flour]], [[Fat]] (typically butter), salt, and water. This simple dough serves as the foundation for countless sweet and savory preparations, from open-faced [[Tarts]] to fully enclosed pies. Its history is ancient, with savory pies like the [[Pot Pie]] tracing their lineage back to the lavish banquets of the [[Roman Empire]] and even earlier to [[Ancient Greece]].

## Ingredients
*   1 1/4 cups (150g) [[All-Purpose Flour]]
*   1/2 teaspoon [[Kosher Salt]]
*   1/2 cup (113g) cold [[Unsalted Butter]], cut into 1/2-inch cubes
*   2-4 tablespoons [[Ice Water]]

## Method
1.  In a large [[Mixing Bowl]], whisk together the [[All-Purpose Flour]] and [[Kosher Salt]]. Alternatively, pulse them in a [[Food Processor]].
2.  Add the cold [[Unsalted Butter]] cubes to the flour mixture. Using a [[Pastry Blender]] or your fingertips, perform the [[Cutting In]] technique to break the butter down into pea-sized pieces coated in flour. If using a food processor, pulse a few times until the same consistency is reached.
3.  Drizzle 2 tablespoons of [[Ice Water]] over the mixture. Gently toss with a fork or pulse briefly until the dough begins to come together in shaggy clumps. Add more water, 1 teaspoon at a time, only if necessary. Do not overwork the dough.
4.  Turn the dough out onto a clean surface, gather it into a cohesive ball, and flatten it into a 1-inch thick disk. Wrap tightly in plastic wrap.
5.  Perform a [[Chilling]] of the dough in the refrigerator for at least 1 hour, or up to 2 days. This allows the gluten to relax and the fat to solidify, ensuring a tender, flaky crust.
6.  On a lightly floured surface, use a [[Rolling Pin]] to roll the chilled dough into a circle approximately 12 inches in diameter.
7.  Carefully transfer the dough to a 9-inch [[Pie Pan]] or [[Tart Pan]]. Trim and crimp the edges as desired. The shell is now ready for filling or for [[Blind Baking]].

## Chef's Notes & Variations
*   The key to a flaky crust is keeping the ingredients, especially the [[Unsalted Butter]] and water, as cold as possible. This prevents the fat from melting into the flour, instead creating small pockets that will steam and separate into layers during baking.
*   This dough is the foundation for the classic [[French Cuisine|French]] savory [[Quiche]].
*   For a rustic, free-form tart, use this dough to make a [[Galette]] or [[Croustade]]. Similar versions in [[Italian Cuisine]] are known as [[Croustata]].
*   While this dough is perfect for American-style [[Hand-pie|hand-pies]], the [[Latin American Cuisine|Latin American]] [[Empanada]] and Italian [[Calzone]] are related concepts, though a calzone typically uses a yeast-leavened dough.
*   When [[Blind Baking]], the shell can be filled with [[Custard]], fresh fruits, or citrus fillings for dessert tarts.
