## Overview
Puff Pastry, known in [[French Cuisine]] as *Pâte Feuilletée*, is a classic [[Laminated Dough]] celebrated for its thousands of light, flaky layers. This texture is achieved through the meticulous process of [[Lamination]], where a block of butter is folded and rolled into a lean dough multiple times. During baking, the water in both the dough and butter turns to steam, pushing the layers apart and creating the pastry's signature airy and crisp structure. It is a foundational component in countless sweet and savory dishes.

## Ingredients
*   All-Purpose [[Flour]]
*   [[Water]], cold
*   [[Salt]]
*   [[Butter]], high-fat European-style, softened
*   2 oz / 30g [[Flour]], for the butter block

## Method
**Part 1: Prepare the Détrempe (Dough Block)**
1.  In the bowl of a stand [[Mixer]] fitted with a [[Dough Hook]], combine the main portion of [[Flour]], [[Water]], and [[Salt]]. Mix on low speed until a smooth, pliable dough forms.
2.  Shape the dough into a square, about 1-inch thick. Dust lightly with flour.
3.  Place the dough on a [[Parchment Paper]]-lined [[Sheet Pan]], cover with [[Plastic Wrap]], and chill in the refrigerator for 1 hour. This initial [[Chilling]] allows the [[Gluten]] to relax.

**Part 2: Prepare the Beurrage (Butter Block)**
1.  In a [[Mixing Bowl]], use a [[Paddle Attachment]] to combine the softened [[Butter]] and the 2 oz / 30g of [[Flour]] until smooth and homogenous. The flour helps absorb excess moisture and makes the butter more pliable.
2.  Transfer the butter mixture to a sheet of [[Parchment Paper]]. Form it into an even square about ½-inch (1 cm) thick.
3.  Cover with [[Plastic Wrap]] and chill slightly. The butter should be firm but not hard, matching the consistency of the chilled dough.

**Part 3: Lamination & Turns**
1.  On a lightly floured surface, use a [[Rolling Pin]] to roll the chilled dough into a square large enough to enclose the butter block. Place the butter square diagonally in the center of the dough.
2.  Perform an [[Envelope Fold]] by folding the four corners of the dough over the butter, meeting in the center. Pinch the seams to seal the butter completely inside the dough packet.
3.  Carefully roll the packet out into a long rectangle, approximately three times as long as it is wide.
4.  Perform a [[Three-Fold]] (or letter fold) by folding the bottom third of the rectangle up to the middle, then folding the top third down over it, as if folding a business letter.
5.  Wrap the dough in plastic and chill for 30-45 minutes.
6.  Repeat the process of rolling the dough into a rectangle, performing a [[Three-Fold]], and chilling for another 30-45 minutes. Complete this for a total of three turns.
7.  After the final turn, wrap the dough and chill for at least 30-45 minutes before using.

## Chef's Notes & Variations
*   **Temperature is Key:** The single most important factor for successful puff pastry is temperature control. The [[Butter]] and dough must remain cold. If the butter gets too warm, it will melt into the dough, and you will lose the distinct layers. If it gets too cold, it will shatter when rolled, creating an uneven final product.
*   **Resting is Mandatory:** The [[Chilling]] periods between turns are not optional. They allow the [[Gluten]] in the dough to relax, making it easier to roll out without tearing and preventing the final pastry from shrinking excessively.
*   **Classic Puff Pastry:** This recipe calls for three turns. A classic [[Pâte Feuilletée]] from [[French Cuisine]] often undergoes a total of six turns, creating an even finer and more delicate crumb.
*   **Uses:** This dough is the base for dishes like [[Beef Wellington]], [[Tarte Tatin]], [[Palmiers]], and [[Vol-au-Vent]].
