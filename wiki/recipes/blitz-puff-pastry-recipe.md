## Overview
Blitz Puff Pastry, also known as Rough Puff Pastry, is a streamlined method for creating a [[Laminated Dough]]. Originating as a quicker alternative within [[French Cuisine]], this technique incorporates chunks of cold butter directly into the dough from the start, rather than enclosing a single large block of butter as in classic [[Puff Pastry]]. The subsequent process of rolling and folding creates hundreds of flaky layers, achieving a similar, though slightly less uniform, result with significantly less effort.

## Ingredients
*   [[All-Purpose Flour]]
*   [[Salt]]
*   Cold [[Unsalted Butter]], cut into pieces
*   Cold [[Water]]

## Method
1.  **Combine and Mix:** In the bowl of a [[Stand Mixer]] fitted with a [[Dough Hook]] attachment, combine the [[All-Purpose Flour]], [[Salt]], and cold [[Unsalted Butter]]. Mix briefly on low speed just to coat the butter pieces with flour. Add the cold [[Water]] and continue to mix on low until the flour is just hydrated and the mixture forms a sticky, shaggy mass with visible chunks of butter. Do not overmix.

2.  **First Chill:** Wrap the dough in [[Plastic Wrap]] and chill in the refrigerator for approximately 30 minutes to firm up the butter and relax the gluten.

3.  **Fold and Turn:** Place the chilled dough on a lightly floured surface and roll it out into a rectangle about ½ inch (1 cm) thick. Perform a [[Letter Fold]] (three-fold) or a [[Book Fold]] (four-fold). This completes the first turn.

4.  **Repeat and Chill:** Repeat the rolling and folding process three more times, for a total of four turns. After each fold, turn the dough 90° before rolling again. This [[Turning]] technique ensures the layers are even. Dust the dough with flour as needed to prevent sticking, but be sure to brush off any excess before folding. If the dough becomes warm or difficult to work with, chill it for 20-30 minutes between turns.

5.  **Final Rest:** After the final turn, wrap the dough tightly and chill for at least 1 hour before using in any recipe.

## Chef's Notes & Variations
*   The key to a flaky result is keeping the butter cold and in distinct pieces within the dough. If the butter melts and incorporates fully, you will lose the layers.
*   Unlike traditional [[Puff Pastry]], where a single butter block (beurrage) is encased in dough (détrempe), the "blitz" method simplifies this into a single initial mixture.
*   Brushing off excess flour before folding is crucial. Trapped flour can prevent the dough layers from fusing during baking, resulting in a dough that separates or has a dry, pasty texture.
*   This dough is ideal for applications like turnovers, sausage rolls, or a rustic [[Tarte Tatin]]. For applications requiring extreme precision and lift, such as a [[Vol-au-Vent]], the classic method may be preferred.
