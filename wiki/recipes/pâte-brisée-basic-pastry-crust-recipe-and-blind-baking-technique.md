## Overview
Pastry Crust, known in [[French Cuisine]] as Pâte Brisée, is a simple, flaky dough fundamental to tarts, pies, and quiches. It is a "short" pastry, meaning it is tender and crumbly due to fat inhibiting [[Gluten]] development. The classic ratio is 3 parts flour, 2 parts fat, and 1 part water, and its success relies on keeping the ingredients cold and handling the dough minimally.

## Ingredients
- 1 lb / 450 g [[All-Purpose Flour]]
- 1 tsp / 5 g [[Salt]]
- 8 oz / 225 g [[Butter]], [[Lard]], or [[Vegetable Shortening]], chilled and cubed
- 4 oz / 240 ml [[Water]], ice cold

## Method
1.  **Combine and Cut in Fat:** In a large [[Mixing Bowl]], combine the [[Flour]] and [[Salt]]. Add the chilled fat. Using a [[Pastry Blender]] or your fingertips, perform the [[Cutting In]] technique to work the fat into the flour until the mixture resembles coarse crumbs with some pea-sized pieces of fat remaining.

2.  **Add Liquid and Shape:** Add the cold [[Water]] all at once. Mix gently with a fork or your hands until the dough just comes together into a shaggy ball. Do not overwork it. Shape the dough into a flat disk, wrap tightly in [[Plastic Wrap]], and perform a [[Chilling]] in the refrigerator for at least 1 hour.

3.  **Roll Out:** Lightly flour a work surface and a [[Rolling Pin]]. Unwrap the chilled dough. Begin [[Rolling]] from the center of the dough outwards, easing up on the pressure near the edges. Lift and rotate the dough a quarter turn after each roll to ensure even thickness and prevent sticking. Lightly dust with more flour as needed. Roll to a thickness of approximately 1/8” (3mm).

4.  **Line the Pan:** Gently fold the dough into quarters or roll it onto the [[Rolling Pin]] to transfer it to a [[Pie Pan]] or [[Tart Pan]]. Unfurl the dough and ease it into the pan, pressing it gently into the bottom and up the sides. Avoid stretching the dough, as this will cause it to shrink during baking. Trim the excess dough, leaving a slight overhang.

5.  **Prepare for Baking:** For a pre-baked shell (a technique known as [[Blind Baking]]), chill or freeze the dough-lined pan for 30 minutes. Perform [[Docking]] by pricking the bottom of the crust with a [[Fork]] or a [[Dough Docker]] to allow steam to escape. Line the shell with [[Parchment Paper]] and fill with [[Pie Weights]] or dried [[Beans]] before baking.

## Chef's Notes & Variations
- **Temperature is Key:** The most critical factor for a flaky crust is temperature. The [[Butter]] or fat must remain cold and in distinct pieces. If it melts into the flour, the crust will be tough, not flaky.
- **Minimal Mixing:** Over-mixing develops [[Gluten]], the protein network in flour that gives bread its chewiness. For a tender, short pastry, you want to minimize gluten development by handling the dough as little as possible.
- **Fat Variations:** All-[[Butter]] crusts offer the best flavor. [[Lard]] produces the flakiest crust. [[Vegetable Shortening]] creates a very tender crust but lacks flavor. Many bakers use a combination, such as half butter and half lard.
- **Blind Baking:** The process of [[Blind Baking]] is essential for pies with unbaked fillings, like a cream pie, or fillings that have a shorter bake time than the crust. The [[Pie Weights]] prevent the bottom from puffing up and the sides from slumping.
