## Overview
[[French Onion Soup]] (Soupe à l'oignon gratinée) is a quintessential [[dish]] of [[French Cuisine]], celebrated for its deep, [[savory]] [[Beef Broth]] base, intensely [[Caramelized Onions]], and a signature topping of toasted [[Baguette]] slices smothered in melted [[Gruyère Cheese]]. Originating in [[France]], this hearty [[Soup]] is a comforting classic, often served as a [[starter]], especially during colder seasons, offering a [[rich]] blend of [[Umami]] and [[sweetness]]. Its distinctive final step involves [[Gratinating]] under a [[broiler]], creating a [[golden]], [[bubbly]] [[cheese crust]].

## Ingredients
*   4 large [[Onions]] (yellow or sweet), thinly sliced
*   2 tbsp [[Butter]]
*   1 tbsp [[Olive Oil]]
*   1 tsp [[Sugar]] (optional, to aid [[Caramelization]])
*   1 cup [[Dry White Wine]] or [[Dry Sherry]]
*   6 cups [[Beef Broth]] (preferably homemade [[Brown Stock]])
*   1 [[Bay Leaf]]
*   1 sprig fresh [[Thyme]]
*   [[Salt]] and freshly ground [[Black Pepper]] to taste
*   8 slices [[Baguette]], about 1-inch thick, toasted
*   1.5 cups grated [[Gruyère Cheese]] (or [[Comté]], [[Emmental]])

## Method
1.  **[[Caramelize]] the Onions**: In a large, heavy-bottomed [[Dutch Oven]] or [[Stock Pot]], melt the [[Butter]] with the [[Olive Oil]] over medium-low [[heat]]. Add the thinly sliced [[Onions]] and [[Sugar]] (if using). [[Cook]] slowly, [[stirring]] occasionally, for 30-45 minutes, until the [[Onions]] are deeply [[golden brown]], very [[soft]], and [[sweet]]. This slow [[Maillard Reaction]] is crucial for developing the [[soup]]'s characteristic [[depth of flavor]].
2.  **[[Deglaze]]**: Increase the [[heat]] to medium. Pour in the [[Dry White Wine]] or [[Dry Sherry]] and [[scrape]] up any [[browned bits]] from the bottom of the [[pot]] with a [[Wooden Spoon]]. Let it [[simmer]] for 5 minutes until the [[liquid]] has mostly evaporated, concentrating the [[flavors]].
3.  **[[Simmer]] the Soup**: Add the [[Beef Broth]], [[Bay Leaf]], and [[Thyme]] sprig to the [[pot]]. Bring the [[mixture]] to a [[boil]], then reduce the [[heat]] to low, cover, and [[Simmer]] for at least 30 minutes, or up to an hour, allowing all the [[flavors]] to meld. Before serving, remove and discard the [[Bay Leaf]] and [[Thyme]] sprig. Season with [[Salt]] and freshly ground [[Black Pepper]] to taste.
4.  **Prepare for [[Gratinating]]**: Preheat your [[Oven]] [[broiler]]. [[Ladle]] the hot [[Soup]] into oven-safe [[Soup Crocks]] or [[bowls]]. Place a toasted [[Baguette]] slice on top of each [[bowl]], ensuring it floats on the surface of the [[soup]].
5.  **[[Gratinate]]**: Generously [[sprinkle]] the grated [[Gruyère Cheese]] over the [[Baguette]] slices, ensuring full coverage. Place the [[bowls]] on a [[Baking Sheet]] for stability and [[broil]] for 3-5 minutes, or until the [[cheese]] is [[melted]], [[bubbly]], and [[golden brown]]. Watch carefully to prevent [[burning]].
6.  **Serve**: Carefully remove from the [[Oven]] and serve immediately, being mindful of the extremely hot [[bowls]] and [[molten cheese]].

## Chef's Notes & Variations
For an even [[richer]] and more complex [[flavor]], consider using homemade [[Brown Stock]] or a clarified [[Consomme]] as your base. The key to a truly great French Onion Soup lies in the [[patience]] exercised during the [[Caramelization]] of the [[Onions]]; rushing this step will result in a less [[flavorful]] and [[sweet]] [[soup]]. A splash of [[Brandy]] or [[Cognac]] can be added along with the [[Wine]] for an extra layer of [[aromatic depth]]. If [[Gruyère Cheese]] is unavailable, [[Comté]] or [[Emmental]] are excellent substitutes that will provide a similar [[melting quality]] and [[nutty flavor]]. For a [[vegetarian version]], substitute a [[rich]] [[Vegetable Stock]] for the [[Beef Broth]], though this will alter the traditional [[flavor profile]]. Always ensure your [[Soup Crocks]] are certified oven-safe to withstand the high [[heat]] of the [[broiler]].
