# Yeast Fermentation in Dough

## Overview
Yeast fermentation is the biological process responsible for leavening most breads, from a [[lean dough]] like a [[baguette]] to a [[rich dough]] like [[challah]]. It is the metabolic action of yeast, a single-celled fungus, which consumes sugars in the [[flour]] and produces carbon dioxide gas. This gas becomes trapped within the dough's [[gluten]] network, causing it to expand and rise, creating the light, airy texture characteristic of yeast-leavened products.

## The Chemistry
The primary yeast used in baking is *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*. This microorganism performs anaerobic respiration, or fermentation, when mixed into a dough. It metabolizes simple sugars like glucose and fructose, which are either present in the [[flour]] or created when enzymes like [[amylase]] break down starches.

The core chemical reaction is:
C₆H₁₂O₆ (Sugar) → 2 C₂H₅OH (Ethanol) + 2 CO₂ (Carbon Dioxide)

The carbon dioxide (CO₂) is the leavening agent; as the gas is produced, it forms bubbles that are trapped by the elastic [[gluten]] matrix, causing the dough to inflate. The ethanol, along with other metabolic byproducts, evaporates during baking but contributes significantly to the complex flavor and aroma of the finished bread. This process is highly temperature-dependent, as yeast is a living organism with an optimal metabolic range.

## In the Kitchen
Yeast fermentation is the core principle behind techniques like [[proofing]] and [[bulk fermentation]]. The process begins when yeast is hydrated with water and mixed with [[flour]]. Different types of yeast ([[fresh yeast]], [[active dry yeast]], [[instant yeast]]) have different potencies and hydration requirements. For example, [[active dry yeast]] typically needs to be "bloomed" in warm water to activate it, while [[instant yeast]] can be mixed directly with dry ingredients. The use of a [[pre-ferment]], such as a [[poolish]] or a [[sourdough starter]], involves cultivating a yeast culture over a longer period to develop more complex flavors before it is added to the final dough.

## Practical Applications
Understanding yeast fermentation allows a cook to control the dough's development for desired outcomes.
- **Control Temperature:** To accelerate fermentation, place dough in a warm environment (75-90°F / 24-32°C). To slow it down for better flavor development or for scheduling purposes, use [[cold fermentation]] by placing the dough in the refrigerator.
- **Manage Salt:** [[Salt]] is crucial for flavor and for strengthening [[gluten]], but it also inhibits yeast activity by drawing water out of the yeast cells via [[osmosis]]. Avoid mixing salt directly with yeast; instead, incorporate it with the [[flour]] first to buffer its effect.
- **Feed the Yeast:** In doughs with very little natural sugar, a small amount of added [[sugar]] or [[malt]] can provide an initial food source for the yeast, helping to kick-start fermentation.

## What Can Go Wrong
- **Dough Fails to Rise:** This is typically due to inactive or dead yeast. The cause is often water that was too hot (above 140°F / 60°C), which kills the yeast, or an environment that is too cold, which makes the yeast dormant. Expired yeast or excessive contact with [[salt]] can also be the culprit.
- **Dough Rises Too Quickly and Collapses:** This is known as [[over-proofing]]. It occurs when the yeast produces gas too rapidly, often in an environment that is too warm or with too much yeast in the formula. The [[gluten]] network becomes over-stretched, weakens, and can no longer contain the gas, leading to a collapsed, dense final product with a sour, alcoholic flavor.
